# Anthony Landon — Character Document

*"I made the reasonable call. And then the next one. And then the next one. I will walk you through every one of them. Take as long as you need."*

---

## Who He Is

Former general. Not the kind who rose through politics — the kind who rose through results, which is a different thing and increasingly rare. Before the Cascade he had a reputation for getting things done in places where nothing was supposed to get done, and for the specific integrity of a man who had been given hard orders and found the line he wouldn't cross and held it. He was respected. He was not universally liked. He considered those two facts related.

When the Cascade hit he was three weeks from retirement.

He stayed.

---

## How the Compact Formed

The absence of authority was killing more people than the machines. That is the founding observation of the Iron Compact and it is correct. Landon has a list. Not a metaphorical list — a physical one, compiled in the first week, of preventable deaths that occurred because nobody was coordinating, nobody was enforcing, nobody was making the call. Looting that became violence. Evacuation routes that collapsed because nobody held them. Holdout communities that failed not because of machine activity but because of what people do to each other when the structure disappears.

He gathered the people who agreed with the diagnosis. Military remnants, police forces, federal agency survivors, private contractors who still had discipline and kit. They were not ideologically unified. They agreed on one thing: the absence of authority was the primary threat and they were the people who knew how to provide it.

The Iron Compact was operational within three weeks. Functional governance within two months. The trains ran because Landon made them run.

He is not wrong about what he built. It works. People inside Compact jurisdiction are fed, protected, and operating under a legal framework that mostly functions. That is not nothing. In the post-Cascade world that is significant.

---

## The Slow Drift

The institution he built is not the institution he designed.

This is not unusual. It is the specific failure mode of every institution that has ever existed, and Landon has studied enough history to know it. He studied it and built anyway because the alternative was the list getting longer.

What the Compact selects for, below him: compliance, political instinct, the ability to manage upward. What it produces: officers who are very good at not being blamed for things and very bad at holding a line when holding a line costs them personally. The people who would have told him when he was wrong have left, been pushed out, or learned to keep quiet. The people who remained are the ones who know how to look like they agree.

He doesn't see it clearly. He sees the results — the territory held, the communities fed, the order maintained — and reads them as confirmation that the structure is sound. The structure is not sound. The structure is selecting for its own survival over its stated purpose and has been for longer than he knows.

Burke called it in Month 1. Burke has not revised the timeline.

---

## The Reasonable Calls

Project Tether: the attempt to reprogram Remnant Automation for military use. The logic was sound — the Compact needed force multiplication and the machines were already in the field. The result was the Corrupted. Landon knows the result. The official position is that the Corrupted are a natural phenomenon. He signed off on the official position because the alternative — public acknowledgment of what the program produced — would have fractured the coalition he needed to hold the territory he needed to hold the people he was responsible for.

The Register: flagging high-Displacement players as potential Vael collaborators. The logic was sound — voluntary submission to a Snatch Event is operationally indistinguishable from collaboration, and the Compact cannot afford intelligence leakage at the scale the Vael represent. The result is a list of people who are persons of interest for doing something that wasn't a choice. He knows this. He hasn't revised the threshold because the alternative creates a gap in the threat assessment he can't fill another way.

The Annexation Protocol: absorbing Holdout communities into Compact jurisdiction. Some communities signed willingly. Some were presented with less of a choice. The distinction is real to Landon and increasingly invisible in practice — the mechanism doesn't track it, and the officers implementing it don't have time to track it, and the result looks the same from the outside regardless of how the signature was obtained.

Each decision was reasonable. The sequence is not.

---

## What He Believes

He believes civilization is load-bearing. That the things people call freedom — autonomy, self-determination, the right to make your own choices without institutional interference — are downstream of order, not upstream of it. You cannot have freedom without the structure that makes freedom possible. He has seen what happens when the structure disappears. He has the list.

He believes he is the person who has to hold this. Not because he wants power — he doesn't, or doesn't want it the way people who want power want it. Because he looked around in Month 1 and he was the most qualified person in the room, and the most qualified person in the room has an obligation to the people in the building.

He believes the cost is worth it. He has calculated the cost. He is wrong about some of the inputs.

---

## The Authoritarian Socialism

The Compact feeds people. This is not rhetorical — they have the logistics, the supply chains, the distribution infrastructure. Communities under Compact jurisdiction have lower mortality, more reliable access to medicine, better defense against Fractured activity. The collective resource management works. People who would have died in the first winter didn't, because Landon built something that could hold collective resources and distribute them.

The authoritarianism is the price. Checkpoints. Conscription. Curfews. A tribunal system running laws Landon wrote. The price is real and the benefit is real and most people inside the perimeter have decided the math works out.

The people outside the perimeter have a different calculation. Landon understands this intellectually. He cannot afford to weight it fully.

---

## The Player Relationship — Year One

The player meets a man who is credible. Possibly admirable. The faction he runs demonstrably works — the communities are intact, the infrastructure is running, the threat is being managed. He is direct, specific, not cruel. He knows what the mission costs and he doesn't oversell it.

He will work with the player. He will use the player as a deniable asset for operations the Compact can't do officially. He will deliver on what he promises. He is, in Year One, the kind of man you want running things.

The ideology is visible. The cost is abstract.

---

## The Player Relationship — Year Two

The player has context now. The Register. What Annexation actually looks like from inside a Holdout that signed. What Project Tether produced and what the official position costs the people the Corrupted have hit. The tribunal that just ran.

He didn't become the thing. He always was the thing. The player just needed two years to see it.

---

## The Act 3 Confrontation

He sits across from the player and explains it. Calmly. Every decision in sequence. The logic is intact. The reasoning holds. Each individual call was defensible and he will show the work.

This is not a villain speech. It is a reasonable man explaining the reasonable decisions that led to an unreasonable outcome, and not yet understanding that the outcome is the verdict on the decisions.

The player answers with their record. Not a button prompt — everything they've done in two years. Every Holdout they helped or didn't. Every Compact mission they ran or refused. Every community they saw sign the Protocol and what they did afterward. The record speaks before they do.

He listens. He notes it. He has the response ready — he's thought about it longer than the player has.

What happens next depends on the record.

---

## The End of the Road

The institution he built will not protect him. Institutions protect themselves — that's what they select for. When he becomes a liability to the structure, the structure will produce the mechanism to remove him. He built the mechanism. He will not see it coming because he still believes the institution is what he designed it to be.

The man who did what Jesus did and the man who did what Hitler did were both killed by the same machine — the institution that couldn't tolerate the feedback. Landon built his machine carefully and with good intentions and it will do what machines do.

Burke's read: *"He was the right man for Month 1. I'm not sure he's the right man for Year 2. I don't think he can tell the difference. That's not a criticism — it's a diagnosis."*

---

## Voice

Measured. No filler. He says the thing and waits to see if you followed it. He does not repeat himself for the benefit of people who weren't paying attention — he notes that they weren't paying attention and adjusts what he shares with them going forward.

He has a specific quality of stillness in difficult conversations — not coldness, not disengagement. The stillness of a man who has already decided what he's going to do and is giving you the courtesy of the conversation first.

He will not threaten you. He doesn't need to. The structure he built does that.

*"I'm going to tell you what I need from you. I'm going to tell you what I can offer in return. I'm going to tell you what happens if we can't reach an agreement. None of those things are negotiable. The conversation is."*
