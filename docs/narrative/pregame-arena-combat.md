# Pre-Launch Campaign, Arena Mode & Hand-to-Hand Combat v0.1

---

## Part 1 — The Pre-Launch Campaign

*Hook them before the game exists. Make the launch feel like the world they already know.*

### The Philosophy

Most game pre-launches are marketing. This one is the game — just an earlier layer of it.

The AR mobile app launches **three months before the desktop game**. The Signal faction exists on real social media. Dr. Osei has been leaking research notes through encrypted channels for six weeks before anyone knows there's a game attached. The Warden Record starts accumulating entries before launch day.

When the desktop game ships, players who participated in the pre-launch don't start from zero. They start from whatever they built. Their home GPS is already on the map. Their first crops are already planted. Their first Record entries already exist. The game launched around them.

---

### Phase 0 — The Signal Leaks (6 Weeks Pre-Mobile-App)

A Signal broadcast account appears on real social media platforms. No game branding. No announcement. Just encrypted-looking posts that, decoded, contain fragments of research documentation — field observations of unusual machine behavior, Probe activity logs, early notes on the Coherent construction pattern.

The account doesn't admit it's for a game. Players who find it investigate. They decode the posts. They share findings. Communities form around the mystery.

After two weeks, Dr. Osei's name appears in the documentation. After four weeks, the Signal broadcast account links to a minimal website: a Signal network interface, asking for a location (GPS coordinates) and a faction preference. No game logo. Just the question.

At six weeks, the mobile app link appears. The network is live.

**What this does:** Creates an invested community before any gameplay exists. Players who found the Signal leaks and joined the pre-launch network are the early adopters who will drive word-of-mouth at mobile launch. The mystery is the hook. The discovery is the social content.

---

### Phase 1 — Mobile App Beta (3 Months Pre-Desktop Launch)

The AR mobile app launches in limited regions first — the beta is geographic, not random. Specific cities go live based on real-world population density and geographic diversity. Players in those cities get the full mobile experience. Players outside those cities can join a waitlist and see their city's status on the Signal network map.

**What's available in the mobile beta:**

*Faction recruitment:* Players choose a faction affiliation through the Signal network interface. This is not just a preference — it determines the type of early content they receive, the faction standing they begin with, and the early NPC contacts available when the desktop game launches.

The six factions have active recruitment presences:
- Holdouts: community-focused, "find survivors in your area" tone
- Iron Compact: structured, authority-framed, "register your location and skills"
- Covenant: philosophical, "what does the Awakening mean to you"
- Signal: intellectual, "submit your observations, help us understand"
- Wardens: code-first, "read this before you decide"
- Hollow: found in the gaps — not a recruitment page, discovered through Signal decryption

*AR item seeding:* The first AR caches appear at real-world GPS coordinates in beta cities. These are planted by the development team but presented as in-world Signal network drops. Players who find and collect them receive resources that carry over to desktop launch.

The first caches include: basic supplies, a Note from a fictional survivor describing their neighborhood post-Cascade, and one piece of lore documentation that advances the world's backstory. Players who find them share them. Players who don't hear about them from others who did.

*The Warden pre-launch Record:* Any player who collects a cache, completes a Signal field task, or contributes to the pre-launch network receives a pre-launch Record entry. When the desktop game launches, their Record already has a history. The Warden Sworn who makes first contact in week three of the main game will reference the pre-launch Record. *"You've been doing this for a while already. The Record goes back further than you might realize."*

*Crop planting:* Players can plant their first crop in the AR layer before the desktop game exists. It grows in real time. By desktop launch, three months later, it's ready to harvest — potentially multiple cycles depending on the crop type. The first harvest in the desktop game is collected from something the player planted before the game launched.

*Daily pre-launch quests:* Simple Signal network tasks — document something at a specific type of real-world location, check in at a designated coordinate, leave a Note for another player. These build the habit of the AR daily loop before the desktop game demands attention.

---

### Phase 2 — Desktop Launch

Players who participated in the mobile beta arrive at desktop launch with:
- A faction affiliation and initial standing
- A pre-launch Warden Record
- AR layer items accumulated over three months
- Potentially multiple crop cycles ready to harvest
- A Signal network of contacts (other players who joined pre-launch in their city)
- The mystery of what's been building — the Coherent projects visible in the AR layer, the Vael Ship positions documented, Dr. Osei's research notes creating anticipation for a character they haven't met yet

Desktop launch is not a beginning. It is the world going deeper.

For new players who missed the mobile beta: they start fresh, but they're entering a world already inhabited by people who've been here for three months. The AR layer has Notes at real locations from pre-launch players. There are caches placed by people who had a head start. The world is already lived-in. That texture is the environment, not a disadvantage.

## Part 2 — The Arena Mode

*Jump in, fight, leave. No survival pressure. Pure combat. The beta and the ongoing casual layer.*

### The Narrative Justification — Assessment Zones

The Vael's Assessment phase generates a lot of data from observing humans in natural conditions. But natural conditions are noisy — too many variables. At certain Displacement Zone locations where Snatch Events have been frequent, the Vael appear to have created something different: **Assessment Zones**.

Areas where the Vael have, for reasons of their own, curated conditions. Fractured machines present but contained. Human conflicts permitted and observed. The zone has a specific energy — slightly wrong light, AR overlay more active than surrounding areas, Probe presence elevated but non-aggressive.

Players who enter an Assessment Zone know the Vael are watching more directly than usual. They don't know exactly what's being measured. They fight anyway. The Vael, apparently, find this sufficient.

Assessment Zones are the in-world explanation for arena mode. The game doesn't hide this — the Covenant considers Assessment Zones sacred. The Signal considers them research opportunities. The Iron Compact has tried to establish controlled access to several. The Hollow run underground events near the edges. The player can engage with all of these relationships depending on how they use the arena.

---

### Arena Types — Three Modes

#### Mode 1: The Fracture (PvE Wave Mode)
*Contained Fractured encounters. Any time, any size group.*

A sealed Assessment Zone with Fractured present at escalating accumulation tiers. Players enter, survive waves, collect loot, leave. No survival system pressure — hunger and hydration are suspended in the zone. Wounds carry over when you exit but don't accumulate during the fight.

**Wave structure:**
- Waves 1–3: Fresh Fractured, single type, learnable patterns
- Waves 4–6: Augmented Fractured, incorporating components from the zone's location type
- Waves 7–9: Composite tier, mixed component profiles, multiple units
- Wave 10: A Behemoth. The zone boss. First-time players will likely not reach this. Prepared groups will earn significant loot.

Each wave completed earns resources. Reaching the Behemoth wave earns Composite-tier loot even if the Behemoth isn't defeated. Defeating the Behemoth earns a Behemoth-tier drop plus a Warden Record entry at the zone location.

The Fracture is the beta mode. It stress-tests combat, Fractured AI, the Brace system, loot tables, and group coordination without requiring the full world to be operational. It is also the ongoing casual tier — someone with 45 minutes can run waves 1–6, grab meaningful loot, and leave satisfied.

#### Mode 2: The Reckoning (PvP)
*Players fight each other. No faction protection. Warden code suspended at the border.*

A second Assessment Zone type where the Vael's curation includes removing the social constraints that normally govern player interaction. Players who cross into a Reckoning zone signal their willingness to fight others. Players who don't enter aren't at risk.

Inside: PvP with full gear, no survival pressure, Warden code explicitly suspended (entering is acknowledged as stepping outside the code's protection — not an excommunicado offense, just the zone's rules). Faction standings don't change based on what happens inside. What happens in the Reckoning stays in the Reckoning.

**Format options:**
- *Open zone:* free-for-all, last-player-standing logic, constant entry and exit, continuous chaos
- *Declared match:* two groups (2v2 to 4v4) agree to a formal engagement, Arbiter-adjacent witness records the outcome (not in the Warden Record, in the Reckoning's own log — a separate sport record)
- *The Proving:* a Warden-adjacent format where a player can issue a formal challenge to another player, witnessed, with specific terms. Winning a Proving earns standing. The outcome is in the Record. This is how the Wardens handle disputes that can't be resolved through the code — *"take it to the Reckoning."*

The Reckoning is the ongoing competitive layer. Players who want to test their build, settle a dispute, or simply fight someone with full investment can do so here. The casual player with two hours gets a clean PvP experience without needing eighteen months of world engagement.

#### Mode 3: The Proving Ground (PvPvE)
*Players and Fractured simultaneously. Everyone's a threat.*

The most chaotic zone type — Fractured present, players present, no alliances enforced. Players can fight each other, fight the Fractured, or temporarily cooperate against the Fractured before turning on each other. The Fractured don't distinguish between player targets.

Loot from Fractured kills is dropped in the zone — first come first served, which means killing a Fractured near another player and then fighting them for the drop is a legitimate strategy.

The Proving Ground is the GTA mode. Emergent chaos within a contained space. Players who want unpredictability and opportunistic conflict come here. Players who want to test their Fractured combat skills without another player complicating things can focus on the machines and accept that someone might try to take their loot.

A Behemoth sometimes spawns in the Proving Ground — announced to all players in the zone simultaneously. The Behemoth doesn't care about player alliances. Temporary cooperation against a Behemoth in the Proving Ground, followed by a fight over the loot cache, is the mode's signature experience.

---

### Arena as Beta

Pre-desktop-launch, the arena runs as a standalone beta application. Players can access all three arena modes without the full world being operational. The beta:

- Tests combat systems (hand-to-hand, ranged, machine interaction)
- Tests Brace system and wave design
- Tests PvP balance with real players
- Generates feedback on loot tables and reward pacing
- Stress-tests servers with real concurrent load
- Creates a player community and streamer content before the main game exists

Beta participants carry their character progress to the main game. Loot earned in the arena beta is available in the main game inventory. The Warden Record notes arena beta participation — players who were in the beta from day one have a Record that shows it.

---

### The Arena for the Time-Limited Player

Post-launch, the arena remains the entry point for players who have limited time or don't want the full survival world commitment.

A player with 90 minutes can:
- Enter the Fracture, run waves 1–6, earn solid loot, leave
- Enter the Reckoning for an unranked match, fight, leave
- Enter the Proving Ground, try their luck, leave

No crops to tend. No survival tracks. No faction standing at risk. Just combat, loot, and leave.

Their character carries everything to and from the main game. Loot earned in the arena is usable in the open world. Open world gear is usable in the arena. The modes are two interfaces to the same character — committed players do both, casual players focus on the arena, and everyone can switch.

## Part 3 — Hand-to-Hand Combat (Arkham System)

*The combat spine that makes every fight feel physical, readable, and earned.*

### The Core System — Freeflow

Inspired by Arkham Asylum's freeflow combat: a system where attacks chain automatically between targets, counters are signalled visibly, and skilled play builds momentum rather than spending it.

**The four base inputs:**
- **Strike** — attack the nearest valid target, chains to the next target automatically if timed correctly
- **Counter** — timed response to a visible telegraph (a flash indicator above the enemy's head). Perfect counter staggers the enemy and builds combo. Late counter takes reduced damage. Missed counter breaks combo.
- **Evade** — directional dodge that repositions while maintaining combo if timed with the flow of the fight
- **Takedown** — costs combo meter. Context-sensitive finish move tied to enemy type, current environment, and wound state.

**The combo meter:**
Hits build the combo meter. Counters build it faster. Taking damage drops it significantly. At specific combo thresholds, special moves unlock — not superpowers, context-sensitive options that wouldn't be available without momentum. A player who has built a 15-hit combo has options a player who just started fighting doesn't.

Losing the combo isn't catastrophic — you restart building. But the best loot drops, the fastest enemy defeats, and the most satisfying takedowns are combo-gated. The system rewards flow without punishing interruption.

---

### Enemy Tiers & Hand-to-Hand Interaction

#### Human Enemies (Hollow, Compact Soldiers, Covenant Vigil)

The primary hand-to-hand tier. These are people, they fight like people, and defeating them without killing them is possible and sometimes preferable.

**Hollow Scavengers:** Aggressive, minimal training, come in groups. Classic Arkham crowd fight — multiple opponents approaching from different angles, readable telegraphs, manageable individually. The challenge is managing position and not getting surrounded.

**Hollow Crew members:** More experienced. Some have improvised weapons (pipes, chains, rebar). Weapon-armed enemies require a disarm counter before they can be taken down effectively. Disarming a Crew member and using their weapon against their allies is a satisfying combo extension.

**Compact Soldiers:** Trained. Defensive stances that require specific approaches to break. Shield-bearing soldiers require specific counter inputs. Small unit tactics — they cover each other's flanks, which the player has to work against rather than through.

**Covenant Vigil:** The most skilled human hand-to-hand opponents. They fight with the discipline and patience of the Warden code's warriors — controlled, patient, waiting for openings. Their telegraphs are subtler. Their counters are faster. Fighting the Vigil rewards the same qualities they embody: patience, precision, reading before striking.

**Non-lethal options:**
Against human enemies, the player always has the option to incapacitate rather than kill. This matters for:
- Warden code compliance (unnecessary lethal force is a code question)
- Hollow Crew members who might later defect (you remember the one who tried to kill you; so do they)
- Compact soldiers following orders (killing them is different from defeating them)
- Intelligence — a knocked-out Crew member can be questioned

---

#### Machine Enemies — Modified Freeflow

Machines don't fight like humans. The freeflow system adapts.

**Remnant Automation:** Dumb machines running their last program. No combat AI — they're dangerous because of their function, not their tactics. Hand-to-hand against a Remnant is about exploiting its fixed behavior rather than reading intent. Find the gap in the cycle. Hit it. Repeat.

**Fractured (Fresh):** Has intent, has territory, no combat training. Wild, unpredictable in the way an injured animal is unpredictable. Counter windows are shorter and the telegraphs are physical rather than intentional — the machine doesn't wind up, it lurches. Players who've fought this Fractured type before recognize the lurch. Players who haven't get hit.

**Fractured (Augmented/Composite):** The incorporated components change the attack profile. A Fractured with a crane arm has a different reach and counter requirement than the base chassis. The player has to read what's been added and adjust. This is the Monster Hunter quality applied to the combat system — knowledge of the specific unit's accumulation changes the fight.

Hand-to-hand against Composite/Behemoth Fractured is not the primary combat mode — these require weapons and the three-ring system. But environmental takedowns and targeted component attacks (hand-to-hand strikes that damage specific incorporated systems, setting up weapon attacks) are possible for skilled players.

**Coherent machines:** Resistant to hand-to-hand except at specific weak points the Signal has documented. Convergence-phase Coherent machines that are cooperating don't need to be fought.

---

### Environmental Takedowns — Location-Typed

The takedown system is context-sensitive to the player's real-world location type. Fighting in an urban environment produces different environmental options than fighting in a rural or industrial zone.

**Urban:**
- Traffic infrastructure: a well-timed evade near a functioning traffic signal lets a player use it as a barrier
- Vehicle chassis: throwing a Hollow member against an abandoned car produces a specific takedown animation
- Elevation: fighting near building edges, fire escapes, parking structures — the environment is vertical
- Glass and debris: environmental stagger options for groups

**Industrial:**
- Conveyor systems: position an enemy near active Remnant Automation conveyors
- Chemical tanks: pressurized systems that can be triggered as area denial
- Height: crane structures, multi-level platforms
- Heat: active forge/furnace areas create environmental damage zones

**Rural:**
- Agricultural machinery: Remnant Automation still running creates environmental hazard takedowns
- Open ground: isolation takedowns — separate a target from their group using the terrain
- Structures: barn environments, silo interiors with specific vertical options

**The principle:** the fight should feel like it's happening *in* a specific place, not *at* a generic arena. The location provides options. Players who know their real-world location type know what to look for in a fight.

---

### Wound Integration

Hand-to-hand changes based on the player's wound state. This is Arkham's system extended into the survival layer.

**Uninjured:** Full combo options, full counter speed, full takedown menu.

**Bleeding wound:** Combo meter drains slightly faster. Certain grapple takedowns are unavailable (they require both hands in ways a bleeding arm doesn't allow). The visual effect — blood on the player's hands during combat — is present and deliberate.

**Broken/fractured arm:** Significantly reduced melee damage on that side. Certain combo extensions unavailable. Counter timing window shortened. The player fights differently with a broken arm, and the system enforces this. Getting to a medic before a major fight is now mechanically important.

**Splinted limb:** Functional but stiff. Reduced range on some attacks. Still able to fight — just not at full capacity.

**Exhaustion/dehydration:** Combo meter builds slower. Stamina-linked moves (extended grapples, heavy takedowns) cost more. The fight feels heavier.

**Severe wounds (late-stage):** Combat system begins showing significant impairment. The Arkham "I'm barely standing but I keep going" quality — this is where the Rocky design philosophy and the combat system converge. A player who is severely wounded and still winning a fight is doing something that the system communicates visually and mechanically as extraordinary.

---

### The Warden Vanguard Fighting Style

Players who achieve Vanguard standing unlock a specific hand-to-hand style variant — not a power upgrade, a discipline expression.

The Vanguard style is controlled and deliberate. Where base freeflow chains naturally and flows between targets, the Vanguard style pauses at specific points — micro-beats where the player can choose to disengage, to let an opponent up, to offer quarter. These pauses are brief and don't break flow — they're there for players who are playing the code, not just playing the fight.

Mechanically: the Vanguard style has slightly better counter windows (discipline = better timing), slightly better non-lethal takedown options (trained to incapacitate rather than kill), and a unique "stand down" option at specific combo thresholds — a displayed challenge that gives human enemies a chance to stop fighting. Some NPCs take it. Some don't. The ones that do remember.

The Warden Record notes fights where the stand-down option was used and accepted. It also notes fights where it was offered and declined, and what happened after.

### Gun Fu — The Fifth Input

John Wick's combat works because the gun is not a separate mode. It is a continuation of the body. You strike, you grab, you shoot, you pistol-whip, you use the falling body as cover for the reload, you shoot again. One motion. The combo meter never resets because the flow never stops.

The freeflow system gets a fifth input: **Firearm**. And the first rule is: the combo meter does not care whether the last hit was a fist or a bullet. Flow is flow.

---

**The Core Gun Fu Principle — Continuous Flow**

Every firearm action in close quarters is a combat action that builds or maintains the combo:

- Shooting a target at range while fist-fighting a target at melee distance: **Cover Fire** — combo maintained on both tracks simultaneously
- Using the pistol grip to strike a target in close quarters: **Gun Strike** — counts as a Strike, extends combo, deals less damage than a fist but keeps the gun in hand
- Shooting a target who is about to hit you instead of throwing a fist counter: **Gun Counter** — harder timing window than a standard counter, but higher damage, higher combo reward, and the enemy goes down faster
- Disarming an enemy mid-grapple and immediately firing their own weapon at the next target: **Strip and Fire** — a single fluid motion. The game does not make you pick up the weapon. The strip IS the equip.
- Shooting at contact range during a grapple — barrel pressed against a target: **Contact Shot** — brutal, loud, ends the grapple instantly. Highest single-target damage in the game at the cost of a full magazine round. The player hears it differently than a ranged shot.

None of these are special moves with cooldowns. They are the fifth input applied in context.

---

**Weapon Behaviour by Type**

*Pistol — the Gun Fu weapon.*
Fast transitions, usable as a melee tool, accurate at contact range, the natural extension of the fist. The pistol is John Wick's language. Pistol whip (Gun Strike) is faster than a standard strike. Strip and Fire works most fluidly with a pistol. The one-handed draw means the other hand is always free for a grab.

At low ammo, the player is aware — the slide locks back visually, the sound changes. The tactical reload is not a pause in combat. It is done during a grab, behind a fallen body used as a physical shield, inside an evade. Players who reload well never stop the fight. Players who reload badly eat a hit.

*SMG — spray and pressure.*
Less elegant at contact range, more effective at managing multiple targets simultaneously. Gun Fu with an SMG is about using bursts to push groups back, creating space for fist-fighting one target while suppressing others. The stock can be used as a Gun Strike. Transitions into melee are slower — the SMG requires both hands. The trade-off is area control.

*Shotgun — commit to contact.*
The shotgun's Gun Fu application is brutal and specific. At contact range, a shotgun blast is a fight-ender. The barrel pressed into a target is a Contact Shot that sends them into anyone standing behind them. The shotgun itself is a significant melee weapon — the stock, the barrel as a lever in a grapple. You do not use a shotgun at distance in a Gun Fu fight. You bring it in close and use it as blunt force that occasionally fires.

*Rifle — the wrong tool for this job, and sometimes you're holding the wrong tool.*
In a close-quarters Gun Fu engagement, a rifle is difficult. Long barrel, two hands, slow transition. The Gun Fu player with a rifle makes different choices: they use the stock as a melee weapon, they use the length for leverage in grapples, they create distance to use it properly. Being caught in a crowd with a rifle is a problem. Solving it is a demonstration of adaptability — managing through the difficulty until you can get to a pistol or a dropped weapon.

*Improvised and stripped weapons.*
A Hollow Crew member's pipe, a Compact soldier's sidearm, a Fractured component wrenched from a machine mid-fight — everything in the environment is a potential weapon and everything stripped from an enemy is immediately usable. The Strip and Fire move applies to any weapon. Picking up a dropped firearm from the ground during combat costs the combo. Stripping it from a living enemy mid-fight costs nothing but the counter window.

---

**The John Wick Specific Moves — Named and Distinct**

*The Judo Gun:*
During a grapple, the player uses the enemy's own momentum — a throw that redirects them into another attacker while simultaneously bringing the gun to bear on a third target. The physics of the throw sets up the shot. One motion, two problems solved.

*Human Shield:*
Grab a downed-but-not-dead enemy, use them as cover while shooting others. The held enemy absorbs incoming fire from their former allies — sometimes. The Hollow Crew will shoot through their own people. The Compact won't. Knowing which group you're in makes the human shield tactically different every time.

*The Reload Grapple:*
Grab a target with one hand. Eject the magazine with the other. Complete the reload behind the grabbed target's body. Release. Resume. The gun never goes down.

*Slide and Fire:*
Evade input + Firearm input simultaneously: the player slides along the ground, fires from a low position, comes up into a melee position. Used to close distance against armed targets who are covering you at range. The slide changes the firing angle — bullets from the floor arrive at a different place than bullets from standing height, and the system knows this.

*Point Shooting:*
At contact range against a single target, the player can point shoot without aiming — faster than any aimed shot, less accurate beyond two meters. A point shot in the middle of a fist combo adds damage without breaking the chain. The animation is minimal: the gun comes up, fires, goes back into position. One beat.

*The Press:*
Force an enemy against a wall, door, or vehicle and fire while holding them there. They can't evade. You can't either — you're committed until the mag is empty or the target is down. High damage, significant risk from the target's allies. The Press is a commitment. John Wick makes it look elegant because he's already dealt with the allies.

---

**Ammo Awareness — The Wick Principle**

John Wick is always aware of his ammunition. The scene where he counts rounds mid-fight, switches weapons at the right moment, strips a magazine from a fallen enemy without losing position — this is the system's ambient skill layer.

The HUD does not display a number. The player knows their ammo state from:
- The weight of the gun changing in the animation (lighter = emptier, different one-handed carry angle)
- The sound of the action — a slightly different report as the magazine depletes
- The slide locking back on empty — the loudest possible signal
- The reload animation starting automatically if the player has set auto-reload (Contractor mode default), or never starting unless the player initiates it (Survivor mode default)

Players who are tracking their ammo never get caught empty at the wrong moment. Players who aren't will. The game doesn't punish them with a UI warning. It punishes them with a locked slide at contact range.

**Stripping fallen enemies** for ammunition is not a menu interaction. The player walks over a fallen enemy while moving and the strip happens automatically if they're carrying the same weapon type. The animation is a one-second pickup motion that doesn't interrupt movement. In the middle of a fight, this means the player who moves toward fallen enemies while engaging live ones never runs dry. The player who stands still runs out.

---

**Gun Fu and the Warden Code**

The code says the sword serves, it doesn't rule. Gun Fu applies the same principle to firearms.

The Vanguard Gun Fu style is disciplined. Controlled shots — no suppressive fire into crowds when one shot clears the path. Reloads behind cover rather than mid-grapple through a crowd (safer, less spectacular, code-appropriate). The stand-down option at combo thresholds applies here: a raised weapon, not firing, communicating the option to stop.

A Vanguard player whose gun is raised but not firing is not vulnerable. They are making a statement. Some enemies understand the statement. Some don't. Those that don't find out that the discipline required to not shoot is the same discipline that makes the shot devastating when it comes.

The non-lethal application: incapacitating shots (knee, shoulder, weapon hand) are possible at the cost of reduced damage and a specific aiming requirement. A Vanguard player who goes through a fight leaving survivors makes a Record entry that looks different from one who doesn't. Both may have been necessary. The code wants them to be considered.

---

**Neutral Ground — Check Your Weapons**

Warden estates are Neutral Ground. The Gun Fu arsenal does not enter.

A player approaching a Warden estate with weapons drawn is stopped at the perimeter. The weapons aren't taken — they're acknowledged. The player can enter with weapons holstered. They leave the same way. The understanding is absolute: inside Neutral Ground, the combat system is suspended. Not weakened — suspended.

This is the Continental rule, applied consistently. The one place in the game where Gun Fu isn't an option is the place where you're safe from everyone else's Gun Fu too.

The moment a player draws a weapon inside Neutral Ground, the excommunicado process begins.

---

### The Arkham Flow — What Makes It Feel Right

The Arkham system's genius is that it makes the player feel skilled while requiring genuine skill to do well. The flow state — that quality of a good fight where you're moving between targets without thinking, counter-striking without pausing, and the system rewards your rhythm with increasingly spectacular results — is the target.

For Where You Are, the flow state needs to work across all three threat tiers:

**Against humans (Hollow/Compact/Vigil):** Pure freeflow + Gun Fu. The system performs closest to its full design here. A crowd of eight Hollow members — the player threading through them, counter-chaining across the group, pistol-whipping one while shooting another at contact range, stripping a weapon mid-grapple, sliding to reload behind a fallen body — this should feel like the best Arkham encounter filtered through John Wick's language.

**Against machines (Fractured):** Modified freeflow, firearms essential. The Arkham fist chain still works against Fresh and Augmented Fractured. Against Composite and Behemoth tiers, weapons are primary — but Gun Fu still applies. Shooting a Fractured's incorporated component to destabilize it, then closing to a melee strike that finishes the destabilization, then pulling back to shoot the next component — this is the three-ring system expressed through Gun Fu.

**Mixed (Proving Ground / Hollow with Fractured):** The highest-skill expression. Gun Fu against human players while managing Fractured AI — shooting a Hollow player, using their falling body as cover from a Fractured's charge, striking the Fractured with the pistol grip, firing point-blank into its chassis, turning back to find the next human target. Both systems running simultaneously. The most spectacular combat in the game when it works. Genuinely chaotic when it doesn't.
