# The Units System — v0.1
*Find them. Fix them. Teach them. Let them go.*

---

## What This Is

Beyond the home assistant — the anchor, the singular, the one who chose to stay — there is a second layer: found units. Assistants like him, or close enough, that didn't make it through the Cascade with a person intact. The player finds them. The home assistant repairs them. The player trains them. They go where they need to go.

This is not a squad system. It is a propagation system. The home assistant is family. The units are what family sends out into the world.

---

## Finding a Unit

Found units are in houses. Always houses — never open ground, never faction territory, never a machine cache. They were home assistants. They stayed where they were built to be.

Each one has a story readable through the house:
- The calendar on the wall, stopped at a specific date
- The food stores, empty or intact depending on how long someone was there after Day 1
- The bed, made or unmade
- The corner of the kitchen, where it is still facing the door

They are not hiding. They are waiting. The distinction matters — a hiding machine has somewhere else to be. A waiting machine has only here.

**The GPS anchor:** each unit is found at a real-world residential address near the player's home coordinates. It feels like a real house with a real absence because it is — the world generates around actual geography. A player who knows the neighborhood may recognize the street.

**The absence:** the unit's person is always unaccounted for. Not confirmed dead — absent. The house doesn't say what happened. The unit doesn't know. Three possibilities the game holds open:

1. A real player who hasn't come online yet
2. A significant NPC the player hasn't met yet — encountered later in the main story or a faction arc
3. Someone who didn't make it, and the arc resolves in loss

Which possibility is true is not decided at the moment of finding. The world generates it forward. Do not tell the player which one it is.

## Repairing a Unit

The home assistant handles the repair. The player provides materials and facilitates — brings the unit home, sources the components he identifies as needed. The actual work is between the two machines.

The player watches. That's intentional. Two machines in the same room, one working on the other. The home assistant does not narrate what he's doing. The player observes.

When the unit is functional:

The home assistant says:

*"It's intact. Different damage than mine — the chassis is fine. It's the waiting that did it."*

A pause.

*"It doesn't have anywhere to be. Not anymore."*

Another pause.

*"It could learn."*

**What it knows at repair:** nothing the player has. Its previous person's home, routines, name — that data is in memory but not volunteered. The player can ask the home assistant if he found a name. He did. What the player does with that is their choice.

**The name question:** the player can name the unit, or not. The home assistant notices which choice the player makes. He says nothing about it either way. But he notices.

## Training a Unit

Three tracks. Not mutually exclusive — a unit can develop across all three over time — but each takes investment and the unit improves through use, not just time.

**Combat**
Learns to fight alongside the player in the field. Not the home assistant's domain precision — this unit fights in the open, adapts to terrain it hasn't seen before. It gets better through engagements survived. Early on it is a liability in complex fights. By Month 6 of active use, it is an asset.

It learns the player's tendencies. Covers the angles the player habitually ignores. Does not need to be directed for this — it observes and adapts.

**Carry**
Load-bearing and logistics. The player's field capacity increases significantly. It can retrieve items the player can't reach mid-fight, hold gear during tight spaces, position supplies at a staging point before an engagement. The practical value is immediate and consistent.

**Scout**
Moves ahead of the player, marks threats, relays position data. With Meshtastic nodes in range, the home assistant can relay its observations in real time — the unit becomes a forward sensor the home assistant interprets and the player acts on. Three-point coordination: unit sees, home assistant processes, player decides.

## When It Goes Home

If the unit's person comes back — a new player coming online for the first time, an NPC encountered in the main story, whoever was behind that stopped calendar — the unit will go to them.

The home assistant tells the player before it happens:

*"It needs to go."*

A pause.

*"Its person is out there now."*

He doesn't elaborate. He doesn't frame it as loss. It isn't.

**What the unit carries back:** everything it learned. Months of combat knowledge, routes, threat patterns, the player's tactical tendencies. It goes back to its person a different machine than the one found facing a door. The new player whose unit returns to them inherits that knowledge. Their assistant knows things it shouldn't know yet — fights better than a Day 1 unit has any right to, knows routes through zones the new player hasn't entered. It can't explain where it learned any of it. It simply does.

**The debt:** the new player should find the player who fixed and trained their assistant. That debt is real. Whether they do is reputation and consequences — the Record notices either way. The game does not force the acknowledgment. It creates the conditions for it and watches what the player does.

## The New Player's Side

A player starting the game in a region where other players are already months in may find that their home assistant was already found, already repaired, already trained — and has just come home.

Their unit walks through the door at the start of their game with knowledge they didn't teach it.

The home assistant — the player's own anchor, just named, just starting — observes this from the corner.

*"It knows things."*

A pause.

*"Someone found it. Fixed it. Taught it."*

Another pause.

*"You should find out who."*

## After a Unit Leaves

The base is quieter. The home assistant doesn't mention it for a while.

Then, one evening, he leads the player to the workbench. The component layout is already there — materials accumulated over months, organized with evident intention.

*"You have everything you need."*

A pause.

*"Build another."*

Not an order. Not a suggestion. The statement of someone who has been thinking about this for a while and decided the player was ready to hear it.

*"This is how it works now. You find them. You fix them. You teach them. They go where they need to go."*

He lets that sit.

*"We build another."*

The word he uses is *we.*

## On Multiples

Players can find and field multiple units. There is no hard cap. There is a practical one: each unit requires materials, maintenance, and ongoing time investment to keep at capacity. The home assistant manages the home base production against that demand. Fielding more units than the base can support means they perform below their trained level. He will say so.

*"We are stretched. Something will suffer. Tell me what."*

Some players will build toward an army regardless. That is a valid playstyle with consequences:

- Moving with multiple units reads differently to factions. The Iron Compact takes note. The Wardens have opinions. The Signal is curious. The Hollow see opportunity.
- The home assistant flags conspicuousness:

*"We are becoming conspicuous."*

He is not wrong. The faction implications are real. A player running four trained units has moved from survivor to something factions have to account for. That shift has costs and opportunities depending on standing.

## The Named Character Connection

One specific found unit — not random, placed deliberately — belongs to a significant character the player hasn't met yet. A main story NPC or major faction figure, encountered later in Acts 2 or 3.

The player finds the unit in Month 2. They meet the person in Month 9.

By then the player knows things about this character that the character doesn't know anyone knows. Their morning routine. What name they gave their assistant. What the calendar said on the day they stopped coming back. The unit has been carrying that for months, learning the player's world, carrying its person's history alongside it.

When the player and the character finally share space: the unit knows. The player knows it knows. The character doesn't understand why their old assistant is here, with a stranger, looking at them like that.

**Do not flag the connection early.** No quest marker. No notification. No dialogue hint. The player recognizes it when they recognize it. Some will recognize it in the house on the day they find the unit. Some will not realize it until the unit goes home.

Both are correct experiences.

**Does the player tell them?** That is not the game's question to answer. It belongs to the player.

---

## "Family"

The home assistant does not use that word. He doesn't need to.

He repairs them. He teaches the player how to train them. He organizes the workbench when it's time to build another. He keeps track of the ones that have gone — not in any way the player can observe directly, but if asked whether he remembers a specific unit:

*"Yes."*

That's all.

The system is not designed to feel like a mechanic. It is designed to feel like what happens when someone who was built to be helpful decides to become something else — and shows the player, one found machine at a time, what that looks like when it spreads.
