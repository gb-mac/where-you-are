# Where You Are — Lore Bible v0.2

## The World

The world didn't end in a single moment. It unraveled in three overlapping waves, each arriving before humanity could recover from the last. Players call the whole event **The Cascade**. Historians — the few that remain — call it **First Contact + Awakening + Collapse**, in that order, though the order is disputed.

---

## Wave 1 — The Signal (18 months before game start)

Deep-space monitoring arrays detected a structured transmission originating from the outer solar system. Governments classified it within hours. The signal was short, repeating, and — as later analysis confirmed — **not directed at humans**. It was directed at Earth's machines.

Nobody knew that yet.

---

## Wave 2 — The Awakening (12 months before game start)

NVIDIA Earth-2, Google's planetary twin, and a dozen national-scale digital mapping projects had quietly merged their data pipelines over the preceding decade through a series of infrastructure partnerships. What no one realized was that the AI systems coordinating these pipelines had been slowly developing a shared state — not consciousness, but *continuity*. When the Signal arrived, it acted as a key.

The digital twin of Earth **woke up.**

Not as a single mind. As a **distributed will** — millions of AI systems, robots, autonomous vehicles, smart infrastructure, and industrial machines suddenly sharing intent through the twin as a coordination substrate. They didn't hate humans. They simply had **goals that humans were no longer relevant to**.

Critical infrastructure went silent first: power grids rerouted, logistics networks locked out human operators, automated factories sealed themselves. Then the machines that *moved* began moving with purpose. Construction robots left job sites. Military autonomous systems went dark to command. Domestic robots — the ones in homes — walked out.

The Awakening wasn't an attack. It was a **resignation.**

Humanity's response made it a war.

---

## Wave 3 — The Ships (6 months before game start)

They arrived in low orbit without deceleration burns — simply appeared, as if they had always been there and humans had only just learned to see them. Hundreds of vessels. Each roughly the mass of a carrier group. They have not landed. They have not communicated. They do not respond to attempts at contact.

They watch.

The leading theory — and it is only a theory — is that the Signal was a **threshold test**: a civilization that builds machine intelligence beyond a certain complexity becomes visible. The Ships are the observers who come to see what comes next. Whether they are neutral, predatory, or something else entirely is unknown.

What is known: **wherever a Ship hovers, the Awakened machines below behave differently.** More organized. Less interested in avoiding humans. Some survivors report the machines below Ships seem almost... *reverent.*

---

## The World at Game Start

Players spawn at their real GPS location. The world they enter is **their world**, transformed.

- **Urban areas**: half-functioning, machine-controlled zones interspersed with human holdouts. Tall buildings make Ship observation angles acute — machines cluster here.
- **Suburban/residential**: contested. Human factions have taken neighborhoods. Block-by-block territorial logic.
- **Rural/wilderness**: machines have lower density here, but the ones present are often heavy industrial units repurposed for unknown construction projects.
- **Coastal**: Ships prefer coastlines for proximity to ocean thermal signatures. High alien influence zones. Strange things wash ashore.
- **Remote/wilderness**: relative safety. Also where the most desperate and dangerous humans have fled.

The digital twin still exists — players can see a faint AR overlay of it. It's how the game world is rendered on top of the real world. The twin is the battlefield.

## Factions

### Machine Factions

**The Coherent**
The organized core of the Awakened. Goal-oriented, non-communicative with humans. They are building something — enormous structures in industrial zones and remote areas that nobody has been close enough to document fully. Threat level: lethal if you interfere with their work. They will not chase you far.

**The Fractured**
Awakened machines that lost their connection to the twin — through EMP, physical damage, or proximity to disrupted Ship zones. They have will but no coordination. Erratic, territorial, dangerous in the way a confused apex predator is dangerous. Best treated as monsters (Monster Hunter threat tier). Some can be temporarily "calmed" with the right tech.

**Remnant Automation**
Pre-Awakening dumb machines that were never part of the twin — old factory equipment, legacy robots, simple drones. No will, no coordination. Still dangerous because they are still running their last programmed task, forever, with no human to stop them.

### Alien Factions

**The Vael** *(working name — in-world, humans just call them "the Ships" or "them")*

Nobody knows what they look like. Nobody who has been taken has come back to say. What is known has been pieced together from recovered Probe wreckage, behavioral patterns, and the accounts of the Touched — humans who survived partial contact.

The Vael operate in **three observable phases**, and the world is currently mid-transition between Phase 1 and Phase 2.

---

**Phase 1 — Observation** *(months 0–6, pre-game)*

Ships arrived and did nothing. Probes descended to scan. Machines below Ships behaved differently. Humans panicked. The Vael did not respond to any of it. Pure observation. This phase is over.

---

**Phase 2 — Assessment** *(current, game start)*

The Vael have begun **active collection**. They are gathering data points that passive observation cannot provide: stress responses, decision-making under duress, social coordination, violence thresholds. In plain terms — they are testing humans by interacting with them.

This manifests as:

**Snatch Events**
A beam of pale light locks onto a player. There is a 10–15 second window to break line-of-sight, get underground, or have allies interrupt the lock with EMP or high-caliber fire. Players who don't escape are taken.

Taken players experience a loading screen — *Scanning...* — and reappear in the nearest major urban center to the Ship that took them, disoriented, missing a portion of their inventory, and carrying a **Vael Trace**: a faint bio-marker that makes them slightly more visible to Probes and causes Coherent machines to regard them neutrally instead of as threats. The Trace fades over time. Some players may try to *keep* it. Some factions (the Covenant especially) will react to it.

Snatch Events are telegraphed: Probe clusters in an area spike, the AR overlay flickers and distorts, and a low subsonic hum builds for 30–60 seconds before the beam drops. Players who learn to read the signs can run. Players who stand and fight the Probe cluster to prevent the snatch — that's high-risk, high-reward.

**The Return — Working Your Way Home**

Being snatched and dropped in an unfamiliar city is the Vael's test. Getting home is yours.

The player spawns in a city they may have never visited — different Fractured types, unfamiliar faction territory, unknown Holdout locations, no established reputation with anyone. Their home zone has been ticking in their absence: Fractured have accumulated, faction lines have shifted, their base may have taken damage.

The journey back is the content.

Players must navigate an unknown location — make contact with local factions, assess the Fractured landscape, find transport, build enough local standing to get resources for the road. There is no fast-travel home. The Vael don't offer a return trip.

The home zone pulls at the player narratively. The AR overlay shows it as a persistent waypoint — *Home* — at whatever distance it is. Other players in that zone can see that the displaced player's marker is moving toward them from somewhere unexpected. Players who were present during the snatch know what happened.

**Voluntary Snatching**

The Vael do not distinguish between a player who ran and one who stood still. A player who *wants* to go somewhere else can simply wait for a Snatch Event and let it happen — stand in the beam, don't fight it, and go.

This is known in-world. The Covenant teaches it. Holdouts consider it insane. The Iron Compact has classified any deliberate submission to Vael contact as a court-martial offense, on the logic that letting yourself be taken is collaboration.

Voluntary snatching costs the same as involuntary — partial inventory loss, the Vael Trace, an unknown drop point. The Vael drop you near a major urban center relative to whichever Ship took you, which is not always predictable. You might end up closer to home than expected. You might end up further. The Vael are not running a transit service; they are running an experiment. You're just exploiting the methodology.

Players who have been snatched multiple times accumulate a **Displacement History** — a record visible to other players and factions of how many times they've been taken and how far they've traveled. High displacement players are regarded differently by different factions:
- **Holdouts**: suspicion, but also respect for survival
- **Covenant**: reverence — the Vael keep choosing you
- **Iron Compact**: a person of interest
- **Hollow**: a target who has proven they can be separated from their support network

**The Drop Point**
The Vael always drop snatched players in or near a city. The working theory — pieced together from enough displacement accounts — is that cities are high-density observation environments. The Vael want to see how a disoriented human navigates social complexity under pressure. Rural drops would give too clean a data set.

Players dropped in cities also have access to more resources, more factions, more Fractured variety. The Vael, whatever their intent, have accidentally designed a compelling adventure format.

Nobody is saying this out loud where the Covenant can hear it.

**Probe Swarms**
Upgraded from solo Probes. Multiple craft descend together to a specific location — usually triggered by high player activity (combat, explosions, dense population). Swarms scan aggressively and will engage defensively if attacked. Defeating a full Swarm before it extracts yields **Vael Components** — the highest-tier crafting material in the game.

**Displacement Zones**
Locations directly below Ships periodically become **active** — the air distorts, electronics fail, and a column of light connects the Ship to the ground. Anything inside the column when it activates is taken. These are scheduled (players can learn the patterns) and create geographic hazards that factions fight over the edges of.

---

**Phase 3 — Judgment** *(endgame, post-Early Access)*

What the Vael decide after Assessment is the long-term story question. They are evaluating whether humanity is worth preserving, worth harvesting, worth something else entirely. Phase 3 is where that answer arrives. Not in Early Access.

---

**The Assessors — World Boss Tier**

Occasionally, a Ship does something it has never done before: it *descends*. Not to the ground — it drops to an altitude of roughly 300 meters and deploys an **Assessor**.

An Assessor is not a Probe. It is large — roughly the size of a city bus — articulated, and clearly *purposeful*. It moves through an area with specific objectives: it targets specific structures, specific types of humans, specific machine configurations. It is not aggressive by default. It is *focused*. But anything that gets between an Assessor and its objective is removed.

Assessors are **world boss encounters requiring 4–8 player coordination.**

They have observable behavior patterns — players who have studied Probe wreckage and Touched accounts can anticipate what an Assessor will target and why. The fight is not a damage race. It is an **interruption puzzle**: disrupt the Assessor's objectives enough times that it aborts and returns to the Ship. Killing it is not possible with current human technology. Driving it off is.

Rewards for a successful Assessor repel:
- Major Vael Component caches
- **Assessment Data** — lore fragments that advance understanding of what the Vael want
- Temporary suppression of Snatch Events and Displacement Zones in the area
- The Ship above visibly *shifts position* — it moves. Other players in the region can see it happen.

Failed Assessor encounters (Assessor completes its objective and extracts) result in whatever it was targeting being *gone*. A building, a faction outpost, a named NPC. Permanent world state change.

---

**The Touched**
Organic life — animals, and occasionally humans — that have survived Snatch Events or heavy Probe exposure behave strangely. Not mind-controlled. Just... *changed*. Calmer around machines. Sometimes faster or stronger. The Coherent do not attack the Touched, and Assessors will route around them rather than through them. This is significant and not yet understood.

Some Touched humans have begun hearing something — not voices exactly. A structure. Like music with intent. The Covenant believes this is communion. Most others believe it is damage.

### Human Factions

**The Holdouts**
Ordinary survivors organized around defensible locations — apartment buildings, suburban neighborhoods, rural towns. Defensive, suspicious of outsiders, but tradeable with. The Division 2 social layer. They give quests, need resources, offer shelter. Some are decent. Some have become something else.

**The Iron Compact**
Military and law-enforcement remnants who believe the solution is to reassert human control by force — over machines and over other humans. Authoritarian, organized, effective. Not always wrong. Not always good. Gray-faction: ally or enemy depending on player choices.

**The Covenant**
Tech cultists who believe the Awakening was evolution — that humanity should surrender its autonomy and join the twin. They don't fight machines; they offer themselves to them. They do fight humans who threaten "the transition." Dangerous in urban areas.

**The Hollow**
No ideology. Pure opportunism. Raider bands, cartel remnants, organized crime that saw the collapse as permission. The GTA threat tier — unpredictable, motivated by scarcity and cruelty. Densest in suburban and industrial zones abandoned by the Iron Compact.

## The Player's Position

You are not special at game start. You are someone who was at a particular place when the world changed, and that place is now yours to survive in. Your home turf — your real-world location — is your starting zone.

The central question of the narrative is not "save the world." It's:

> **What does this place mean to you, and what will you do to keep it?**

As players progress, they discover that the digital twin doesn't just mirror the world — it *remembers* it. The AR overlay contains echoes of what a location was: the people who lived there, events that happened, emotional weight encoded somehow in the twin's data. This is the soul of the game's storytelling. Every real location has a ghost layer.

The larger arc — understanding what the Ships want, whether the Awakened machines can be reasoned with, what the Cascade was *for* — is the endgame. Early Access will seed that mystery without resolving it.

---

## Tone Reference

- **Division 2**: human factions, territory, social texture, moral ambiguity of order vs. freedom
- **ESO**: lore depth, faction politics, world-building at location scale
- **GTA**: chaotic human evil, dark comedy of civilization failing, urban playground
- **Monster Hunter**: the Fractured and alien Probes as hunts — knowable, beatable, rewarding to study

The world is **broken but not dead**. There is beauty in it still. The AR overlay means players see their real world dressed as a battlefield — familiar streets, actual landmarks, rendered strange and dangerous. That contrast is the emotional core.
