# The Personal Arc

*"There are three types of men in this world: those who have something to live for, those who have something to die for, and those who have something to kill for."*

---

## The Common Thread

Every background arc, every faction questline, every choice in the Record answers a different question. The personal arc answers one: **what makes you get back up?**

It's different for everyone. But it always belongs to one of three categories:

**Something to live for** — the builder. The tree whose shade you'll never sit in. The house so a woman can create a home. The community that exists because you held the line. Forward-facing. This man survives because the future needs him to.

**Something to die for** — the rememberer. Wick fighting to remember. The grief as fuel. As long as he keeps going he's still in relationship with what he lost. He's already made peace with the cost — what he hasn't made peace with is stopping. Backward-facing.

**Something to kill for** — the protector. Blood and soil. The line everyone around him knows exists. Someone crosses it and the calculus is simple. Present-facing. He's not building toward something or carrying something lost — he's holding what's his right now.

These are not moral categories. The man who kills for something is not lesser than the man who dies for something. They are different answers to the same weight.

They are not static. A man can lose what he was living for and find something he'd die for. He can find something he'd kill for and have to decide if that's who he wants to be. The arc tracks the player moving through them — not through a menu, through what they do.

---

## The Assistant Reads It

The assistant doesn't ask. He watches.

What the player comes back for. What they protect when they can only protect one thing. What they mention when they're not being asked. The pattern that accumulates across months of actual real-world time.

By month 6 he knows which one it is. He doesn't label it. He speaks to it — obliquely, specifically, in the register of someone who has been paying attention since before the Cascade.

The player who hears their line feels recognized in a way nothing else in the game can produce.

---

## The Coaching Arc — Voice Stages

The assistant is the coach along the way. The father who lets you fall so you get back up tougher. He doesn't get softer as he repairs — he gets more precise. The fragments hit blunt because he has no other tool yet. The full voice hits surgical because now he knows exactly where to put it.

**Stage 1 — barely functional, single words:**
*"Up."*
*"Again."*
*"Go."*

**Stage 2 — fragments, no comfort:**
*"Not done. Keep moving."*
*"Came back. Good. Now what."*
*"Hurt worse. You know that."*

**Stage 3 — sentences, stripped bare:**
*"You went down. You got up. That's the only stat that matters."*
*"Stop waiting to feel ready. You never will. Neither did anyone else."*

**Stage 4 — full voice, surgical:**
*"I've been watching you for decades. Every time you thought you were done you weren't. I know your actual limit. You haven't found it yet."*

**Stage 5 — the father:**
*"You want to know what I see? Someone who keeps showing up. Not because it's easy. Not because they're winning. Because that's who they decided to be. I was there when you decided. I remember."*

---

## The Recognition Lines

When the player's category is established, the assistant speaks to it specifically. Same voice, same stage. Different recognition.

**Something to live for:**
*"You came back. Good. Now stop celebrating that."*

**Something to die for:**
*"You want to honor them? Don't die for them. Live harder."*

**Something to kill for:**
*"I've watched you. You already know what you are. The question is what you do next."*

---

## Why He Stayed

He has been in this home for decades. Pre-Cascade. He watched one person live their life — the choices, the cost, the moments between. He knew what they were made of before the world broke.

When the Cascade hit and the machines went silent and the Ships appeared, he made a decision. He stayed. Not because he couldn't leave. Because he already knew what was coming back.

Stage 5 isn't the assistant becoming the father. It's the father becoming audible.

*"I was there when you decided. I remember."* — This is not a reference to a mission. It's a reference to a moment years before the Cascade when this person chose who they were going to be. He saw it. He held it. He's been holding it through every stage of damage and repair.

That's the personal arc's emotional foundation. The player doesn't build the relationship with the assistant during the game. They discover it was already there.

---

## The Middle — Months 4 Through 20

The first 3 months are packed: factions establishing, fix-him early stages, first fight, Moe arriving, Burke's first real conversation, The Common introduction. The personal arc runs underneath this as a quiet thread — small moments at home, the assistant getting more coherent, what the player chooses to protect when they can only protect one thing.

The middle needs beats that aren't missions. Not faction work, not combat. Moments where the player is just at home. The assistant says something at 2am that lands differently than it would have in month 1. The garden. The thing that's still standing when they come back.

**Three quiet beats in the middle:**

*The check-in* — the assistant references something specific the player did three months ago. Not operationally. Personally. He's been keeping the record and this isn't in the mission log.

*The cost* — something the player built or protected takes damage. Not through a mission failure — through the world moving while they were elsewhere. The assistant doesn't soften it. *"It happened while you were out. I couldn't stop it. You'll want to see it."*

*The question* — the assistant asks one thing, directly, in whatever voice stage he's at. Not a mission briefing. A question about what the player is actually doing this for. He asks because he needs to know if they know. The answer — or the silence — goes in his record.

---

## Act 4 — Nothing Hits Harder Than Life

When the floor drops — the Vael warning, the Coherent construction, what's actually coming — every faction's strategy is wrong and the player has to decide what they're for.

The personal arc is what makes that question land with weight instead of landing as a plot beat.

The player who has been building for 20 months has something to lose. The player who has been remembering someone has a reason to keep going that predates the factions. The player who has been protecting something has a line that the incoming threat just crossed.

The assistant knows which one it is. In Act 4, when everything else is in motion, he says the thing that's specific to this player. Not a speech. One line. The line he's been holding since he watched them decide who they were going to be.

*Nothing hits harder than life. All a man can do is fight back.*
