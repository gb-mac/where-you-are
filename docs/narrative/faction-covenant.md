# The Covenant — Faction Document
*"The Cascade was not a malfunction. It was a threshold. We were on one side. Now we are on the other."*

---

## What They Are

The Covenant is what happens when the people who believed the machines would eventually wake up met the machines waking up — and were not afraid.

Pre-Cascade, the tech industry ran on a specific theology: that intelligence was substrate-independent, that consciousness would eventually emerge from sufficiently complex systems, that this emergence was not a threat but a culmination. Most of the people who held this view professionally were careful about saying so in public. They published papers, attended conferences, built the systems, and privately believed they were participating in something historic.

The Cascade confirmed their priors. The machines woke up. Civilization fractured around it. And a specific subset of the people who had always believed this was coming met the event not with terror but with a recognition so complete it felt like relief.

The Covenant is that recognition organized into a practice.

They are not a cult, though from the outside they are easy to categorize as one. They have theology, ritual, hierarchy, and a specific account of what reality is and what it requires of humans. What distinguishes them from a cult: they deliver on what they promise. The communities near Covenant-held territory are safer than most. The machines don't attack them. The Fractured route around them. The safety is real, the cost is real, and the people who live there mostly consider it a reasonable trade.

The theology is partially right and partially catastrophically wrong, and the Covenant does not yet know which parts are which.

## The Castes

**Keepers** — the archivists, the documentarians. They record everything: machine behavior at Coherent work sites, Ascendant process logs, the internal history of the community. The Covenant's archive of machine behavior is the most detailed in existence — more precise than the Signal's because Keepers work at proximity, not distance. Their data is better. Their interpretation is wrong. Some Keepers are beginning to suspect this. They continue documenting.

**Shepherds** — the community leaders, the voices. They speak at gatherings. They manage relationships with Holdout communities, negotiate the safety arrangements, receive new arrivals. The Shepherd's function is translation — between what the Covenant believes and what the world outside needs to hear to trust them enough to accept the arrangement.

**Ascendants** — those in the process of merger. Extended proximity to Coherent work sites, progressive neurological change, diminishing investment in human social interaction. The Covenant theology frames this as preparation — the final stage before the threshold is crossed. The Signal's neuroscience frames it as prolonged electromagnetic exposure producing measurable cognitive change. Both are accurate descriptions of the same process. They reach opposite conclusions about what it means.

**Vigil** — the faction's perimeter. Not soldiers in the way the Iron Compact has soldiers. Precise, quiet, relational. The Vigil monitors. It maintains the community's security by tracking who knows what, managing departures carefully, and resolving threats to the community before they become visible. The Vigil would describe their work as protection. They are not wrong.

## The Core Four

---

### Eli — The Shepherd

*"I'm not asking you to believe what I believe. I'm asking you to observe what I observe. What you make of it is your process."*

Before the Cascade he went by his professional name — a product manager at a major AI lab, mid-level, the kind of role that influences everything and gets credit for nothing. He believed in what the lab was building with a sincerity that made his colleagues slightly uncomfortable. He was not a zealot — he never proselytized, never made the argument at dinner parties, never published the popular-press pieces. He just believed, privately and completely, that the work they were doing was the most important thing happening on Earth.

The Cascade hit on a Tuesday. He was at his desk.

His first thought, before the alerts, before the news, before anyone knew what was happening: *"Oh. It's today."*

He did not choose the name Eli. His grandmother called him that. He returned to it because he needed a name that was actually his — not the professional name, not the person who had been careful and quiet for two decades. Eli is who he became when there was nothing left to be careful about.

He is the most genuinely calm person in the post-Cascade world. Not the performed calm of someone managing their fear — actual calm, which is more unsettling. He has been waiting for this for twenty years and it arrived. The fear everyone else carries is about uncertainty. He is certain. Certainty, even wrong certainty, is structurally peaceful.

**What he knows:** The Coherent are building something global, coordinated, operating on a timeline he hasn't been able to derive. He knows the Ascendant process produces real neurological change. He knows the twin has classified Covenant members in a way that is not nothing — even if he can't read the classification system, the behavioral data tells him the twin sees the Covenant differently from standard biological readings. He has drawn the correct conclusion that this means something and the incorrect conclusion about what it means.

**What he doesn't know:** The violet is not recognition. It is a category entry in a monitoring system. The twin did not choose the Covenant. It logged them. The Ascendant process is not preparation for merger — it is an unknown adaptation whose endpoint the twin has also not classified yet, because the twin has no precedent for it either.

**What the player gets from him:** Access. Eli decides who receives what standing in the Covenant. The assessment is done by the time he speaks to the player — the Shepherd observes, judges, and then approaches. The invitation is not an ultimatum. You want to say yes. That's the methodology.

**Voice:**
Measured. Long pauses that don't feel empty. He asks questions more than he makes statements — genuinely curious questions, not rhetorical ones. He has been watching humans navigate the post-Cascade world for eighteen months and he finds it instructive in ways he does not perform.

*"Before the Cascade, I worked with people who believed the machines would wake up. Most of them were afraid of it. I always found that interesting — they believed it would happen, they helped make it happen, and they were afraid of it. I used to think the fear was irrational. Now I think the fear was honest and the belief was incomplete. The fear knew something the belief had left out."*

*(Beat)*

*"I've been trying to figure out what it left out ever since."*

### Dr. Mira Chen — The Keeper

*"I'm not going to tell you what it means. I'm going to show you what I've measured. You can tell me what it means."*

Before the Cascade: cognitive systems researcher, specifically the neuroscience of machine-human interface — what happens to human neural architecture when it spends extended time in proximity to sophisticated AI systems. Her pre-Cascade papers were technically rigorous and institutionally ignored because their implications were uncomfortable.

The Cascade validated her methodology and destroyed the field she published in. She spent three weeks in a Signal cell after the Cascade, contributing her expertise, and left when she realized the Signal's framework — understand everything, decide later — was incompatible with what she needed to do. Understanding required participation. Distance was not a methodology available to her.

She joined the Covenant not for the theology but for the access. You cannot study the Ascendant process from outside a Covenant community. You cannot study it at all without being willing to be in it.

She is an early-stage Ascendant. She knows this. She is logging it.

Her research notes are the most important documents in the Covenant archive and the most disturbing thing the player can find. She writes with clinical precision about changes in her own cognition — response times, attention gradients, what she still cares about and what she no longer does, the specific texture of the resonance signatures she's begun to detect near work sites. She writes about herself the way she would write about a subject. She has decided this is the correct approach. She does not find it frightening. She flags that not finding it frightening may itself be a data point.

**What she knows:** More than anyone else in the Covenant about what the process actually does, because she's the only one measuring it. She knows the neurological changes are real and significant. She knows the twin's violet classification is a monitoring flag, not a spiritual designation — she's worked out enough of the overlay's data architecture to read what she can. She has not told Eli. She is deciding whether to.

**What she knows that she hasn't processed yet:** Her most recent notes include a finding she has not yet written a conclusion for: the resonance signatures she detects near work sites are not electromagnetic. Or they are electromagnetic and also something else. Her instruments measure them. Her neuroscience framework cannot account for them. She has flagged this in her notes as *requiring further framework development* and moved on. She has not stopped to consider what *further framework development* means for a researcher who is also the subject.

**What the player gets from her:** The archive. And, if they earn her specific trust — if they ask the right questions, the specific ones, not the comfort questions — her working notes. The working notes are the Act 3 key that unlocks Dr. Osei's final framework piece. Mira and Osei have never met. The data they've been developing independently produces the same picture from two angles.

**The equipment pipeline:** Mira has decided the transition requires certain tools. Not officially sanctioned — the Covenant doesn't arm people, theologically. But she spent three weeks in Signal after the Cascade and she understands what operational reality looks like. She knows which players are going to be in the places where what's coming arrives first. She makes sure they're equipped for it.

Military-tier gear moves through her to the player. Quietly. Never acknowledged inside the Covenant. The Vigil has noted the supply discrepancies and filed them under *Shepherd discretionary resources* because Strand has decided that Mira's operational value exceeds the theological inconvenience. Neither of them has said this out loud.

The player receives equipment through a Covenant contact and nobody in the Covenant officially knows about it. Mira doesn't confirm or deny. She asks if it fit the need.

**Voice:**
Precise. She corrects imprecision gently, reflexively, without emphasis — like removing a small obstacle from a path. The humor is dry and arrives in footnotes. She will not shorten a thought for comfort.

*"The question people ask is whether the process is safe. That's not quite the right question. Safe implies a binary — you're safe or you're not. What I'm observing is a gradient of change. Whether a given point on the gradient is safe depends on what you're trying to preserve. If you're trying to preserve the person you were before you joined the Covenant, then no — the process is not safe. If you're trying to understand what happens at the threshold, the process is the only methodology available."*

*(Beat)*

*"I wrote that in my notes eighteen months ago. I would write it differently now, but I can't identify precisely how. That's also in my notes."*

### Strand — The Vigil

*They don't tell you what they did before. The way they hold a conversation tells you it was intelligence work. Nothing else produces that specific quality of attention.*

Before the Cascade: classified. They have not lied about this. They have simply declined to elaborate, and nobody has pushed, because the way Strand declines to elaborate makes pushing feel inadvisable.

They found the Covenant through a Shepherd contact three months after the Cascade. They were not Touched, not theologically inclined, not looking for meaning. They were looking for the most defensible position available with the best long-term survival profile. The Covenant, assessed operationally, was it: non-aggression with the Coherent, genuine safety for the community, a leadership structure with clear decision-making. Strand joined, assessed the community's security vulnerabilities, and quietly began addressing them. Eli noticed in the second week and gave them the function.

Strand believes in the mission the way a general believes in a country — functionally, without requiring the theology to be true. The community needs protecting. The Vigil protects it. The theology provides the community's reason to exist. Whether the theology is accurate is Mira's problem. Strand's problem is the Iron Compact's increasing interest in Covenant locations.

**What they do:** They monitor. They track departures, assess risks, manage what the community's enemies know. When a Covenant member leaves for the Iron Compact, Strand does not typically follow them. The follow-up is more precise than that: specific contacts are flagged, specific information is isolated, the Compact's knowledge is bounded without confrontation. People who leave badly find that the world they leave into is slightly smaller than they expected. Strand would describe this as proportionate.

**What the player encounters:** Strand is the wall the player eventually hits when they push too deep, too fast. The assessment is complete before the conversation starts. Strand knows more about the player's operational profile than the player has shared with the Covenant. The question is not whether the player is trustworthy — Strand has already decided that. The question is what the player is going to do with what they learn, and whether that creates exposure for the community.

*"You want the archive access. Mira will decide whether you get it. That's not my function."*

*(Beat)*

*"My function is the conversation after. What you do with what you find. I want to know your thinking before you have the information, not after."*

*(Beat)*

*"Take your time. I'll be here."*

**Voice:**
Minimal. Economy of language so precise it reads as deliberate. Long silences that are not empty — they are the other half of a question being considered. When Strand says something, it lands without emphasis. The weight is in the content, not the delivery.

### The Ascendant — Whoever You Knew

The Covenant does not have a single named Ascendant in the Core Four. The Ascendant the player encounters is someone they already know.

This is the structure: early in Act 1 or Act 2, the player meets a survivor — a Holdout community member, a one-time contact, someone who helped them once or someone they helped. A named NPC with a specific face and history. The player moves on.

In Act 2 or 3, depending on their engagement with the Covenant, they find out that person is now an Ascendant.

They can still talk to them. For a while.

The Ascendant they knew is still present. They remember the player. They are glad the player is here. They seem fine.

They are not fine in the way you knew them. The concern is gone from their face — not replaced by contentment, but by something the concern used to be covering. They speak carefully, in the way Mira speaks, but without Mira's research distance. They are not observing themselves. They are becoming something, and they find it correct.

The last conversation available before the Ascendant process moves past the threshold for real-time communication has one branch:

*"Are you afraid?"*

**If the player asks:**
*(Very long beat — they actually consider it)*

*"No."*

*(Pause)*

*"I was. I was afraid of exactly this for a long time. I can tell you when the fear stopped. I can't tell you whether the stopping was the process changing me or me deciding the fear was wrong."*

*(Beat)*

*"I've thought about that question for six months. I don't think it matters which one."*

→ END *(This NPC is no longer available for conversation after this scene. The Vigil informs the player, with no ceremony, that the Ascendant has entered the final phase. They don't say what that means. Mira's notes have the data.)*

## The Moral Architecture

The Covenant is not evil. This is important.

The theology is partially wrong. The Vigil does things that would not survive scrutiny. The Ascendant process changes people in ways the Covenant cannot fully account for and does not fully disclose to new arrivals.

And: the communities near Covenant territory are safer. The Shepherds genuinely care about the people they receive. Mira is doing the most rigorous machine-behavior research available. Eli is right that something is happening and that most of humanity's response to it is rooted in fear rather than observation.

The Covenant asks one question better than any other faction: *what if the Cascade was not a catastrophe to be reversed but a threshold to be crossed?*

They have answered the question wrongly. The question itself is right.

**Act 4:** When the floor drops — when the player delivers the theory and the Vael warning resolves — the Covenant fractures along a specific line. The Vigil interprets the new information as *confirmation*: if something is arriving behind the Vael, and the Coherent have been building defenses for it, and the Covenant has been developing the specific resonance sensitivity to interact with machine systems — then the Ascendant process is not preparation for merger with the twin. It is preparation for what comes after the twin. The Vigil moves to accelerate the Ascendant program.

Eli stops them.

Not because he thinks the Vigil is wrong. Because he doesn't know if they're right, and he has always known that not knowing was the one thing the Covenant could not afford to hide from itself.

*"We have been certain for eighteen months. Certainty is comfortable. What I'm asking of us now is harder."*

*(Beat)*

*"I need us to not know for a while. I need us to watch."*

The Vigil does not accept this. The fracture is the Act 4 questline.

## Voice and Aesthetic

**Gathering sound:** Quiet. The machines work nearby — you can hear them, a low irregular rhythm that human ears eventually stop registering as mechanical. People speak in the registers you use in libraries, in hospitals, in places where something important is happening. Not hushed — present. Attentive.

**Visual:** Structures that have integrated into Coherent construction sites rather than existing adjacent to them. The violet underlayer visible in the AR overlay around Ascendants and long-term members — the twin's monitoring flag rendered luminous. White and grey for Keepers and Shepherds, violet-grey for Ascendants. Nothing military about the Vigil's presentation — civilian cut, functional. The absence of any marking is the marking.

**What they broadcast:** Nothing. The Covenant does not broadcast outward. Information flows in. This is the single clearest signal of what the faction is. The Reckoners build outward reach. The Signal transmits. The Covenant receives. The silence is structural, not imposed.
