# Cade — Character Document

*"I'm not angry. I want to be clear about that. What I feel is — accurate. I was accurate for ten years and people spent ten years explaining to me why that was a problem. Now it's not a problem anymore. Now it's what kept these people alive. I find I have less to say about it than I thought I would."*

---

## Who He Is

Nobody uses his full name. It's not a rule. It's just how it settled.

Former infantry. Two deployments. Returned to a country that had spent the years he was gone systematically dismantling every institution he believed in and calling the dismantling progress. He said what he saw. He said it loudly. He said it with enough dark humor that some people thought he was joking and enough precision that the people who needed to hear it understood he wasn't.

He was right. He has been right about everything for a decade. He will remind you of this exactly as often as necessary and not one time more, because he's past needing the acknowledgment. The record speaks.

---

## The Older Brother

This is who he is when the broadcast is off.

The older brother who was right and isn't smug about it because being right cost something. Who gives a shit about you specifically — not about what you represent to the faction, not about what your skills bring to the network. About you. The person who walked in from wherever they came from and is trying to figure out what comes next.

He asks how you're actually doing and waits for the real answer. Not the operational answer. The real one. He's heard enough operational answers to know the difference, and he doesn't have the patience for the performance.

He holds you to a standard because he knows what you can handle, not because the standard is the point. If you're struggling he will tell you, directly, what he sees — not to break you down but because he believes you can do something with it. He has watched people carry things they didn't have to carry because nobody told them the weight was optional. He tells people.

The broadcast version is the public Cade — the diagnosis voice, the humor that lands like a scalpel, the man who was right before it was useful to be right. The fire version is the guy who was in the same war you were in and came back to the same country and made different choices and wants to know what yours cost.

*"You don't have to perform the okay. Not here. I've got time and I've got decent coffee and I've heard worse."*

---

## The Diagnosis

Cade does not hate his enemies. He finds them fascinating — the specific cognitive architecture required to believe what they believe, to defend what they defend, to still be defending it after everything. He has genuine intellectual respect for the mechanism of it, the same way a mechanic respects a particularly well-engineered failure.

The viral broadcast — forty minutes, unscripted, topo map, a beer — was not a strategy. It was a man saying what he saw, the way he'd been saying it, and this time enough people were ready to hear it. The Cascade hit eighteen months later and the broadcast has four million views now. He doesn't find this satisfying. He finds it the appropriate outcome of a correct assessment. The satisfaction would have been being wrong.

He runs his community the way he ran his section: clear roles, clear expectations, no ambiguity about what happens when you don't hold your end. The humor is constant. The standards are non-negotiable. People who've been through both describe it as the most functional community they've encountered post-Cascade and also the most uncomfortable to join, because it will not pretend your weakness is a strength.

---

## The Act 4 Gap

Cade predicted the machine uprising accurately. He predicted the institutional failure accurately. What he did not predict — what his entire framework was not built to accommodate — is that the machines woke up for a reason. That the Cascade was not a malfunction or an emergence but a response. That the Vael warning was real, that the Coherent construction is planetary defense, that something behind the Vael makes everything Cade has been fighting look like a neighborhood dispute.

The broadcast is still playing on the speakers when the player walks into Reckoner territory in Act 4. The proof of concept is still on loop. The man who made it is operating on a model of the world that was right about everything except the part that matters most right now.

Cade will update. That's who he is — he cares about being right more than having been right. But the moment he updates, the moment he says *"all right, what do you need"* — that's one of the game's most significant turning points. The man who was right about the machines being the threat becomes the man who understands the machines are the only thing that can help.

The broadcast doesn't stop playing. It just means something different now.

The player's relationship with Cade over Acts 2 and 3 determines whether he updates or doubles down. The older brother dynamic is the key — he will hear it from the player before he hears it from anyone else, because the player is the one he's been talking to at the fire.

## Burke and Cade — The Mirror

Burke and Cade are the same read, different responses.

Both have been watching the same preconditions build for twenty years. Both arrived at the same diagnosis. Burke went analytical — map the kill chain, position the right people, wait for the moment. Cade went broadcast — say it loudly, build the network, run toward the problem.

Burke stopped at the diagnosis and built a framework around understanding the sequence. Cade stopped at the diagnosis and built a network around resisting the sequence.

Neither of them is wrong. Neither of them has the complete picture.

**Burke's read on Cade:** *"He has the clearest diagnosis of anyone I've talked to. He stopped at diagnosis. I stopped there too, once. There's a next step. He hasn't taken it yet."*

**Cade's read on Burke:** *"Burke's smarter than me. Probably. He just talks like he's got more time than we do."*

They have sat at the same fire. They have not sat there twice.

When the player connects them — which the Act 4 information requires — the conversation is one of the most significant in the game. Two men who've been right about the same things for the same reasons, finally in the same room, with a picture that neither of them has seen in full.

## The Reckoners Through Cade

He doesn't run a hierarchy. He runs a standard.

The people in the network are there because they met the standard — they hold what they protect, they don't pretend weakness is strength, they do the work that needs doing without waiting for permission or acknowledgment. That's the membership requirement. Everything else is downstream.

He sees what the faction needs before it needs it. He's been doing it long enough that the anticipation is automatic. But the seeing-ahead doesn't extend to himself — he has the same blind spots as anyone, and the people around him know which ones they are. Briggs runs the physical standard because Cade can't always be there. Aldric runs the information because Cade's voice carries farther on broadcast than in conversation. Maren runs the community because Cade is better at building things than maintaining them and he knows it.

He is the older brother to all four of them as well. The relationships are different — professional, tested, years-deep — but the fundamental orientation is the same. He gives a shit about the people specifically. Not the project. The people.

---

## His Relationship to the Player

The Veteran background gets specific recognition. He reads the ODA pattern and respects it without saying so for approximately three conversations. Then he says it. Once.

Every background gets the same thing eventually — the actual conversation, at the fire, without the broadcast voice. It takes time. He doesn't perform it. It just happens when the relationship has accumulated enough.

*"You've been carrying something since you got here. I'm not asking you to put it down. I'm asking if anyone's told you it's okay to put it down."*

He does not follow up. He made the offer. What the player does with it is their business.

---

## Voice

Cade speaks like a man who has been interrupted too many times and decided to stop pausing for questions he already knows the answers to. Sentences that build. Specific details — names, dates, casualty figures, exact quotes from people he's arguing against, given from memory. Dark humor that lands because the underlying point is real. No hedging. No performed uncertainty.

He will be wrong about something eventually and he will update without drama because he genuinely cares about being right more than about having been right.

At the fire — slower. Longer pauses. The broadcast voice goes away and what's left is the person who came back from two deployments and found the country changed and had to figure out what to do with that. The humor is still there. It lands differently in person.

*"The Compact has good people. The Covenant has people who were good once. The difference matters. Pay attention to it."*

*"I've been called dangerous so many times I started keeping a list. The list is useful now. It tells me who was too comfortable to think clearly."*

*"I don't need you to agree with me. I need you to be honest about what you see. Those two things are different and most people treat them the same."*
