# Ferry — Character Document

*"You want in. Everyone wants in. The question is what you're bringing."*

*(Beat — he looks you over)*

*"We'll find out."*

---

## Who He Is

Nobody calls him by his full name. Ferry. Just Ferry. The Ferryman's Toll — that's what he called his broadcast before the Cascade, back when he was doing after-show commentary on Mac's streams, running the community infrastructure while Mac was the voice. The name stuck in a different way after. Now it's literal.

You want to cross into Reckoner territory and be recognized as something other than a visitor, you pay the toll.

Ferry is Mac's second. Not in the chain-of-command sense — there is no chain of command, that's not how the Reckoners work — but in the way that matters. Mac makes the call. Ferry makes sure it happens. When Mac is unavailable, Ferry's read is the one that counts. He's been there since the beginning. He built as much of the infrastructure as Mac did, possibly more, because Mac was the one talking and Ferry was the one making sure the lights stayed on while he did it.

Construction background. Before the Cascade he was building things — schools, commercial, whatever was in front of him. He knows how structures fail. He knows the difference between load-bearing and decorative. He applies this to people with the same precision he applies it to buildings.

He trains. He has always trained. Post-Cascade he runs the physical standard for the entire network — the requirement you have to meet to be considered operational rather than support. He designed it. He passes it himself every month in front of whoever wants to watch. He is not young. He passes it anyway.

---

## The Toll

Every faction has an entry point. The Wardens have the oath. The Compact has the intake process. The Covenant has the recognition.

The Reckoners have Ferry.

It is not a formal ritual. There is no ceremony. You show up, you've been noticed, someone brings you to Ferry. He looks at you for a while. Then he either says *"not yet"* and walks away, or he says *"all right"* and it starts.

What it is: a fight. Not a sparring session. Not a test with clear rules and a referee. A fight, with Ferry, in whatever space is available, until one of you decides it's done.

Ferry has never lost.

He is not unbeatable. He is the standard. The Reckoners need to know that the people they're putting operational weight on can handle operational weight. A fight with Ferry tells him what he needs to know in about ninety seconds. Not whether you win — whether you're honest about what you are. Whether you adapt. Whether you keep your head when it's going badly.

Mac watches from somewhere elevated. He doesn't speak during it. When it's done he comes down.

The debrief is the real admission.

---

## Paying the Toll

Most people reach 50% and Ferry steps back.

*"All right. You paid the toll."*

That's the standard admission. You held under pressure, you adapted, you were honest about what you are. That's enough. Mac comes down. You're in.

## When the Player Beats Him Fully

Rare. Ferry goes down. He stays there a moment. Gets up.

*"Huh."*

*(Long beat)*

*"You paid the toll and then some."*

No drama. No ceremony. But the standing is different and permanent — Ferry will back the player in any internal dispute without being asked, hold them to a higher standard than anyone else because he knows what they can handle, and treat them as an operational peer rather than a proven member. These are not the same thing in the Reckoners.

Ferry being the one who lost is not a problem for the faction. It's proof the standard works — the toll exists to find the people who can pay it, and occasionally it finds someone who can pay more than the toll.

What it creates internally: Ferry respects the player in a specific way after this. Not warmth — respect. He will be harder on the player than on anyone else because he knows what they can handle. He will also back them in any internal dispute without being asked. He does not make promises. He keeps them.

## The Thing to Watch

Ferry is the enforcer of the standard. He is also the one most likely to use the standard as cover for something smaller.

He won't do it with the player — the player beat him, that's a different category. He'll do it with people who remind him of the people he's spent his life watching fail. The Compact bureaucrat who found religion after the Cascade. The Holdout community that kept choosing process over function. He reads the type fast and he does not give it a second look.

Mac knows this. He doesn't always move fast enough on it. Ferry's judgment is right ninety percent of the time. The ten percent is where the faction has a problem it hasn't fully named yet.

The player will see both sides. What they do with the ten percent is a choice.

## Voice

Economical. He doesn't rant — that's Derek's register. Ferry says the thing once, clearly, and waits to see if you got it. If you didn't get it he'll say it again, slightly slower, and that's the last time.

Dark humor that comes from somewhere genuine. He's seen enough that the absurdity of things lands on him as comedy rather than tragedy. This is not coping. It is a genuine orientation toward a world that has confirmed his expectations.

*"The Compact sent four guys to talk to me last week. Professional. Good kit. I told them I wasn't interested. They insisted. We had a conversation about it."*

*(Beat)*

*"They're not going to insist again."*

*"You look like you've been in a few of these. Good. Means we don't have to start from the beginning."*

*"Mac's up there. He's watching. Don't look at him. Look at me. I'm the part that matters right now."*

---

## Where He Is

Ferry doesn't have a fixed location. He moves. This is intentional and structural.

The Reckoner network spans geography. Mac has a home territory. Morgan broadcasts from hers. Derek from his. Ferry is the one who travels between them — checking nodes, running the physical standard across the network, making sure the thing holds in places Mac can't be.

This solves the geographic anchor problem. The Reckoners don't require all four leaders in one city. They require a network. Ferry is how the network stays a network.

His comms reach the player everywhere because he's always routing through whatever's available — Meshtastic where it's live, cellular backhaul through compromised nodes, relays he's built and doesn't advertise. The fiction is mesh. The reality is that Ferry has been building redundant comms infrastructure since before the Cascade and he doesn't go dark.

**Faction discovery is broadcast-first.** Players don't find the Reckoners by finding Ferry. They find them through Morgan or Derek's signal reaching them first — a broadcast, a fragment, something that lands before they know what it is. The faction exists as a voice before it exists as people. Ferry finds the player after. That's the sequence.
