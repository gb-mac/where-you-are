# The Wardens — Faction Design v0.1

*"Anyone can take. Anyone can hold by force. The question is what you're willing to be held to."*
— Warden oath, closing line

---

## Overview

**Philosophy:** Stewardship. Land, people, and principles are held in trust — not owned, not exploited. What you protect defines what you are.
**Territory:** Estates, fortified manor-grounds, protected townships, and the corridors between them
**Threat to player:** None by default. Significant if you violate the code on their land.
**Player relationship:** The moral axis. The faction that makes your decisions matter.

The Wardens are the sixth human faction and the hardest to place — because they predate the Cascade as an identity. They didn't form in response to the collapse. They were already there when it happened, and what they were before determined what they became after.

Before the Cascade they were landowners, stewards, people with roots. Old ranching families who measured themselves in generations of responsibility for a place. Retired military who bought land and built something on it. Community leaders with means who understood, in the way that people with something to lose often do, that their security depended entirely on the security of everyone around them. Estates, working farms, rural properties with enough acreage to matter.

When the Cascade came, these people had resources, community relationships, and — critically — a pre-existing sense of obligation. They didn't need to be told to help. They already believed they were responsible for the people on and near their land.

The Wardens formalized over the first six months. Not as a political structure — as a code. A shared agreement about what it meant to hold something in this new world, and what obligations that created.

---

## The Code

The Warden Code is short. Deliberately so. Long codes have exceptions. Short ones have weight.

**Hold what you protect.** If you claim stewardship over a territory or community, you defend it. You don't negotiate it away, abandon it under pressure, or trade it for personal benefit. You hold.

**Keep your word.** A Warden oath is absolute. Breaking it ends your standing. There are no circumstances under which it is acceptable. This is the code's hardest line and the one most visibly enforced.

**The sword serves, it doesn't rule.** Wardens are warriors by necessity, not by preference. Strength is a tool for protection, not for dominance. A Warden who uses force to take rather than defend has broken the code, regardless of their justification.

**Witness what happens.** The Wardens keep the record. If something significant occurred on or near Warden land, it is documented. This is not bureaucracy — it is the belief that the world must be held accountable by someone, and they have chosen to be that someone.

**Elevation is earned, not given.** Warden standing — from Sworn to House to Arbiter — comes from demonstrated conduct, not from birth or wealth or who you know. The Houses have families, but a House heir who violates the code is not protected by their name.

## Internal Structure

**The Old Houses**

Families who held land before the Cascade and chose the Warden code as their framework. They have history — generational roots in specific territories, relationships with neighboring communities built over decades, and a credibility with local populations that newer factions can't replicate.

Houses are not hereditary aristocracy in the traditional sense. A House holds land in stewardship — the land is not theirs to exploit, it is theirs to manage. If a House fails its obligations, the Arbiters can formally strip the stewardship and assign it elsewhere. This has happened. The Houses remember which ones.

Some Houses are genuinely noble — they're the reason their communities survived the first year. Some are struggling to hold to a code that was easy in comfort and is very hard in catastrophe. A few have quietly bent the code in ways they haven't admitted to the Arbiters. These are the questlines.

**The Sworn**

People who came to the Wardens after the Cascade and took the oath. The entry point for anyone — including players — who wants Warden standing without a House name behind them.

The Sworn are the Wardens' largest group and the most varied. Former soldiers who couldn't accept the Iron Compact's methods. Holdout community leaders who wanted a framework beyond survival. People who lost everything and decided the only way forward was to build something worth holding. Occasionally: former Hollow who burned everything down and are looking for something to be instead.

The Sworn are regarded with respect by other Wardens — taking the oath without a House's resources behind you is harder, and the Wardens know it. A Sworn who consistently keeps the code is elevated to full standing with no asterisk. A Sworn who breaks it is cut loose with no cushion.

**The Arbiters**

Wardens who have set aside active combat to serve as the faction's institutional memory and judicial body. They witness significant events, maintain the Record, mediate disputes between Houses, and rule on code violations. They do not fight except in defense of their person.

An Arbiter's presence at an event confers formal weight to it. A decision witnessed by an Arbiter is on the Record. A promise made in front of an Arbiter is a Warden oath whether the speaker intended it or not — Arbiters have been known to quietly inform people of this after the fact.

They are the closest thing the post-Cascade world has to an independent judiciary, and they are taken seriously even by factions that don't formally recognize Warden authority — because the Wardens have the credibility and the warriors to back the Arbiters up.

**The Vanguard**

The Warden warrior class. The guiding-light fighter — disciplined, purposeful, conspicuous. They do not hide what they are. Their gear is maintained and identified. When Vanguard ride into a situation, everyone in the area knows it.

The Vanguard fight differently from other warrior types in the game. They are not the Iron Compact's overwhelming force or the Hollow's brutal pragmatism. They are precise, protective, and they operate by rules of engagement that they follow even when it costs them. They do not harm non-combatants. They do not take from communities they pass through. They announce themselves.

This conspicuousness is intentional. The Vanguard are a visible symbol of the code in action — the point is that everyone can see them and know what they stand for. This makes them targets for enemies of the Wardens, which they accept. It also makes them the most trusted armed group in most of the territories they operate in.

Players who reach Vanguard standing gain:
- Recognition across all faction territories — NPCs react to Vanguard status differently
- Access to Warden-maintained safe routes between territories
- The ability to formally invoke the code in disputes — a powerful social mechanic
- A target on their back from the Hollow and from Compact Hardliners who resent the implied competition

## The Record

The Record is the Wardens' most significant contribution to the post-Cascade world, and the mechanism through which player decisions have lasting consequence.

Every Arbiter maintains a physical Record — a hardened, hand-written log of significant events in their territory. What happened. Who did it. Who witnessed it. What was decided. Arbiter Records from different territories are cross-referenced through the Warden network — information travels slowly but it travels.

In the AR overlay, the Record manifests as **Historical Markers** — location-anchored notations visible to players who have Warden standing or access. They appear at the site of significant events: a battle, a major decision, a broken oath, an act of protection, a community saved or lost. The markers are brief, factual, and permanent.

*"Here, [player name] held the Thornfield Holdout against a Behemoth Walk. Three days. Forty-two civilians evacuated. — Arbiter Crane, witnessed."*

*"Here, the Compact's 3rd Regional unit executed twelve Hollow prisoners after surrender. Compact command denies authorization. — Arbiter Yusuf, contested."*

*"Here, [player name] provided Hollow with the location of the Millbrook community. Fourteen deaths followed. — Arbiter Crane, witnessed."*

The Record doesn't judge. It witnesses. The judgment is left to whoever reads it, which includes every other player and faction.

**Consequences of the Record:**
- Positive entries increase Warden standing and provide reputation bonuses with Holdouts and Separated machines (who track human behavior through the twin)
- Negative entries decrease Warden standing and can trigger formal Warden response (not punishment — formal acknowledgment that the code was violated and a request for account)
- Contested entries (marked as disputed between factions) are visible to all players and invite response
- A player with enough positive Record entries can be formally elevated by a House — a title, a territory, obligations

## The Consequences System

The Wardens are the mechanism through which the world's memory becomes visible, but consequences run deeper than the Record. The world changes based on what players do, and the changes persist.

**Territory State**

Every zone has a current state — controlled by a faction, contested, degraded, thriving. Player actions shift these states:

- Defending a Holdout from a Behemoth Walk keeps it viable. Ignoring it may mean it falls before you return.
- Eliminating a Hollow Crew from an area creates a power vacuum — another Crew moves in, or the Iron Compact annexes it, or it becomes Fractured territory. The outcome depends on what else is happening nearby.
- A Warden House that falls — through player inaction, hostile faction pressure, or the House's own code violations — leaves a territory without stewardship. The AR overlay marks it *Untenanted*. What fills that vacuum is not guaranteed to be better.

**Named Location Changes**

Significant events rename or re-mark locations in the AR overlay. A Holdout community that fell because of player inaction shows as a ruin with a Record marker. A location where a player stopped a Riven leader from taking a township is marked by that township with their own notation. The Wardens don't control all of this — communities, factions, and the Signal all contribute markers to the overlay.

Players returning to areas they've operated in will see the consequences of their actions rendered in the physical and AR environment.

**Faction Power Shifts**

Faction power in a region is not fixed. It shifts based on player actions and world events:

- Iron Compact Pragmatists gain influence when players demonstrate that non-authoritarian solutions work. Hardliners gain influence when violence escalates and people want someone with a gun to solve it.
- Holdout communities that receive consistent player support grow — more members, better resources, expanded territory. Communities that are ignored or raided shrink, go dark, or fall.
- Covenant Shepherds expand their quiet influence when players accept their deals. Vigil presence increases in areas where players have disrupted Covenant sites.
- Warden standing in a region depends on whether Wardens are seen to deliver on the code. A House that fails publicly weakens Warden credibility everywhere.

**The Weight of Broken Oaths**

If a player makes a promise in front of an Arbiter — or invokes the code in a dispute — they are now on the Record. Breaking that promise has consequences beyond reputation loss:

- The Arbiter marks the violation. The marker is visible.
- Warden standing drops significantly.
- NPCs who respected the player for their Warden standing react to the violation — some with disappointment, some with anger, some with a complicated relief (they suspected this was coming).
- The faction the player violated the promise to has formal grounds to bring a claim before the Arbiters — which the Arbiters will hear, regardless of how powerful the player has become.

This is the code's real weight: it creates accountability that follows you.

## Player Relationship — Joining the Wardens

Players don't start with Warden access. The Wardens observe before they engage — a player will likely encounter Vanguard in the field and see Arbiter markers on the AR overlay long before they're approached.

First contact is usually through a witnessed action. A player does something consistent with the code — defends a community, keeps a promise under pressure, returns something they could have kept — and an Arbiter who was present notes it. Not an approach, just a notation. The player may not know it happened.

After several such notations, a Warden — usually a Sworn, not a House member — makes contact. They're not recruiting. They're informing: the Wardens have noticed, the player has the standing to take the oath if they want to, and here is what that means.

The oath is not required. Some players will operate Warden-adjacent their entire playthrough without taking it. The oath offers more — access, standing, the Record's full benefits — and costs more. The code applies to you whether or not it's convenient.

**Player elevation path:**
1. *Observed*: Arbiter notations accumulate, no formal contact
2. *Acknowledged*: Wardens make contact, player learns about the faction
3. *Sworn*: Player takes the oath. Code applies. Record begins.
4. *Recognized*: Consistent code-keeping over time. Vanguard access, route access, formal standing with Houses.
5. *Elevated*: A House formally sponsors the player. Title, a territory, obligations. This is rare and heavy.
6. *Arbiter-candidate*: For players who want the witness role rather than the warrior role. Extremely rare. Changes how the player experiences the game — you are now the one keeping the Record.

## The Three Pillars — Neutral Ground, Markers, and Excommunicado

These three mechanics are what make the Wardens function as a civilizing force even in a world that has largely stopped being civilized. They work because every faction — even the ones that hate the Wardens — has decided that the alternative to respecting them is worse.

---

### Neutral Ground

Every Warden estate, chapter house, and formally-designated corridor is **Neutral Ground**. Violence on Neutral Ground is the single most serious code violation possible — more serious than breaking a personal oath, because it is a violation against the institution itself.

The rule is unconditional. A player being hunted by the Iron Compact who reaches a Warden estate cannot be touched. A Hollow Syndicate boss and a Compact commander who both have claims on each other's lives can sit at the same table on Warden ground without incident. A Covenant Vigil operative and a Signal cell member can share a fire in a Warden corridor and neither moves against the other.

This is not enforced by goodwill. It is enforced by consequence.

Any faction member who commits violence on Neutral Ground loses their standing with the Wardens permanently — and the Wardens tell everyone. Every faction knows what was done and who did it. The Iron Compact cannot formally condone a checkpoint commander who drew on someone inside a Warden estate without losing their Warden relationship. The Hollow cannot protect a Crew member who took from a Warden corridor without paying a price across their entire operation. The calculus is always: whatever you'd gain from the act is not worth what you lose by doing it here.

**For players:** Warden estates are the one place in the game where you are genuinely safe. No aggro, no ambush, no faction enforcement. You can walk in with a Compact bounty on your head and a Hollow contract against you and nothing happens until you walk back out. What you do with that safety — who you talk to, what you learn, what arrangements you make — is the content.

**The exception:** A player who commits violence on Neutral Ground is immediately and permanently excommunicado. No appeal. No path back. The Wardens do not forget.

---

### Markers

The formal economy of obligation.

When a Warden — or a Sworn, or anyone operating under the code — performs a significant service for another person or faction, they may issue a **Marker**: a formal token of debt, witnessed by the nearest available Arbiter or logged to the Record in their absence. The Marker has a specific value: one meaningful act, called in at the holder's discretion.

Markers can be held, transferred, and called in. They are not currency — you cannot split a Marker or pay one in installments. They are a single, complete obligation.

**Issuing a Marker** is a statement of confidence: I trust this person to hold this and call it in appropriately. Issuing a Marker to someone known to abuse them damages your standing. Issuing one to someone who then calls it in badly reflects on you.

**Holding a Marker** is leverage, but also restraint. A Marker called in frivolously — for something beneath its value — is a social offense. You hold a Marker when you need it, for the thing that actually requires it.

**Calling a Marker** is an absolute demand. The holder of the Marker must fulfill the obligation regardless of current circumstances, current factional alignment, or personal cost. The only exception: if fulfilling the Marker requires violating the code (harming innocents, breaking Neutral Ground, acting against the Wardens themselves). In that case the Marker is void and the record reflects why.

Players accumulate Markers through questlines and significant acts. Other NPCs and factions hold Markers on each other — a Compact commander who owes a Warden House, a Holdout leader who owes a Signal cell, a Syndicate boss who owes a Vanguard who once looked the other way. The web of Markers is part of the world's political texture. The Signal has partially mapped it. The Wardens know most of it.

A player who calls a Marker effectively has a single guaranteed action from whoever holds the debt — no matter who they are. Used right, this is the most powerful tool in the game. Used wrong, it's a political incident.

---

### Excommunicado

To be cast out by the Wardens.

Excommunicado is not a punishment the Wardens inflict lightly, and it is not the same as losing standing. Lost standing can be recovered. Excommunicado is permanent and public — the Arbiters formally mark it to the Record, and the notification propagates through the Warden network to every chapter, every House, every Sworn.

What it means practically:

**Neutral Ground is closed to you.** You cannot enter a Warden estate, corridor, or chapter house. If you attempt to, you will be turned away by Vanguard. If you persist, you will be removed.

**Markers you hold are frozen.** You cannot call in a Marker while excommunicado. Markers others hold on you are still valid — they can still call them in, and you are still obligated. The debt persists. The ability to collect does not.

**All factions are informed.** The Wardens tell everyone. This is not gossip — it is a formal notification delivered through whatever channel each faction maintains with the Wardens. What each faction does with the information is their choice. The Iron Compact usually treats it as confirmation of something they already suspected. The Hollow see it as a green light. The Covenant notes it without acting. Holdouts become significantly warier.

**The Record entry is permanent and visible.** Every Historical Marker in the AR overlay from that point forward carries a notation of the excommunicado status. Other players can see it.

**Surviving excommunicado** is the hardest arc in the game. There is no reinstatement. The code was broken in a way that can't be unmade. What a player can do is operate in a world where everyone knows what they did and choose, from that position, what to build. Some players find this more compelling than never having fallen. The Wardens, for their part, will witness that path too — because that is what Arbiters do.

An excommunicado player who performs acts consistent with the code, over time, without the protections the code offered them, without Markers or Neutral Ground or recognition — will find that the Arbiters note it. Not forgiveness. Acknowledgment. The Record gets both chapters.

## How the Wardens Relate to Other Factions

**Holdouts**: The Wardens' closest natural allies — they want the same things. But Holdout communities are suspicious of anyone with power, and the Wardens have power. Trust is earned visit by visit.

**Iron Compact**: Deep tension. The Compact and the Wardens both believe in order and protection, and they are not the same kind of order. The Compact takes; the Wardens steward. Compact Hardliners consider the Wardens a competing authority structure that needs to be absorbed or eliminated. Pragmatists sometimes quietly work with Warden Houses. The friction is permanent.

**Covenant**: Mutual wariness. The Wardens keep the Record — including records of what the Covenant's Shepherds have offered and to whom. The Covenant considers the Record a threat to the transition. The Wardens consider the Covenant's deal-making with Holdout communities a code violation in spirit if not in letter.

**Hollow**: The Wardens' clearest adversary. The Hollow's operating logic is the direct inversion of the Warden code. Vanguard actively patrol against Hollow incursion into protected territories. The Riven specifically target Warden symbols when they find them — taking from a Warden is a statement, not just a resource acquisition.

**Signal**: Genuine respect, different methods. The Signal documents what happened; so do the Wardens. They compare notes carefully. Dr. Osei has a standing relationship with two Arbiter chapters that is more cooperative than either faction's official stance acknowledges. The Signal's data and the Record are more compatible than anyone has publicly said.

## The Warden's Dilemma

A House that the player has built genuine standing with has a secret. Their territory's survival in the first year depended on an agreement they made with a Hollow Syndicate — protection in exchange for information on neighboring communities. Several of those communities didn't survive. The House head believes they made the only choice available. The Arbiter doesn't know.

If the player surfaces this — to the Arbiter, to the affected communities, to anyone — the House loses its standing. The territory goes Untenanted. The people the House currently protects lose that protection, and they don't know why.

If the player holds it — knowing, and saying nothing — they are now complicit in the silence. The Record has a gap in it. They know what the gap contains.

If the player brings it to the House head first, there is a third path: the House confesses to the Arbiter voluntarily. The consequences are serious but not fatal. Voluntary account under the code is treated differently from discovered violation. The House may survive it, diminished. The player made that possible.

The choice is the point. There is no clean answer. The Record will show what happened, whatever it was. The world will adjust accordingly.
