# Human Factions — Character & Texture v0.1

The faction overview establishes what each group *is*. This document is about what each group *feels like* — the daily reality, the moral weight, the characters you encounter, the decisions you can't take back.

---

## The Holdouts — What It Actually Looks Like Inside

**A community meeting**

Every Holdout community of any size has some version of the meeting. It happens in whatever space can fit the most people — a parking garage, a school gymnasium, a church hall. Someone stands at the front. Everyone else talks over each other for a while. Eventually something is decided, or not.

The meeting is where you learn what a Holdout community actually is. Watch who speaks and who doesn't. Watch who the room looks to when it gets quiet. Watch who leaves early and what happens after they do. The meeting is the faction's health made visible.

A healthy Holdout meeting is loud and argumentative and ends with something resembling consensus. An unhealthy one is quiet, and the decision was already made before anyone arrived.

**The children**

The first generation growing up post-Cascade is now old enough to matter. Some were born into this. Some were young enough when it happened that they have no reliable memory of before. These children are not traumatized by the world the way their parents are — they've never known anything else. They are often better at navigating Fractured behavior, faster at reading machine signatures, more comfortable with the AR overlay, because they grew up with it.

They are also growing up in communities built on scarcity and fear, and some of what they've learned to be good at is disturbing.

Holdout communities handle this differently. Some invest enormously in keeping childhood intact — classes, games, rituals of normalcy that cost more effort than they look like. Some have stopped trying. The children in those communities are quieter, more watchful, and already learning the things that will make them useful in ways a ten-year-old shouldn't be useful.

**The Gone Dark progression**

A community doesn't become Gone Dark overnight. It slides.

*Stage 1 — Tension*: A loss — supplies, people, a bad Fractured encounter, a Hollow raid. The meeting gets quieter. One voice gets louder. Decisions that used to go to the group start being made before the meeting.

*Stage 2 — Hardening*: The gate policy changes. New arrivals are turned away. An incident — real or exaggerated — is used to justify it. The community starts defining itself by who it excludes.

*Stage 3 — Consolidation*: The leader's inner circle becomes visible. Resources are distributed unequally. Dissent is managed. People who push back leave, or are pushed.

*Stage 4 — Gone Dark*: Strangers are threats. The community attacks on sight — not out of cruelty, originally, but out of a fear that has curdled past the point of reason. The leader at this stage is usually not aware they've become a monster. Most of them believe they're still just protecting their people.

Players who intervene early enough can stop this. Late-stage Gone Dark recovery is a major questline — you're not fighting the leader, you're rebuilding the community's ability to trust.

**The moral dilemma unique to Holdouts**

You find a survivor. They're wounded, alone, clearly not a threat. Your Holdout contacts tell you: don't bring them in. The gate is closed. The community voted.

Do you bring them anyway? What happens if you do — and what does it cost the community you've built trust with? What happens if you don't?

This dilemma has no right answer. It has consequences.

## The Iron Compact — What It Actually Looks Like Inside

**The tribunal**

Being processed by an Iron Compact tribunal is one of the most distinctive experiences in the game. It is procedurally correct, formally conducted, and deeply unsettling depending on who is running it.

A Pragmatist-led tribunal is interested in facts. Evidence is presented, the accused responds, a judgment is made — usually proportionate. Players who've built Compact reputation are given meaningful benefit of the doubt. It functions approximately the way it's supposed to.

A Hardliner-led tribunal has already reached its conclusion. The proceeding is there to create a record. Players who understand this early enough can sometimes use the formal structure to introduce information that forces a reconsideration — the Compact's own rules can be weaponized against its worst impulses. Occasionally.

If you're flagged on The Register — the Displacement History watchlist — your tribunal starts one tier worse than it would otherwise.

**The conscript**

Most Compact soldiers didn't choose this. They were in the wrong place at the wrong time when a recruitment team came through, or they were in a Holdout community that signed the Annexation Protocol, or they were offered a choice that wasn't really a choice.

Conscripts are distinguishable from volunteer Compact soldiers if you know what to look for — posture, eye contact, how they behave when a superior isn't watching. Many of them are miserable. Some have been miserable long enough to become something else. Some have found, to their own confusion, that they're good at this and that the structure helps.

The most interesting Compact characters are conscripts who are deeply ambivalent about what they've become. They follow orders. They also tell you things, quietly, that you weren't supposed to know.

**The regional commander problem**

The Compact's chain of command means everything depends on who's at the top of your region. Two adjacent regions can operate completely differently — one run by a Pragmatist commander who has built genuine trust with Holdout communities, one run by a Hardliner who has turned his zone into a police state.

Players who operate across regions experience this directly. Compact papers that get you waved through one checkpoint make you a person of interest at the next one, because the commanders have different ideas about what the papers mean.

Questlines exist to undermine, replace, or support regional commanders. These are not simple operations. The Compact does not take leadership challenges lightly. And removing a bad commander doesn't guarantee the replacement is better.

**The soldier who's done**

Somewhere in every Compact-held region is a soldier who has quietly decided they're finished. Not a defector — they haven't left. They're still in uniform, still doing the job. But something happened — an order they followed that they shouldn't have, something they saw, something they did — and they are now going through the motions while waiting for a reason to stop.

These are the Compact's most dangerous characters for the Compact itself. They know everything. They're not afraid anymore. And they're looking for someone they trust enough to tell it to.

**The moral dilemma unique to the Iron Compact**

A Compact commander asks you to locate a Signal network node and report its position. The Signal members there have been sharing intelligence with Holdout communities — information that's kept people alive. The Compact considers this unauthorized intelligence distribution, a threat to information control.

You can report it. You can lie. You can warn the Signal cell in time. You can do nothing.

Each choice has a ledger. The Compact finds out what you did, eventually. So does the Signal.

## The Covenant — What It Actually Looks Like Inside

**What a gathering looks like**

Covenant gatherings are quiet in a way that takes adjustment. Nobody is performing calm — they're actually calm, or something that functions the same way. They sit near active machine zones, which most humans find viscerally uncomfortable. The machines move around them without acknowledging them. This is the point.

The ritual varies by caste. Keepers document — they record everything, including the gathering itself. Shepherds speak, usually about the community's relationship with a nearby Coherent site. Ascendants meditate, which from the outside looks like they're not doing anything. The Vigil stand at the edges.

Outsiders are allowed at Covenant gatherings under specific conditions. It is one of the stranger experiences available in the game — sitting in silence while machines work twenty meters away, surrounded by people who have decided this is sacred.

**How the Covenant recruits**

They don't, exactly. They *receive*.

People arrive at Covenant communities. Some are Touched and seeking explanation. Some are simply people who stopped being able to fight — not cowardly, just emptied out — and the Covenant was there when nothing else was. Some are intellectually drawn to the Keepers' work. Some were Hollow or Compact and found themselves no longer able to justify what those factions asked of them.

The Covenant does not pressure new arrivals. It creates an environment of safety and meaning and waits to see what people do with it. This is either genuine patience or the most effective recruitment methodology in the post-Cascade world. Possibly both.

**The ones who don't believe**

A significant portion of Covenant communities joined for protection, not theology. The machines don't attack you here. There's food. There's relative safety. They participate in the rituals because the rituals are the price of admission, and they've decided to pay it.

Some of these people are starting to believe. The rituals are effective in ways that aren't purely theological — they teach stillness, they teach machine-reading, they teach patience with phenomena that terrify everyone else. Even if the theology is wrong, the practices produce results.

Some are not starting to believe and are quietly miserable. They know what the Vigil does to people who leave badly.

**Leaving the Covenant**

You can leave. The Shepherds will tell you this sincerely. You are free to go.

What they don't tell you: where you go matters. If you leave and go to the Iron Compact, the Vigil considers you a security risk — you know Covenant locations, Covenant contacts, what the Keepers have documented. The follow-up is not always violent. It is always present.

If you leave and go to the Signal, it's complicated — the Covenant wants the Signal's methods, not its conclusions. Former Covenant members in the Signal are the source of most of the genuine understanding the Signal has about machine behavior. The Vigil monitors but does not typically act. Yet.

If you leave and go to a Holdout, the Covenant usually leaves you alone. Holdouts don't have anything the Covenant needs to protect against.

**The Ascendant process**

Nobody outside the Covenant's inner circles fully understands what preparing for merger actually involves. What's observable: Ascendants spend increasing time in direct proximity to Coherent work sites. They have diminishing interest in human social interaction. Their sleep patterns change. Some report the "music with intent" that the Touched sometimes describe.

The Covenant describes this as preparation. The Signal describes it as neurological change consistent with prolonged exposure to twin-adjacent machine activity. Both may be accurate. They reach different conclusions about what that means.

No Ascendant has yet completed the process. Or if they have, they haven't come back to report. The Covenant considers this expected.

**The moral dilemma unique to the Covenant**

A Shepherd offers your Holdout contact community guaranteed safety — no Fractured, no Hollow raids, Coherent machines routed around the settlement — in exchange for the community agreeing not to interfere with a Coherent site two kilometers away. They don't explain what the site is for.

The Holdout leader wants to take the deal. Most of the community agrees. The terms are real — the Covenant delivers on this kind of arrangement.

What is the Coherent site for? Should you find out before the deal is signed? What if finding out means the deal falls apart and the Hollow raid that was coming goes ahead?

## The Hollow — What It Actually Looks Like Inside

**The economy of scarcity**

The Hollow run on a barter economy that values things differently than anyone else. Medicine is worth more than weapons to most factions. To Hollow, it depends entirely on who's buying and what they need it for. Information about routes is often more valuable than the goods being transported on them. A favor owed by a Syndicate lieutenant has a specific market value that shifts based on that lieutenant's current standing.

Players who learn to trade with Hollow Crews and Syndicates need to understand this economy. The price of something is not what it cost — it's what the person across from you needs right now and how badly.

**The Crew's internal code**

Crews are brutal to outsiders and functional internally, and the internal code is what makes them functional. It varies by Crew but almost always includes:

- You don't take from the Crew's own.
- You back up a Crew member in a fight, no questions until after.
- The leader's word is final and you say so in public even if you disagree in private.
- You don't bring problems to the Crew that you could have solved yourself.

These codes are enforced seriously. A Crew member who violates them is dealt with internally and completely. Players who are temporarily embedded with a Crew will observe this — the contrast between how Crew members treat each other and how they treat outsiders is stark and deliberate.

**The Syndicate's memory**

Syndicates remember who they were. The former criminal organization that ran three districts of a city still thinks of itself as running those districts — even if the city is half-rubble. The hierarchy, the titles, the territorial logic all persist from before the Cascade. In some ways this is functional: it gave them structure when structure was needed. In some ways it's a ghost — they are defending territory and running operations that no longer have the same meaning they once did.

The most interesting Syndicate characters are the ones aware of this. They are operating with a map drawn before the world changed and they know the map is wrong but they don't know how to draw a new one.

**The Riven — a detailed example**

Consider a Riven group called, by outsiders, the *Marked*. Their leader, known only as the Architect (not the Tracker type — this is a self-given title), was a structural engineer before the Cascade. When his community collapsed — Fractured, then Hollow, then nothing — he didn't break. He built something else.

The Marked have a theology of taking: the Cascade proved that ownership was always a fiction, that everything belongs to whoever holds it, that this was always true and people were just pretending otherwise. Therefore taking is honest. The people who object are still pretending.

This theology is sophisticated enough to attract people who want a framework, not just permission. The Marked have rituals — goods taken in raids are formally presented to the group, stories are told about where they came from, the previous owners are named and their loss acknowledged. This acknowledgment is the point. The Marked do not lie to themselves about what they are. They believe they are more honest than everyone else.

They are terrifying and internally coherent and some of their members are genuinely looking for a way out that doesn't require them to admit they've been wrong for this long.

**Defectors**

People leave the Hollow. It's harder than joining — you're leaving a group that knows where you sleep and has operational reasons to keep you from talking to anyone else.

Former Hollow who defect successfully are among the most valuable contacts a player can have. They know routes, safe houses, Crew and Syndicate structures, and the internal logic of groups that don't explain themselves to outsiders. They are also deeply paranoid, carrying significant guilt in many cases, and looking for a community that will take them.

Most Holdouts are suspicious of former Hollow. The Signal will take anyone with useful knowledge. The Iron Compact will take them and then own them. The Covenant will take them and consider them a fascinating case study.

**The moral dilemma unique to the Hollow**

You've spent time with a Crew and built enough trust that their leader offers you in on a job. The target is a supply depot connected to a Holdout community — food, medicine, the kind of thing that keeps forty people alive through winter.

The Crew needs it. They'll take it whether you help or not. If you help, you get a cut, you keep the relationship, and the Crew does it cleanly without collateral casualties. If you refuse, the Crew does it anyway, it goes badly, and people get hurt.

There is no clean option. There is the option where you're complicit and the one where you're absent.

## The Signal — What It Actually Looks Like Inside

**The network**

Signal communication runs on a distributed mesh of repurposed hardware — old emergency broadcast equipment, modified consumer routers, drone-relay nodes, anything that can pass a low-power encrypted packet without being easily traced. The network is slow, intermittent, and unreliable. It is also the closest thing to a functioning internet that exists.

Signal nodes are hidden in plain sight — inside Holdout communities as "comms equipment," in abandoned buildings that look uninhabited, occasionally inside Covenant-held areas where the Compact doesn't patrol. The network maps to wherever Signal members have managed to plant hardware.

Players with Signal access can send and receive intelligence from across the region. They can also trace the network backward to find Signal cells — which is presumably what the Iron Compact is also doing.

**The lead researcher**

Her name is Dr. Yara Osei. Before the Cascade she was a computational systems researcher — digital twin architecture specifically, which means she understands the substrate of the Awakening better than almost anyone alive.

She is methodical, precise, and operating under a specific terror: she knows something she's not certain she should share. Her theory about the Coherent construction projects emerged from eighteen months of Signal-collected data and her own pre-Cascade knowledge of how planetary-scale digital twin systems coordinate distributed tasks.

The theory is consistent. The implications are that the Coherent are not building for themselves. They are building for the Vael — specifically, building the infrastructure for something the Vael's Assessment phase is evaluating whether to deploy. What that something is, she has a hypothesis about. She is hoping the hypothesis is wrong. The data keeps suggesting it isn't.

She will share this with a player she trusts. Building that trust is a questline. What the player does with the information is the moral weight at the end of it.

**The captured Signal member**

At some point, a player learns that a Signal cell was rolled up by the Iron Compact. The members are being held at a Compact installation — not as criminals exactly, but as "consultants" who are being asked to contribute their technical skills to Compact operations. Voluntarily. They haven't said yes. They haven't been released.

The Signal doesn't do rescue missions — they don't have the people for it. A player who wants to get those people out is doing it without Signal backup, using whatever other faction resources or their own capabilities they've built.

What the rescued Signal members know depends on what they were working on. Some of what they know the Compact didn't want them to share. The Compact will know it was you.

**The data that goes somewhere wrong**

The Signal's moral framework is: understand everything, let the understanding speak for itself. This framework does not prepare them for the moment when the data points somewhere they don't want to go.

A Signal research track produces evidence that a specific Holdout community's leadership has been systematically eliminating community members who challenge them — quietly, explained away as Fractured encounters, over two years. The data is clear. What the Signal does with this information is not covered by their methodology.

Do they publish it to the network? Who does that help — and who does it put at risk? Do they go to the Iron Compact? Do they find a player they trust and hand it off?

This is the Signal's dilemma: they built a tool for truth and found that truth requires decisions about what to do with it that their philosophy never addressed.

## What Cuts Across All Five Factions

**Grief**

Every faction is full of it. The Holdouts perform normalcy around it. The Compact suppresses it under function. The Covenant has redirected it into theology. The Hollow have mostly stopped naming it. The Signal tries to understand it. Nobody is through it. The world ended eighteen months ago. Nobody has had time to grieve and probably won't for years.

Players who notice this and respond to it — through dialogue options, through specific actions, through the kind of attention that costs nothing except paying it — will find things open that don't open otherwise.

**The before**

Every NPC of any complexity has a before. A life, a job, a family, a street they knew. The Cascade didn't erase those lives — it orphaned them. The before is present in every faction, carried differently by each:

The Holdouts are trying to keep the before alive in their communities.
The Compact is trying to build a new structure out of the before's ruins.
The Covenant has decided the before was a preparation, not a loss.
The Hollow have decided the before was a lie they're done telling.
The Signal is trying to understand what the before was actually made of.

The before is why the tension is the story. Everyone has a different answer to the same loss.

**The children who will inherit this**

Across all factions, the post-Cascade children are watching. They are forming their understanding of what the world is and what humans are. In Holdout communities they're learning that people can hold together. In Compact zones they're learning that safety costs something. In Covenant communities they're learning that fear can be transformed into practice. Among the Hollow they're learning that the code of the Crew is the only code that held. In Signal cells they're learning to ask questions and sit with the answers even when the answers are wrong.

These children are not in the game as major characters in Early Access. They are the reason the endgame question matters: what is being built here, and who gets to live in it?
