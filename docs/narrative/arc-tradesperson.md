# The Tradesperson Arc — Craft, Lineage, and the Back Door

*"I made it myself. It took me two weeks. I got it wrong four times before I got it right."*
*"That's correct technique."*
— Tradesperson player and a Warden Armorer, first meeting

---

## Overview

Every background has a natural path into the Warden faction. The Veteran walks in through the code's alignment with military discipline. The Homesteader arrives through the landowner tradition — the Houses recognize their own. The First Responder earns it through community standing. The Researcher gets there late but deep, through the Signal's relationship with the Arbiters.

The Tradesperson goes in through the back door.

Not the oath. Not the Record. Not the Moot. Through the workshop — and specifically, through the fact that a Maker can recognize good work when they see it, and the Accord obliges them to say so.

This is the only background that can achieve a standing relationship inside a House before formally taking the oath. It is also the only background that creates a specific moral question the Accord eventually has to answer: the Maker has acknowledged the work, the House has acknowledged the Maker's acknowledgment — so why haven't you sworn?

This document covers the Tradesperson's specific arc through the Warden faction. For background starting conditions and general play style, see `character-creation.md`. For the full Maker system, see `warden-makers.md`.

---

## The Craft Eye

The Tradesperson has something no other background has at Day 1: the ability to read how things are made.

Where other backgrounds see a good piece of gear, the Tradesperson sees *how* it was built. The join at the shoulder. The reinforcement at the stress point. The specific choice of hardware at the load-bearing clip. Whether the person who made it understood what the gear would face, or whether they were working from a pattern without understanding it.

This is passive and always on. It surfaces in specific dialogue options, investigation paths, and environmental observations unavailable to other backgrounds:

- Examining recovered Warden gear, the Tradesperson reads the maker's signature without being told what to look for
- At a Holdout community, they can assess the construction of fortifications and make a judgment about who built them and when
- Fractured accumulation tiers are readable at a glance — they see the assembly, not just the result
- Broken or field-repaired gear tells them a story about the conditions it was repaired in and who might have done it

This is not a quest marker. There is no UI indicator. The information surfaces in dialogue and in the way the Tradesperson sees the world. Other players learn these things over time through play. The Tradesperson knows them immediately because they already know how to look.

## The Starting Blueprints

The Tradesperson begins with a small set of blueprints — fabrication instructions for field gear, improvised equipment, basic weapons components. These were accumulated before the Cascade: trade manuals, proprietary repair docs, specifications from manufacturers they've worked with, techniques passed down from whoever trained them.

What the Tradesperson does not know at the start: some of those blueprints use construction methods adjacent to Warden technique. Not identical. Adjacent. The specific load distribution approach on one harness design. The join method on a specific plate carrier configuration. The edge geometry on a maintenance blade.

These aren't Warden blueprints. They weren't made by a House Maker. But they share something with Warden work — a principle, a technique, a way of thinking about what the carrier will face and building accordingly.

When a Maker sees the Tradesperson's work for the first time, they notice this. They don't mention it immediately. But they file it.

Where those blueprints came from is a thread. Who taught whoever taught the Tradesperson's source? That question leads somewhere inside the Houses — a lineage that predates the Cascade and connects a pre-Cascade craft tradition to what the Wardens formalized as ideology. The Tradesperson didn't know they were part of this lineage. The Bladesmith — who has the longest memory of the four Makers — is most likely to recognize it and least likely to explain it immediately.

## The Maker Finds Them

The Tradesperson does not seek out a Maker. The Maker comes to them.

Word travels in the post-Cascade world at the speed of people — which is slower than the old world but more reliable, because information that travels by mouth has been filtered by whoever chose to carry it. When someone in the area is doing good work — field repairs that hold, fabricated gear that functions, improvised solutions that solve problems cleanly — the word reaches the right ears.

The first contact is not a recruitment pitch. It is a problem.

A Maker — likely the Armorer or the Tailor, depending on what the player's work has been visible in — shows up at the player's home base with something broken. Something they could fix themselves but haven't, not because they can't but because they want to see how someone else approaches it.

There is no quest marker for this. No faction notification. A knock at the door (or the equivalent — someone waiting at the perimeter, someone who asked around until they found the player's base). They introduce themselves. They have a piece of gear that needs attention. Can you look at it?

The evaluation is entirely built into the task. The Maker watches how the player approaches it — whether they diagnose before they start, whether they understand what failed and why, whether the repair is better than the original or just adequate. They say very little during the process. They are not warm and they are not cold. They are paying attention.

When it's done:

*"Where did you learn this?"*

The answer doesn't matter as much as the question. The Maker is not asking for provenance. They are acknowledging that the work implied training from somewhere. That question is the relationship beginning.

## The Fix-Him Crossover

The fix-him questline runs separately and earlier — the Tradesperson's first repair goal is the assistant, whose mobility stage requires field-level technical work in the first weeks of play.

The Maker's first visit may coincide with or follow the assistant's partial repair. If the Maker arrives and the assistant is functional at Stage 2 — moving, limited communication, clearly patched — the Maker will look at the assistant's chassis.

*"You did this."*

Not a question. A reading of the work.

*"Show me the join."*

The assistant repair is the most personal act of craft the Tradesperson has done since the Cascade. It is also, from the Maker's perspective, a master class in how this person thinks about the work — what they prioritized, what they were willing to leave imperfect, what they got right on the first try and what they had to redo. The assistant is a complex system with a history of specific damage. Repairing it correctly required understanding the damage, not just the technique.

The Maker understands this. They've been repairing complex systems with histories of specific damage since the Cascade. This is what they do.

The crossover moment creates a specific bond: the Maker recognizes that the Tradesperson's relationship to craft includes an emotional dimension — they fixed the assistant because the assistant mattered, not just because the problem was soluble. That distinction is not trivial to a Maker. Craft without investment produces technically adequate work. Craft with investment produces something else.

## Maker-Peer Standing

The Tradesperson can achieve a recognized standing with a Maker — specifically, with the first Maker who found them — before they are Sworn, before they have been formally Observed, before the Wardens have made any official contact.

This is not a faction track. It does not show in the elevation path. It exists in a specific relationship with a specific NPC, acknowledged between them but not officially logged.

What it means practically:

- The Maker will speak candidly with the Tradesperson in ways they won't with a stranger — not about House secrets, but about the work, about the Accord's implications for craft, about what it means to be accountable for what you make
- The Maker's workshop is accessible. Not Neutral Ground — that requires the formal Warden designation — but a place where the Tradesperson is known and not questioned
- When the Tradesperson eventually moves toward formal Warden contact, the Maker's quiet awareness of them changes the nature of that contact. The Wardens don't arrive to observe someone unknown. They arrive to formalize a relationship they already have context on.
- If the Tradesperson takes the oath, the Maker's acknowledgment of them is already in the House's informal record. Not the Arbiter's Record — the Makers' own knowledge of who they've seen and what they've seen them do.

## The Moral Question

Somewhere in Month 2 or early Month 3, after the relationship with the Maker has developed — after several visits, several pieces of work seen and acknowledged, possibly after the assistant has reached Stage 3 and the Maker has noted that too — the Maker will ask directly:

*"Why haven't you sworn?"*

This is not a recruitment pitch. The Maker is not trying to acquire the Tradesperson for the Wardens. They are asking a genuine question about a gap they've noticed: someone who does work that reflects the code, who has demonstrated the values the code asks for, who has been seen doing so by someone whose judgment is trusted — and who has not formalized it.

The player can answer however they want. The Maker will hear it.

Some answers close the conversation. Some open it further. The Maker does not push. But the question has been asked, and the player knows it was asked, and the Record — which the Maker is not an Arbiter, but is part of the Warden network — has a shape to it now that didn't exist before.

## The Vouching Mechanic

If and when the Tradesperson takes the Warden oath, the Maker can vouch for them at the Moot.

A vouch from a Maker carries specific weight. The Maker is not a political actor — they're not aligned with the power dynamics within the House, they're not advancing an agenda. When a Maker vouches, the Moot reads it as: *this person has been seen, consistently, doing work that reflects the code, by someone whose only instrument is craft.* It is the most unmediated endorsement available.

Other backgrounds entering the Wardens go through the standard elevation path: Observed → Acknowledged → Sworn, with the Wardens doing the observation and the assessment. The Tradesperson's oath starts from a position where one person inside the House already knows them. They enter with existing internal standing, not external reputation.

**What this creates:** The Tradesperson is trusted faster inside the House, by the people who build and maintain its capacity. That is a different kind of access from what Vanguard standing or Arbiter attention provides — it is trust from the people who know what the House actually is, as opposed to what it presents.

**What it costs the Maker:** The vouch is on the Record. If the Tradesperson later violates the code, the Maker's judgment is in the Record alongside the violation. The Maker has skin in the game. They knew this when they vouched. That weight should be felt.

## The Lineage Thread

The Tradesperson's blueprint provenance is a thread that can run under the arc for months before it resolves. It is not the main story. It is not a faction questline. It is a personal thread with significant depth available for players who pursue it.

**What pursuing it looks like:**

The Bladesmith is the Maker most likely to recognize the technique in the Tradesperson's early work. They may not volunteer it. If the player has built enough relationship — several visits, demonstrated that they can be trusted with complicated information — the Bladesmith will at some point indicate that they've seen this before.

*"This technique. Where did it come from?"*

The thread from there leads backward through whoever trained the Tradesperson's source — back through the Cascade, through a pre-Cascade craftsperson who had a relationship with a Warden House or a House predecessor, through a tradition that was being passed down through informal channels before it was ever formalized as ideology.

**What's at the end:** Not a dramatic revelation. The lineage thread is fundamentally a discovery that the Tradesperson was closer to the Accord than they knew — that the code they're being asked to take was already in the work, in the way they were taught to build things. The craft was the teaching. They just didn't know what it was called.

This is the Tradesperson's version of what the Record gives other backgrounds: a sense of who they were before, reflected back in something they didn't expect.

## Month-by-Month Arc Shape

**Weeks 1–2:** Day 1 tools are immediately tactical. The Cascade is readable in a way it isn't for other backgrounds. The assistant's Stage 1 damage is diagnosable. The first repair attempts happen within the first week. The player establishes a workbench.

**Weeks 3–4:** The fix-him questline Stage 1 components are identifiable. The Tradesperson's field repairs are visibly better than other players' — NPCs in Holdout communities notice. The first indicator of Maker interest surfaces: someone mentions that there's been inquiry about who's been doing the repair work in the area.

**Month 2:** The Maker makes first contact. The evaluation task. The *"Where did you learn this?"* moment. The assistant may be at Stage 2 by now — the crossover happens if so. The relationship begins.

**Month 2–3:** Workshop access. Candid conversations about the craft and what the Accord asks of it. The fix-him questline continues. The Bladesmith sees the work for the first time and says nothing yet. The Warden formal contact is held off — the Maker has indicated, quietly, that it's not time yet.

**Month 3:** The Maker asks: *"Why haven't you sworn?"* The player's answer shapes the next phase. If the player is moving toward the oath, the Maker begins signaling to the House that this is someone worth watching formally. The Behemoth Walk world event tests everything built in the first ninety days — the Tradesperson's capacity here is visible to the Wardens.

**Month 4–6:** Formal Warden contact. The elevation path begins at *Acknowledged* rather than *Observed* — the Wardens are not arriving to assess someone unknown, they are formalizing a relationship they have context on. The Bladesmith may begin the lineage conversation.

**Month 6+:** If the oath is taken, the Maker vouches at the Moot. The lineage thread continues under everything else. The assistant's repair arc converges with the Warden arc in specific ways — a Maker who has seen the assistant across multiple repair stages has watched the Tradesperson's relationship with craft become something more than technical.

## Playing This Background Without the Warden Path

Not every Tradesperson takes the oath. Some players with this background will build deep Holdout relationships, Separated standing, or Compact-adjacent paths. The Maker relationship is still available to them — craft peer standing transcends faction alignment.

A Tradesperson who never takes the Warden oath but maintains the Maker relationship is an interesting condition: trusted inside the House's informal network, not bound by the code, not on the Record in the formal sense. The Maker will note, at some point, that this arrangement is unusual. They won't pressure. But they will note it.

If a Tradesperson who never swore is excommunicado for reasons related to another faction — Compact or Hollow — the Makers' network knows about it but the Warden formal machinery does not apply to them. The Maker relationship is complicated but not severed. They're not in the code, so the code's consequences don't reach them. But the work is still their work, and the Maker is still accountable for it.
