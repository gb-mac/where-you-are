# Excommunicado — Full System Design v0.1

*"The Wardens don't hunt you. They just make sure everyone else knows where you are."*

---

## The Division Parallel

The Division's rogue agent system works because it inverts the social contract — someone who was trusted, who carried authority, who operated under a flag of protection, chose to break the rules that flag represented. The betrayal is what makes it land.

Excommunicado is the same inversion, with more permanence and more texture. A Sworn who goes excommunicado didn't just break a rule — they broke an oath they chose to take, in front of a witness, in a world that desperately needs people who keep their word. The weight of that is heavier than a rogue agent designation. And unlike the Division's system, there is no timer. No extraction that clears the status. You carry it.

But you also keep playing. And the world keeps watching.

---

## The Declaration

Excommunicado begins with a formal Declaration by an Arbiter — always in-person, always witnessed, always logged to the Record.

The Declaration is not rushed. If the violation was witnessed directly by an Arbiter, it can happen immediately. If it was reported, the Arbiter investigates first — interviews witnesses, reviews available evidence, checks the Record for context. False declarations are themselves a code violation; Arbiters do not issue them carelessly.

When the Declaration is made:

1. The Arbiter formally states the violation — specific, factual, no embellishment
2. The excommunicado player's AR overlay changes: a permanent **Mark** appears on their profile, visible to other players with any Warden standing or access
3. The Warden network propagates the notification — every House, every chapter, every Sworn receives it within hours via the network's relay system
4. Every faction with a Warden relationship receives formal notification — the Compact, the Holdout councils the Wardens are allied with, the Signal contacts, the Covenant chapters they communicate with
5. The Historical Marker appears at the site of the violation

The player is notified. Not warned — notified. The decision has been made. The world knows.

## The Mark

The Mark is visible in the AR overlay to any player who has Warden standing — which, at Recognized tier and above, is a significant portion of the active player population.

The Mark displays:
- The player's identifier
- The nature of the violation (specific, factual — exactly as the Arbiter stated it)
- The date and location of the Declaration
- Current approximate location (updated in real-time)

The Mark is not a target reticle. It is information. What other players do with that information is their choice — and their responsibility.

Players without Warden standing cannot see the Mark directly. But they will notice that other players react to the marked player differently. NPCs react differently. Warden estates turn them away. The social texture of the world changes around them in ways that communicate the status even without the overlay notation.

## The Hunt

The Wardens do not maintain a standing assassination force for excommunicado players. They have the Vanguard, and the Vanguard have other obligations. What the Wardens do is make the excommunicado player's status universally known — and let the world respond.

**Vanguard Response**

Active Vanguard who encounter an excommunicado player are obligated to deny them Neutral Ground access and to formally inform any nearby Warden-aligned community of their presence. They will not initiate combat unprovoked — the code doesn't permit aggression without cause. But if the excommunicado player attempts to enter Neutral Ground, the Vanguard will physically stop them. If the player uses violence to try to force entry, the Vanguard will respond with force.

Vanguard will also accept and formally witness **Bounty Declarations** against excommunicado players from injured parties — factions or individuals who have a legitimate grievance from the violation that caused the excommunicado. A witnessed Bounty Declaration means the Vanguard will actively pursue the excommunicado player on behalf of the injured party. This is rare — the Wardens don't become an enforcement arm lightly — but it happens.

**Player Bounties**

Any player with Warden standing can formally register as a Hunter for an excommunicado player by declaring to an Arbiter. This:
- Gives them enhanced AR tracking of the marked player's position
- Grants them temporary access to Warden safe routes in the marked player's likely territory
- Registers the Hunt in the Record — if the Hunter succeeds, it's noted. If the Hunter fails, or if the marked player defeats them cleanly, that's noted too.

Hunters are still bound by the code. A Hunter who kills civilians while pursuing an excommunicado player, or who uses the Hunt as cover for their own violations, faces their own Arbiter review. The Hunt does not suspend the rules — it operates within them.

**Player choice: Help or Hunt**

Non-Warden players and players who haven't declared as Hunters face a simpler choice: do you help the marked player, ignore them, or try to collect?

Helping an excommunicado player — sheltering them, providing resources, actively fighting Hunters on their behalf — does not automatically make you excommunicado. But it is noted by Arbiters who observe it. At low Warden standing, it costs you nothing formal. At Recognized or Elevated standing, helping a marked player without accounting for it to an Arbiter is a code question that the Arbiter will eventually raise. The code doesn't require you to hunt. It does expect you to account for what you chose.

Ignoring a marked player is neutral. The world doesn't require you to be an enforcer.

Hunting without Warden registration is just PvP — no formal standing, no Record entry, no Vanguard support. You're doing it for whatever personal reason you have, and the consequences are yours.

## Operating Excommunicado

Being excommunicado changes what the world offers and what it costs. It doesn't end the game. Some players will find the excommunicado state more interesting than operating under the code's protection.

**What you lose:**
- Neutral Ground access — the one unconditional safe zone in the game is closed
- Marker call-in — any Markers you held at the time of excommunicado are frozen. The debtors are released. You kept the debt and lost the tool.
- Formal Warden support — routes, introductions, standing
- Reputation buffer — the Mark follows you into interactions with every Warden-aligned faction

**What you still have:**
- Everything you built before the excommunicado. Skills, resources, territory knowledge, relationships with non-Warden factions.
- The Hollow don't care about the Mark — or rather, they care about it the way they care about anything: as information about opportunity. A high-standing excommunicado player might be exactly the kind of contact a Syndicate wants.
- The Covenant's reaction depends on *why* you were excommunicado. A violation against the Wardens is interesting to them. A violation against machine activity is something else.
- The Signal will work with you if you have useful information or skills. They note the Mark but they're pragmatic.
- Other excommunicado players. There is an informal network — not organized, not safe, but real. People who are outside the system and have learned to operate there.

**The Dark Corridors**

Territory that falls entirely outside Warden jurisdiction — deep Hollow zones, Fractured-dense areas, locations so contested that no faction has real authority — operates by different rules. No Neutral Ground because there's no Warden presence to enforce it. No Mark advantage for Hunters because the AR infrastructure in those areas is degraded.

Excommunicado players who learn to operate in Dark Corridors find a different kind of freedom. The rules of the rest of the world don't follow you there. Neither does the support.

Dark Corridors are high-risk, high-resource zones. They're where excommunicado players go to rebuild — and where the most desperate or dangerous people in the world already operate.

## The Wrongful Excommunicado

The system is run by people. People make mistakes. People can be manipulated.

A wrongful excommunicado — a player or NPC declared excommunicado on the basis of false evidence, manufactured testimony, or an Arbiter who was compromised — is the system's darkest moment. The Wardens' authority depends entirely on the integrity of the Record. A false entry in the Record is not just an injustice; it is an attack on the institution itself.

Wrongful excommunicado is a major questline type. The player (or an NPC they're invested in) was declared based on a lie. Proving it requires:

- Finding the original witnesses and establishing what they actually saw
- Tracing who provided false testimony and why
- Bringing the evidence to an Arbiter — a different Arbiter than the one who issued the Declaration
- The reviewing Arbiter must weigh the evidence, interview the original Arbiter, and make a determination

If the wrongful declaration is overturned:
- The original entry is not deleted — the Record doesn't delete — but a formal **Correction** is appended, visible alongside the original entry, explaining what was found
- The player's excommunicado status is lifted
- The individual who provided false testimony faces their own Arbiter review
- If an Arbiter issued the Declaration in bad faith, they face removal from the Arbiters — the most serious consequence the Wardens can impose on one of their own
- The Historical Marker at the violation site gains a Correction marker

If the wrongful declaration is not overturned — because the evidence isn't sufficient, because witnesses can't be found, because the wrong people got there first — the player remains excommunicado, knowing the truth, in a world that doesn't.

That's the hardest version of the story. And the Wardens, if they're worth anything, have to be capable of being wrong about someone.

## The Long Game — Rebuilding in the Open

The excommunicado arc doesn't end with the declaration. It begins there.

A player who chooses to keep operating — visibly, under the Mark, without the protections the code offered — is doing something the Wardens didn't design for and can't quite categorize. The code doesn't have a path back. But the Arbiters have eyes.

Over time, if an excommunicado player consistently acts in ways that align with the code's values — protecting communities, keeping their word, operating with the discipline and purpose the Vanguard exemplify — the Arbiters note it. Not forgiveness. Not reinstatement. Acknowledgment.

The Record gets a new section: *Conduct Following Excommunicado.* It's visible. Other players can read it. Factions that work with the Wardens can see both chapters — what was done, and what was done after.

Some Warden communities will quietly open their doors to a marked player with a long positive post-excommunicado Record. Not formally — the Neutral Ground rule doesn't change — but a Holdout under Warden protection might look the other way when a marked player camps on their edge. A Sworn might share information they technically shouldn't. A House heir might meet with you off the estate grounds.

None of this is amnesty. All of it is human.

The goal of the excommunicado arc, narratively, is not redemption in the traditional sense. It's the question of what you build when you can't build under the system that built you. Some players will answer that question with violence and go fully Hollow-adjacent. Some will answer it by doing exactly what they did as Sworn, with no recognition and no safety net. Both are legitimate. Both are on the Record.

## The Division Parallel — What This Adds

The Division's rogue system is thrilling in the moment — the scramble, the visibility, the other players vectoring in. It resets. Excommunicado doesn't.

What this adds over the Division model:

| Division Rogue | Excommunicado |
|---|---|
| Timer-based, resets | Permanent, no timer |
| Everyone can see you | Warden-standing players can see you — social spread from there |
| Hunted by other players | Hunted by a formal system with rules, plus players who opt in |
| Cleared by extraction | Cleared only by wrongful declaration appeal — otherwise never |
| Moral weight: temporary | Moral weight: permanent. The Record has it forever. |
| No narrative beyond the moment | A full story arc with faction consequences |

The rogue moment in the Division is exciting. The excommunicado arc in *Where You Are* is a campaign.

## Named NPC Excommunicado — World Events

NPCs can go excommunicado. When a named NPC — a House heir, a Compact commander, a Sworn with significant questline history — is declared, it's a world event.

The Declaration propagates to all players in the region. The NPC's behavior changes visibly. The world's reaction to them shifts. Factions that were allied with them have to decide what to do.

This is the system demonstrating that no one is above it. A House heir going excommunicado for violating the code is not a glitch in the Warden authority structure — it is the authority structure working as designed. The hardest thing a code-based institution can do is apply the code to itself.

Players who were allied with that NPC have to decide: honor the declaration, or stand with someone they believe in who the system has turned against?

That decision goes in the Record too.
