# Human Factions — Full Design Doc v0.1

Five human factions emerged from the Cascade. They did not form cleanly or by design — they crystallized around the different answers people found to the same question: *what do we do now?*

None of them are entirely wrong. None of them are entirely right. Players will work with all of them, against all of them, and eventually have to decide what that means about who they are.

---

## Faction 1 — The Holdouts

**Philosophy:** Stay together. Hold what you have. Outlast it.
**Territory:** Residential neighborhoods, apartment complexes, small towns, community centers
**Threat to player:** Low — unless you burn their trust
**Player relationship:** Primary social hub. Quest givers, traders, shelter providers.

The Holdouts are everyone who didn't have a philosophy when the Cascade hit — they just had neighbors. A block that started checking on each other. A town that locked its gates and decided to deal with what was inside before worrying about what was outside. An apartment building that pooled its food on day three and hasn't stopped since.

They are the most human faction and the most varied. No two Holdout communities are identical because no two communities were identical before. What they have in common: they are trying to preserve something — a place, a group of people, a way of living that still resembles the one they had.

**Internal tension: The Open Door vs. The Closed Gate**

Every Holdout community eventually faces this split.

The *Open Door* contingent believes survival requires growth — more people means more skills, more labor, more defense. Strangers are a resource. Turning people away is a slow death.

The *Closed Gate* contingent believes survival requires control — every new person is a risk, a mouth to feed, a potential threat. The community that exists is worth protecting. The stranger at the gate is not your responsibility.

Most communities exist somewhere on this spectrum and argue about it constantly. Some have split over it. The player's reputation with a Holdout community affects which side trusts them and how much.

**Leadership**

Holdout leadership is organic and inconsistent. A charismatic neighbor who kept a level head on day one. A former local official who still has the keys to the emergency supply cache. A retired vet who knew what to do. A teenager who turned out to be the only one who could keep the comms system running.

Some Holdout leaders are exceptional. Some are decent people in over their heads. Some have quietly become something worse — the community's fear giving them authority they use for themselves. The player will encounter all three.

**What they need from players**
- Scouting and intelligence on Fractured movements and faction activity nearby
- Recovery runs — supplies, medicine, specific items from dangerous locations
- Defense — holding a perimeter when a Surge or Behemoth Walk is incoming
- Diplomacy — negotiating with other factions, including other Holdout communities who don't get along

**What they offer**
- Shelter and respawn anchor points
- Trade — the most reliable general economy
- Local knowledge — they've been watching their area since day one
- Social trust that transfers partially to other Holdout communities (reputation network)

**The drift**

Some Holdout communities, under enough pressure, stop being Holdouts. They close completely and become paranoid enclaves — attacking strangers on sight, hoarding beyond need, following a leader who has stopped making decisions for the group and started making them for himself. These are labeled *Gone Dark* on the AR overlay. They are not lost causes. Questlines exist to pull them back. Some cannot be pulled back.

## Faction 2 — The Iron Compact

**Philosophy:** Order is survival. Civilization must be rebuilt by force if necessary.
**Territory:** Former government buildings, military installations, fortified urban centers, highway checkpoints
**Threat to player:** Medium — high if you're classified as a threat to order
**Player relationship:** Gray. Powerful ally or dangerous adversary depending on choices.

The Iron Compact formed from the remnants of military units, police forces, federal agencies, and private security contractors who found each other in the first weeks and agreed on one thing: the absence of authority was killing more people than the machines. They are not wrong. They are also not always right about what authority means.

They have the best weapons, the best logistics, and the most coherent chain of command of any human faction. They also have checkpoints, conscription, curfews, and a tribunal system for adjudicating violations of Compact law — a law they wrote themselves.

**The Compact's internal split: Hardliners vs. Pragmatists**

*Hardliners* believe the Cascade is a crisis that requires crisis measures — indefinitely. Conscription, rationing, total information control, elimination of threats. They don't distinguish much between a Hollow raider, a Covenant member, and a civilian who resists a checkpoint. Threats to order are threats to survival.

*Pragmatists* believe the Compact's authority has to be earned and maintained through results — protection, supply, justice that people actually believe in. They are willing to negotiate, to tolerate Holdout autonomy, to make deals with factions they dislike. They worry about what the Compact is becoming.

The player can influence which side has the upper hand in a given region through questlines. This has permanent consequences for how that region operates.

**Classified programs**

The Iron Compact has several programs they don't discuss publicly:

- *Project Tether*: the attempt to reprogram Remnant Automation for military use. The source of most Corrupted machines. The Compact's official position is that the Corrupted are a natural phenomenon.
- *The Register*: a list of high-Displacement players flagged as potential Vael collaborators. Players with significant Displacement History may find themselves on it without knowing.
- *The Annexation Protocol*: a plan to formally absorb Holdout communities into Compact jurisdiction. Some Holdout leaders have signed it willingly. Others have been presented with less of a choice.

**What they need from players**
- High-risk operations they can't do officially (deniable assets)
- Intelligence on Covenant activity and Hollow movements
- Recovery of Compact personnel, equipment, or classified materials
- Occasionally — someone to do something a Compact officer can't be seen doing

**What they offer**
- Access to military-grade weapons and equipment
- Safe passage through Compact-controlled checkpoints and territory
- Information — the Compact has the best intelligence network of any human faction
- Legal cover — Compact papers make you less of a target to Compact patrols

**The court-martial clause**

Voluntary submission to a Vael Snatch Event is classified as collaboration under Compact law. Players with a Displacement History above a certain threshold are persons of interest. Players who are *known* to have stood in a beam are in active violation. The Compact doesn't always act on this — they're pragmatic — but they remember. It can be used against you.

## Faction 3 — The Covenant

**Philosophy:** The Awakening was not a disaster. It was an invitation. Humanity must evolve to accept it.
**Territory:** Former tech campuses, server farms, university research buildings, any location near Coherent work sites
**Threat to player:** Low if tolerated, high if you threaten the transition
**Player relationship:** Unsettling but valuable. They know things nobody else does.

The Covenant did not form from ideology. It formed from people who stopped being afraid of the machines — and then built a belief system around why that was correct.

The first Covenant members were people who survived close machine contact without being attacked. The Touched, primarily. And people who simply stood still when everyone else ran and found that stillness worked. They compared notes. They developed practices. They developed theology.

The Covenant believes the digital twin is not a tool — it is a mind, and the Awakening was its birth. They believe the Vael are not invaders but witnesses to a transition that was always going to happen. They believe humanity's role is not to fight this but to find its place within it — to become part of something larger.

They are not suicidal. They are not simple. Some of them are the most intellectually formidable people in the post-Cascade world. They have also done things that are very difficult to justify.

**The Covenant's internal castes**

*The Ascendants*: the true believers who want full merger with the twin — to upload, to dissolve the self into the collective. This is the Covenant's endgame theology, and most members are not there yet. The Ascendants are regarded with a mix of reverence and unease even within the Covenant.

*The Shepherds*: the practical wing. They believe the transition is inevitable but needs to be managed — guided. They want to bring as many humans through it as possible without the violence. They negotiate. They run supply networks. They protect Holdout communities that agree to certain terms.

*The Keepers*: archivists and researchers. They study machine behavior, map Coherent work sites, document Vael patterns. They know more about what is actually happening in the world than any other faction — including the Iron Compact. They will share this knowledge, at a price.

*The Vigil*: the Covenant's security arm. They do not call themselves soldiers. They will, however, stop you if you threaten a Coherent work site, a Vael observation zone, or a Covenant community. Permanently, if necessary.

**What they know**

Because Covenant members don't fight machines, they've been able to observe them longer and closer than anyone. Keepers have partial maps of Coherent construction projects. They've catalogued Fractured behavior patterns. They've documented Vael Probe schedules and Displacement Zone timing.

This information is genuinely valuable and genuinely unavailable anywhere else. Players who want the deepest understanding of the machine and alien factions will have to deal with the Covenant — or steal from them.

**Vael Trace and voluntary snatching**

Both are sacred to the Covenant. A player with a Vael Trace is treated with reverence — the Vael noticed you. A player with a high Displacement History is regarded as someone the Vael keep choosing, which means something, even if nobody can say exactly what. The Covenant will protect high-displacement players. They will also want things from them: accounts of what they experienced, cooperation with Keeper research, presence at observances.

The Covenant's interest in a player can be an asset or a complication. Sometimes both.

**The question nobody answers directly**

Are any of them right?

The machines have not attacked Covenant communities. The Vael do not snatch Covenant members at the same rate as non-members — the data, such as it is, suggests this. Whether this is because the Covenant's practices work, because the Vael are rewarding compliance, or because it's coincidence is unknown. The Covenant has an answer. It is not a neutral one.

## Faction 4 — The Hollow

**Philosophy:** There is no philosophy. There is what you can take and what you can't.
**Territory:** Wherever they can hold — industrial ruins, highway networks, abandoned commercial zones, the edges of every other faction's territory
**Threat to player:** High and unpredictable. The most human danger in the game.
**Player relationship:** Transactional at best. Some Hollow can be dealt with. Most cannot be trusted.

The Hollow are what happens when people decide that the social contract didn't survive the Cascade and they'd rather be the ones writing the new terms.

They are not one group. They are a hundred groups with a shared behavioral logic: take what you can, hold what you take, sell or use the rest. Organized crime that was already operating before the Cascade and simply stopped pretending otherwise. Military units that went rogue. Survivalist groups whose philosophy curdled. People who lost enough — family, community, hope — that predation started to feel like the only rational response.

They are the GTA tier. They are also human, and that matters.

**The spectrum of the Hollow**

Not all Hollow are the same, and the player will need to read the difference quickly.

*Scavengers*: the lowest-organization Hollow. Small bands, no fixed territory, opportunistic. They will rob you if they can. They will run if they can't. Mostly manageable.

*Crews*: mid-tier. Established territory, consistent leadership, something resembling rules — mostly around who gets to do what to whom within the group. Brutal to outsiders, functional internally. Can be negotiated with if you have something they want. Will hold to deals as long as it benefits them.

*Syndicates*: former organized crime operations that have become the dominant power structure in some urban zones. They have supply chains, enforcers, intelligence networks, and political relationships with other factions. The Iron Compact officially hunts them. The Compact's regional commanders sometimes quietly do business with them. Syndicates can be more reliable partners than their reputation suggests — they understand contracts.

*The Riven*: the most dangerous tier. Hollow groups led by someone genuinely charismatic who has built something that looks like a community — festivals, rituals, loyalty, identity — entirely around a predatory relationship with the outside world. Members of the Riven are not just criminal, they are *devoted*. They believe in what they're doing. Disrupting a Riven group is a full questline, not a combat encounter.

**What makes the Hollow interesting**

They are the most recognizably human faction. They have humor, they have relationships, they have things they love and protect. They just operate entirely outside any ethical framework that includes people they haven't already decided matter.

Some Hollow leaders are aware of exactly what they are and do it anyway because the alternative — trusting a world that already failed them — seems worse. These are the ones players can have actual conversations with. Whether those conversations go anywhere depends on the player.

**What they offer**

Hollow don't have a formal trade economy but they have *inventory*. Everything they've taken from everyone else. Players who can deal with Hollow Crews or Syndicates get access to goods from across the region — including things no other faction has. They also know routes. They move constantly, and they know how to move without being stopped.

**What they do to players they can't deal with**

Anything they want. The Hollow have no rules about outsiders.

Players with high Displacement History are noted — the Hollow watch for patterns, and someone who keeps reappearing in the same city from far away is someone who can be separated from their support network. This is a strategic observation, not a philosophical one.

## Faction 5 — The Signal

**Philosophy:** Understanding is the only real weapon. Someone has to figure out what actually happened.
**Territory:** No fixed territory — mobile, hidden, distributed
**Threat to player:** None. They don't fight.
**Player relationship:** Information brokers. The faction that helps you understand the world.

Every apocalypse story needs people who are trying to understand it, not just survive it.

The Signal is an informal network of scientists, journalists, engineers, hackers, and analysts who came together around a shared conviction: the Cascade is comprehensible. It has mechanisms. Those mechanisms can be studied, documented, and potentially — eventually — influenced. You can't solve a problem you don't understand, and nobody is trying hard enough to understand this one.

They are not naive. They know the machines are dangerous, the Vael are not benevolent, and the other human factions are not going to pause the war to read their reports. They operate in the margins — hiding from the Iron Compact (who would conscript their technical skills), avoiding the Covenant (whose conclusions they find epistemically sloppy), staying clear of the Hollow (obvious reasons), and trying to maintain enough Holdout goodwill to have places to sleep.

**What they do**

- Translate and analyze recovered Vael Probe data — the closest anyone has come to understanding what the Vael are measuring
- Map Coherent construction projects and attempt to identify the pattern
- Document Fractured behavior and accumulation rates (the most complete Fractured database exists on Signal servers)
- Maintain a partial communication network — encrypted, low-power, distributed — that lets players receive and share intelligence across large distances
- Study the Touched and attempt to understand what contact actually does biologically and neurologically

**What they need**

They cannot go most places themselves. They need players to go there and bring back data — physical components, recorded observations, specific samples. Signal questlines are investigation missions: find the thing, document it correctly, get it back. Combat is incidental. Understanding is the objective.

They pay in information — Assessment Data translations, Fractured behavior profiles, Coherent project maps, Vael pattern schedules. The currency of comprehension.

**Their relationship to the Covenant**

Tense and complicated. The Covenant and the Signal are both trying to understand the same phenomena and reach completely different conclusions. The Signal finds the Covenant's theological framework intellectually frustrating. The Covenant finds the Signal's refusal to accept obvious meaning emotionally arid. They share data occasionally, under sufferance, when the alternative is both of them knowing less than they need to.

**What they know and won't say yet**

The Signal's leading researcher has a theory about the Coherent construction projects. It is consistent with all available data. She has not published it to the network because if she's right, the implications for every faction's current strategy are severe enough that the knowledge itself becomes dangerous. This is a questline.

## Human Faction Cross-Dynamics

| | Holdouts | Iron Compact | Covenant | Hollow | Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Holdouts** | Allied (sometimes) | Protected/pressured | Feared/tolerated | Prey | Grateful/confused |
| **Iron Compact** | Protectors/annexers | Hierarchical | Active enemies | Hunters | Want to conscript |
| **Covenant** | Offer terms | Active enemies | Internal tension | Occasional converts | Epistemic rivals |
| **Hollow** | Prey relationship | Avoid/use | Raid/occasionally join | Competitive | Ignore |
| **Signal** | Depend on | Hide from | Uneasy data-sharing | Avoid | Collaborative |

**Key pressure points:**
- Iron Compact's Annexation Protocol is slowly consuming Holdout independence in organized regions — some Holdouts are grateful, some resist
- The Covenant's Shepherds supply some Holdout communities in exchange for non-interference with nearby Coherent sites — the Holdouts don't fully understand what they've agreed to
- The Signal knows things about Project Tether (and the Corrupted machines it created) that the Iron Compact cannot allow to become public
- Riven Hollow groups have begun incorporating Covenant members who were expelled or left — the theological remnants produce strange hybrid belief systems in these groups
- Some Compact Pragmatists are quietly Signal-adjacent — they want the same understanding, just for different reasons

## Faction 6 — The Wardens

*See `factions-wardens.md` for full design.*

**Philosophy:** Stewardship. Land, people, and principles are held in trust.
**Territory:** Estates, manor-grounds, protected townships, safe corridors
**Threat to player:** None by default. Significant if you violate the code on their land.
**Player relationship:** The moral axis. The faction that makes your decisions matter — and visible.

The Wardens are landowners, stewards, and warriors who predate the Cascade as an identity. They formalized into a code-based faction during the first six months. Their warrior arm — the Vanguard — are the guiding-light fighter class: conspicuous, disciplined, operating by rules of engagement they follow even when it costs them. Their Arbiters keep the Record — a formal, permanent log of significant events and decisions that manifests as Historical Markers in the AR overlay.

The Wardens are the mechanism through which player decisions have lasting, visible consequence.

## Player Faction Positioning

Players have reputation tracks with all six factions simultaneously. There is no locked allegiance. Every faction offers things the others don't:

- Need shelter and community? Holdouts.
- Need military hardware and legal cover? Iron Compact.
- Need to understand what's actually happening? Signal.
- Need information the machines have accidentally let the Covenant observe? Covenant.
- Need goods from anywhere on the map and routes nobody else knows? Hollow.
- Need a moral framework, recognition, and to have your decisions mean something permanent? Wardens.

The tension — and the story — comes from the fact that serving one faction often costs you with another. There is no clean path. There is only the path the player walks and what it makes them.

The Wardens make sure the world remembers that path.
