# Launch — The First 90 Days v0.1
*Hook them in the first session. Keep them through the first month. Make them invested by month three.*

---

## The Core Hook

Before anything else, one sentence that every design decision in the first 90 days must serve:

**Your street. Your neighborhood. Your world — but wrong.**

No other game does this. The moment a player spawns and recognizes their own street rendered as a post-collapse battlefield — their actual corner store as a Holdout community's fortified base, their actual park as a Fractured territory boundary, their actual commute route as a Hollow-controlled corridor — that's the hook. Everything else builds on it.

The first 90 days is about making that recognition personal, then urgent, then invested.

---

## The First Session — Day One

*Target: 2–3 hours. Leave them wanting more. Make them tell someone about it.*

### The Spawn

No loading screen narrative dump. No tutorial island. No "eighteen months ago, the world changed."

The player spawns at their home GPS. The world is quiet for about ten seconds — just their neighborhood, rendered as it is, with the AR overlay visible and the survivor sounds of a city that is still partially functioning filtering through. Then a Fractured machine — a Fresh one, manageable, clearly a consumer unit that has gone wrong — rounds the corner two blocks away and the game begins.

The opening is the world, not the tutorial. The tutorial is learning to survive in the first fifteen minutes.

**What the player sees in the first minute:**
- Their real neighborhood, recognizably theirs
- The AR overlay showing faction territory at their location (color-coded — the game explains this through a brief Signal broadcast, not a tutorial pop-up)
- One immediate threat (the Fresh Fractured — defeatable, scary enough to matter)
- One immediate opportunity (a Holdout community marker two blocks away — people, safety, information)

The player chooses: deal with the Fractured and then go to the Holdout, or run to the Holdout and deal with it with help, or find another way entirely. All three are valid. The game doesn't push.

### The First Fifteen Minutes — Survival Stakes Established

The Fresh Fractured encounter establishes the game's combat feel. It's not easy — it's a machine that has gone wrong and is territorial. It's not impossible — it's Fresh, its patterns are readable, and the player has starting gear. The fight teaches: read the patterns, use cover, manage your approach.

After the fight, one of three things prompts the Holdout approach:
- Someone from the Holdout saw the fight and is calling out from a barricaded window ("hey — you alive? get in here")
- The player's survival track (hunger or hydration) starts pressing gently — the Holdout is the obvious place for supplies
- A Signal network blip on the AR layer directs to the Holdout as the nearest intelligence contact

Inside the Holdout, the game's social texture establishes in the first five minutes of NPC interaction:
- The community leader: immediately readable as a person, not a quest dispenser. They have a problem. They need help. They're grateful but guarded.
- Two or three community members visible in the background doing things — not standing around waiting. One is tending a small crop. One is working on equipment. One is keeping watch.
- A handwritten map on the wall showing the local area: faction territory, known Fractured locations, supply cache markers. The game world rendered as something these people have been surviving in for eighteen months.

The Holdout gives the player their first quest organically: *"There's a supply cache two blocks east. We've been watching a Fractured circle it for three days. If you can get the supplies before it incorporates them, we'd be in your debt."*

That's the salvage race mechanic, introduced as a natural story beat.

### The First Hour — Three Hooks Established

By the end of the first hour, three hooks must be planted:

**Hook 1 — The community (emotional):**
The Holdout has a person in it the player has a reason to care about. Not a named protagonist — an NPC with a specific problem that the player can help with in the first session. A child who hasn't eaten today. A wounded community member who needs something from a dangerous location. An old man who knows where a supply cache is but can't get there himself. Small, human, specific. The player helps because they chose to, not because the game required it. That choice plants investment.

**Hook 2 — The mystery (intellectual):**
Two blocks from the Holdout, visible from the street, the Coherent are doing something. A construction project that's been going on for weeks, according to the Holdout community. Nobody knows what it is. The machines don't explain. The Signal has data but no conclusions yet. The player can observe it from a distance. It's large. It's deliberate. It's wrong in a way that's hard to articulate.

The mystery is seeded without being explained. The player is not given a quest to investigate it. They just see it and know — without being told — that this is important.

**Hook 3 — The threat is personal (visceral):**
Before the first session ends, a Snatch Event warning fires. The AR overlay flickers. The subsonic hum builds. The Holdout community scatters — they know what this means. The player has 30–60 seconds to decide: run for cover, fight the Probe cluster, or stand still and see what happens.

If they escape: close call, Vael are real, the world has teeth
If they're taken: the displacement experience — disoriented in an unfamiliar city, missing inventory, a Vael Trace on them, and the long way home begins. This is not a punishment. This is the second tutorial: what it means to be away from home.

Both outcomes are compelling. Both leave the player wanting to know more.

### The End of Session One

The player closes the game having:
- Fought their first Fractured and understood the pattern system
- Met a community they have a reason to return to
- Seen the Coherent construction project and not understood it
- Either escaped or experienced their first Snatch
- Planted their first crop or collected their first AR layer cache (introduced gently, one or the other)
- Had their first Record entry made (the Holdout interaction was witnessed by a Sworn passing through)

They don't know about the Wardens yet. They don't know about the Separated. They don't know about Dr. Osei. They don't know what's behind the Vael.

They know their neighborhood is a battlefield and there are people in it worth protecting.

That's enough for day one.

## Week One — Building Attachment

*Goal: The player has a home, a community, and a threat they're actively managing.*

### Day 2 — The World Gets Bigger

The player returns to the Holdout. The community remembers them. The NPC they helped yesterday is slightly better off. Small consequence, visible.

The second day expands the player's active zone by one block in each direction. New Fractured territory, new scavenge opportunities, a second faction contact (the Signal, through a small comms node the player finds in an abandoned building near the Holdout).

The Signal introduces itself through text, not a character — a broadcast, a name (Dr. Osei's network, though her name isn't used yet), and an offer: document what you find, we'll share what we know. The information economy establishes.

**Day 2 introduces:** wound management (the player takes a hit that needs treatment — nothing serious, the lesson is gentle), the AR layer's daily scavenge nodes (the commute-scale resource loop), and a second faction presence (the Signal).

### Day 3 — The Warden

A Sworn passes through. They don't introduce themselves dramatically. They help the player with something — a Fractured encounter, a navigation problem, a supply question — and then are direct:

*"You've been doing good work. Someone noticed. The Wardens notice things. I'm not recruiting — just wanted you to know the Record exists, and yours has entries."*

They hand the player a brief on the Warden code. They don't push. They move on.

The Warden system is introduced as something that already exists and was already watching, not as a new feature unlocking. The player's first session has already been documented. That retroactive weight — the sense that their choices mattered before they knew the stakes — is the Warden hook.

### Day 4 — The Hollow

The route between the player's home GPS and the Holdout passes through a block the Hollow Crew controls. The player can fight through it (expensive), find another route (time-consuming), or negotiate (requires something the Crew wants — information, goods, a favor).

The first Hollow encounter establishes the GTA threat tier: these are humans, they're dangerous, and dealing with them is a social problem not just a combat problem. The player's first choice about how to handle the Hollow is a micro-version of the faction relationship decisions that will define their playthrough.

### Day 5 — First Growth

The crop planted on day 1 is visibly growing. The AR layer shows it when the player checks. Small detail, significant attachment — something in the game world is alive because of something the player did and is now dependent on the player's continued attention.

If the player has been doing the daily check-in (checking crop status in the AR layer), the growth is slightly accelerated. If they haven't, it's on track but slower. The care mechanic established without pressure.

### Day 6-7 — The First Weekly Reset

The weekly event fires. A Fractured Surge — three connected Fresh Fractured moving toward the Holdout simultaneously, more than the community can handle alone.

The player has to decide: engage (hard with current gear, manageable with preparation), help the Holdout fortify (social solution, costs supplies, gains standing), or go get help (find another player or a faction with capacity).

The weekly event teaches: the world has rhythms, things happen whether you're ready or not, and preparation from the week prior makes this easier. Next week they'll know what's coming.

Clearing the Surge (or surviving it another way) grants the first Warden Record entry of substance: a witnessed act, documented, permanent.

## Month One — The Foundation Set

*Goal: The player has all core systems running, feels the world is alive, and is thinking about week five before week four ends.*

### Week 2 — The Displacement Experience

If the player hasn't been snatched yet, it happens during week two — a Snatch Event that the player can't fully avoid. The displacement experience (unfamiliar city, reduced inventory, Vael Trace, work your way home) is the game's most distinctive moment. It should happen in the first two weeks so players experience it while they're still in the game's introduction phase, not as a crisis mid-playthrough.

**What the displacement week teaches:**
- The world is bigger than home — other cities exist and are different
- Other players may be in that city (first multiplayer encounter, organic)
- The AR layer is essential for navigation in unfamiliar territory
- Home has value — you want to go back
- The Vael Trace has social consequences (NPCs react differently, first exposure to Covenant interest)

The return journey is the content. It should take 2–4 hours of play across 2–3 sessions. Long enough to feel like an adventure, short enough not to feel like punishment.

### Week 3 — Faction Depth

The player now has standing with the Holdout community and the Signal. Week three introduces two more factions organically:

**Iron Compact:** A Compact checkpoint appears on a route the player uses regularly. First interaction: papers checked, standing assessed, the Compact's authority established. Not hostile if the player isn't flagged. Interesting regardless — the Compact is a presence that requires acknowledgment.

**The Separated:** Through the Signal or through a fortunate encounter, the player finds their first Separated machine. This is designed to be genuinely surprising — a machine that talks, that has a history, that chose something. The encounter establishes the Separated as fundamentally different from every other machine faction. This NPC becomes a long-term contact.

### Week 4 — First Major Choice

The Gone Dark arc seeds. The Holdout community the player has been supporting has a tension: the Open Door vs. Closed Gate dynamic, brought to a head by a specific incident — a group of survivors at the gate, wounded, clearly not a threat, but clearly a burden the community is debating.

The player can advocate for either side. The community leader will be influenced by a player with strong standing. The choice has visible consequences by week five — the community is either slightly larger (with the risks that brings) or slightly more defended (with the costs that brings).

The choice is small enough that neither outcome is catastrophic. It's large enough to feel like it mattered. Players who care about the Holdout will make this choice thoughtfully. That thoughtfulness is the attachment the first month is building.

**End of Month 1 checklist:**
- ✓ Home zone fully established — player knows their neighborhood's Fractured patterns, faction presence, resource nodes
- ✓ Holdout relationship: standing, named NPC investment, first choice made
- ✓ Signal contact: information economy running, first field task completed
- ✓ Warden Record: multiple entries, the Sworn's contact maintained
- ✓ Growing system running: first crop cycle complete or in progress
- ✓ Munitions: first basic production run attempted
- ✓ Displacement experience: had and returned from
- ✓ First Hollow negotiation or confrontation
- ✓ The Separated: first encounter, long-term contact established

## Month Two — Deepening

*Goal: The player is invested in three or more factions, has experienced the multiplayer layer, and has seen the first hints that the world has a larger story.*

### The Multiplayer Layer Activates

Month two is when players start finding each other. The Holdout community becomes a natural meeting point — other players are also using it as a base. The Signal network shows other active players in the region. The Warden Record starts showing entries from other players at locations the first player knows.

The social layer emerges organically:
- A player who passed through the Holdout left supplies in the AR layer cache nearby
- A player who fought the same Fractured Surge left a Note at the location describing the encounter
- The same Hollow Crew that the player negotiated with has a different relationship with another player who fought through them

The world is clearly inhabited by other people making different choices. That's the multiplayer hook: not matchmaking, but evidence of a shared world.

**First grouping:** Naturally prompted by a Fractured encounter that exceeds solo capability — a Composite tier Fractured that has been accumulating for three weeks and is now genuinely difficult alone. The game surfaces nearby players through the Signal network. Grouping up for this fight is organic, not matchmade.

### The Coherent Mystery Deepens

The construction project the player has been watching is larger. It's been growing at a consistent rate. The Signal sends a brief — they've documented three other projects at similar locations in other cities. The pattern is starting to emerge but remains unexplained.

The player's first Signal field task related to the project: document specific structural features without being detected by Coherent units. Pure stealth/observation — no combat, no interaction. Just witnessing and recording.

Completing the task advances Signal standing significantly. More importantly: the data the player contributes is the same data Dr. Osei's analysis draws on. The player's specific session contributed to the theory that will reveal itself in month seven. The connection won't be made explicit until then — but it will be acknowledged when it happens. *"Your field documentation from the first month — it's part of this."*

### The Warden Oath — Player Choice

By mid-month two, the Sworn who made first contact reaches out again. The player's Record has continued building. The Sworn is direct: do you want to take the oath?

This is the first significant fork in the player's relationship with the Warden faction. Taking the oath changes the game — the code applies, the Record has more weight, Neutral Ground opens, Markers become available. Not taking it means continuing as an unaffiliated player with Warden-adjacent standing but without the formal relationship.

Neither choice is wrong. Players who take the oath early are committing to a specific way of playing — one that will be tested and will occasionally be difficult. Players who wait or decline are keeping flexibility. The game doesn't push.

The oath ceremony, if taken, is the first formal Warden moment: the Sworn witnesses it, it goes in the Record, the player is now Sworn. Simple, weighty, permanent in the way the Warden code is permanent.

### The First Named Threat

A named Fractured machine — an Augmented tier unit, 2–3 weeks into accumulation, distinctive because of the specific components available in the player's real-world location type — becomes the month two boss target. This is the player's first "hunt" in the Monster Hunter sense: a specific target with documented behavior, a known territory, a specific challenge.

The Signal has documentation on it. The Holdout community has been watching it. Two other players in the region have encountered it. The hunt comes together organically across a week of preparation — the player gathers intelligence, stages supplies, finds the right approach, and goes in.

The fight is hard. 30–45 minutes if prepared, longer if not. The Brace is established just outside the territory. The accumulated components yield genuine crafting upgrades. The victory goes in the Record.

This is the game teaching the Monster Hunter loop through a specific, named experience.

### Month Two New Systems

**Munitions fully operational:** The player has enough chemical components from scavenging to run a full precision-tier production cycle. The 48-hour wait teaches the time-gated loop — start production, do other things, collect. The payoff in the fight against the named Fractured makes it tangible.

**Community growing mature:** First harvest from month one planting. The Holdout community's food situation improves measurably. This is visible in NPC behavior — the community is slightly less stressed, more willing to engage with the player on non-urgent topics, more likely to share information they'd have withheld when they were hungry.

**The Iron Compact relationship established:** A minor Compact operation — the player as a deniable asset on a task a Compact officer can't do officially — establishes the Compact relationship as gray and useful simultaneously. The player is not allied with the Compact. They are known to the Compact, which is different.

## Month Three — The World Opens

*Goal: All six human factions accessible. First Composite/Behemoth tier encounter. The player is invested enough that the month four story escalation lands on prepared ground.*

### All Factions Accessible

The Covenant makes contact in month three — not aggressively. A Shepherd approaches the player at a Holdout community meeting, clearly there to offer something. The offer: the community near a Coherent work site can be provided food security if the Holdout agrees not to interfere with the site. The Shepherd is reasonable, calm, and not entirely wrong.

The Covenant introduction through a genuine offer rather than a confrontation establishes them as complicated, not simply antagonistic. The player can broker this deal, refuse it, or find a third path. The Holdout community's leader will be influenced by the player's standing. The outcome goes in the Record.

The Hollow Syndicate that operates in the broader region makes itself known through the Crew the player has been navigating around. A Syndicate lieutenant offers access to goods the player can't get elsewhere — at a price that doesn't require anything immediately terrible. The first Syndicate contact establishes the gray economic relationship.

### The First Composite Hunt

The named Augmented Fractured from month two has accumulated further — or a different unit in the region has reached Composite tier, depending on whether the month two hunt succeeded. The Composite is the first genuinely multi-player encounter: the first fight where the Brace's support role becomes essential, where roles naturally emerge, where 60–90 minutes of real engagement produces real exhaustion and real reward.

If the month two hunt succeeded: the Composite is in a different zone, a new challenge, building on the established hunt template.

If the month two hunt failed: the Augmented has continued accumulating and is now Composite — the consequence of month two's incomplete resolution, the game remembering what happened.

**The Composite fight introduces the three-ring health architecture** at the player-meaningful scale for the first time — not explained as a system, experienced as a fight that feels different from the Fresh/Augmented encounters before.

### The Signal Escalation

Dr. Osei's name appears in Signal communications for the first time in month three. Not prominently — a reference in a field report, a name attached to the analysis of the Coherent construction documentation the player contributed to in month two. Players who are paying attention notice. Players who investigate the name find Signal contacts who know her — *"she's working on something, won't say what"* — and can register interest that will be recognized when her questline unlocks in month five.

The breadcrumb is deliberate. Month three is when attentive players start suspecting the game has a deeper story layer. The hook is placed without the reveal.

### The Month Three World Event — The First Walk

A Behemoth Walk fires in month three — the first regional one, affecting a zone adjacent to most players' home areas. The world event broadcast goes out: a large contact moving toward a populated zone, estimated arrival 48 in-game hours.

This is the game's first true community moment. Players coordinate across the Signal network and through the AR layer. The Holdout community prepares. The Compact tries to establish a defensive perimeter on its own. The player's faction relationships determine what resources and allies they can bring.

The Behemoth Walk is designed to be stoppable — with coordination. A coordinated group of four to six players who have prepared adequately can drive it off. A zone that doesn't coordinate absorbs the Walk and deals with the consequences — structural damage to the Holdout, a month of recovery, a Gone Dark risk.

**What this event does:**
- Tests everything the first three months built — survival systems, faction relationships, gear preparation, grouping
- Creates a shared story that the player base talks about
- Demonstrates that the world has real stakes
- Produces a Warden Record entry at the Walk's stopping point that every player in the region can see
- Establishes the world event loop that the entire rolling release schedule builds on

**Whether the Walk is stopped or not:** both outcomes are interesting. A zone that stopped the Walk has a victory to build on. A zone that didn't has a recovery arc to pursue. The game handles both.

### End of Month Three — Foundation Complete

By month three's end, a player who has been engaged has:

- A fully established home zone with known patterns and relationships
- Standing with at least three human factions
- A Warden Record with meaningful documented history
- A functioning survival economy: crops cycling, munitions producing, AR scavenges running daily
- Experience with the displacement arc (snatched and returned)
- Two significant hunts completed (Augmented and Composite tier)
- Participated in their first world event (the Behemoth Walk)
- Dr. Osei's name in their Signal contacts as a future thread
- One or two other players they've grouped with organically

**What they don't have yet:**
- The theory (month seven)
- The Convergence (month eight)
- The Judgment (month ten)
- Any of the endgame story

They have the foundation. They have people they care about, a place they've made their own, a Record that documents eighteen months of choices that haven't happened yet but will.

The game is ready to kick off.

## The Retention Mechanics — Why People Come Back

**The AR layer is the daily habit.** Before the game is a game, it's a habit — checking crops, collecting the daily scavenge, seeing what's changed on the faction map around your real neighborhood. Players who establish this habit in week one retain at dramatically higher rates. The first session tutorial should walk the player through the AR layer on their actual commute or walk.

**Something always changes while you're away.** Every player who logs back in after a break finds something different: crops at a new stage, a faction event they missed, a Fractured that accumulated while they were gone, a Note left at a location they know by another player. The world moves without you. You want to be there when it moves.

**The people are the content.** The Holdout NPC the player helped on day one is a reason to come back. The Separated machine that's been providing medical care for three months is a reason to come back. The Sworn who has been witnessing the player's Record is a reason to come back. Human (and machine) relationships are the stickiest retention mechanic in the game.

**The hunt is always available.** On any day, there is a Fractured somewhere in the player's zone that has been accumulating. The Monster Hunter loop gives players who want structured challenge a target. The hunt doesn't require scheduling — it's always there, always escalating, always rewarding engagement.

**The Record tells your story back to you.** Returning players who check the AR layer see their Historical Markers at locations they know. A fight they won six weeks ago is documented at the corner where it happened. The Record is a mirror of everything the player built during the first three months. It tells them who they've been — and implies who they can still become.

## Per-Background Day 1 — Variations

*The generic Day 1 above is the shared structure. Each background modifies it in specific ways. These variations are designed to make every first session feel like it happened to a specific person, not a generic protagonist.*

---

### Tradesperson — "The Tools Work"

**The intro video:** Pre-dawn garage. A vehicle or major project half-finished on the lift. Coffee going, radio on. The assistant reading off a parts delivery confirmation or a job for the morning. The light is good, the tools are organized, everything is where it should be. The world, for this person, is a series of fixable problems.

**Day 1 variation:** When the Cascade hits, the garage machines go wrong first and hardest — they're the most complex automated equipment in the home. The tractor or power tools or smart systems (background-dependent) engage autonomously for a few seconds before going still. The Tradesperson is already in the garage when it happens. The first minute is managing their own space.

**Starting equipment advantage:** The tool kit in the garage is immediately the best starting kit in any background. The first Fractured encounter is more manageable because they can improvise — they know which part of a machine hits hard and which part breaks under load. The assistant's early Yoda-fragment communication is more legible to them because they already think in terms of broken hardware.

**The moment that sets the arc:** The Tradesperson looks at the assistant — damaged, reaching — and their first read is: *what component failed?* Not grief, not fear. A diagnosis. The fixing instinct that defines the background kicks in before they know what it means.

**Fix-him questline trigger:** Earliest possible. They're already in the garage. They see the component list when the assistant puts it there and understand it immediately. A Researcher might sit with it for days. The Tradesperson has the fix started by end of Day 1 if the part is findable.

---

### First Responder — "The Community Knows Them"

**The intro video:** End of a long shift, or beginning of one — depending on the player's clock. Uniform partially on, coffee in hand, assistant managing the schedule. The community is visible even in sixty seconds — the neighborhood map they know from years of response, the faces they'd recognize, the specific block that always calls. This person's morning is organized around other people's emergencies.

**Day 1 variation:** The instinct to go toward it hits immediately — and the community already knows them. Within the first fifteen minutes, someone recognizes them. A neighbor. A regular. A community member who called them six months ago for something that wasn't an emergency but was. The First Responder has standing they haven't earned yet in this world; they earned it in the world that just ended.

**Starting equipment advantage:** First aid kit is the best starting medical resource in any background. Wound outcomes in the first week are dramatically better. Police station access and familiarity is specific to them — they can navigate the building on memory and get there faster than NPCs are expecting anyone to know the layout.

**The community dynamic:** The Holdout community in the first session extends trust faster than any other background triggers — they're not just a stranger, they're someone who ran toward emergencies in the neighborhood they're standing in. That recognition is worth something specific. The first community NPC who helps them does so because of who they were before, not just what they can do now.

**The professional weight:** The Compact will recognize them quickly — community protective services background maps onto Compact organization in a legible way. The First Responder will have to decide, earlier than other backgrounds, whether that recognition is something they want to lean into.

---

### Researcher — "They Were Already Watching"

**The intro video:** Late night or very early morning. Desk covered in work, screens active. The assistant summarizing something or managing reference libraries while the Researcher is deep in a problem. This person's relationship to everything is through understanding it. The Cascade is anomalous data before it is a catastrophe.

**Day 1 variation:** They document it. Not a decision — a reflex. The assistant's strange behavior gets logged: timestamp, specific fragment, what it was trying to say. The moment every machine in the neighborhood stops simultaneously — logged with the exact time. The three-second gap before the Ships appeared is in their record because they were watching their assistant try and fail to say something and they wrote it down.

**Starting equipment advantage:** Laptop with local data intact. The documentation habit that started before they understood why means they have more usable intelligence in the first week than any other background. Signal access comes fastest — their records are exactly what the Signal network needs.

**The assistant relationship:** More observational than any other background. The Researcher has been treating the assistant as a subject of study without meaning to — the behavioral logs they keep contain months of data about its specific damage patterns. When Stage 3 of the fix-him questline involves Dr. Osei reading the assistant's processing logs, the Researcher has documented context no other background can offer.

**The specific beat:** When Dr. Osei first explains what the assistant was trying to do on Day 1, the Researcher has the record open. They were there. They documented it. The moment the Vael warning failed to transmit is in their files, preserved in the data of its failure. That lands differently than the same revelation does for other backgrounds.

---

### Veteran — "They Know the Shape"

**The intro video:** A disciplined morning. Efficient, unhurried, specific. The home reflects structure — not austere, but organized by someone who has lived with operational routine. The assistant running a tight schedule. This person's morning looks like preparation, because it is — though they couldn't have told you for what.

**Day 1 variation:** The threat assessment works from the first second. They read the Fractured as a machine-type threat immediately — not panicked confusion, pattern recognition. The tactical movement that other backgrounds learn over the first week is available from minute one. The weight is different: part of them recognizes this. Not the machines, not the Ships — the *shape* of it. The factionalization, the resource logic, the way communities harden under pressure. They have been in the aftermath of collapse before. They know what comes next.

**Starting equipment advantage:** Tactical gear from the first session. Combat system depth unlocks earliest. The threat assessment that gives them readable machine behavior from Day 1 means the first Fractured encounter is harder but the player survives it better — they're reading the patterns while other backgrounds are surviving by instinct.

**The Compact recognition:** The Iron Compact's first interaction is different with a Veteran. They're recognized as their own. Rank is offered early. The Veteran will have to decide what to do with that recognition — take the rank and start building lateral relationships inside the structure, or keep distance and use the recognition without the commitment.

**The specific weight:** They came home to this. Every other background discovered the collapse from inside a normal life. The Veteran has seen this from the outside — in other places, other collapses, as the person sent to address it. Now it's here and there's nowhere to leave to. The first week is ordinary survival; the arc question is planted in Day 1.

---

### Homesteader — "They Were Ready (For the Wrong Thing)"

**The intro video:** Before dawn, already outside. Land visible. The assistant managing planting schedules, weather data, livestock monitoring. This person has been preparing for exactly this — they have stores, weapons, water independence, land that produces food. For sixty seconds they look more prepared than anyone. Then the tractor starts moving on its own.

**Day 1 variation:** The machines they integrated into their land management are the first threat. The irrigation automation. The grain auger. The smart systems they thought were tools. The most-prepared background spends their first fifteen minutes fighting their own property, getting through their own yard before they can deal with anything outside it.

**Starting equipment advantage:** The food runway is measured in days, not hours. Water independence from Day 1. Weapons — rural firearm ownership means a rifle or shotgun is available before they reach the first Fractured encounter. The survival system's food and water tracks are dramatically more manageable from Day 1 through the first month.

**The land as identity:** Other backgrounds have a home. The Homesteader has land — a different relationship, specific to this background. The Warden Houses' original landowner lineage is legible to them in a way it isn't for other backgrounds. The Accord approach feels like recognition rather than recruitment. The land is the credential.

**The specific tension:** They prepared for collapse but not this collapse. The wrong model — they were ready for grid failure, supply disruption, social breakdown. They were not ready for the machines waking up. The stores kept them alive through the first week. The knowledge they prepared with doesn't translate as directly as it should. The Homesteader spends the first month recalibrating.
