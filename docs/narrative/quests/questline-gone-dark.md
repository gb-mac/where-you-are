# Questline: Gone Dark — Recovery
*"They didn't become this overnight. You're not going to fix it overnight either."*

---

## Overview

Gone Dark communities attack strangers on sight. Not because their people are evil — because they stopped being able to distinguish between a threat and a person. A fear that curdled, slowly, through four stages that each seemed reasonable at the time, until the community was something none of its members would have recognized in Month 1.

The recovery questline is about trust, not combat. You don't fight your way in. You don't remove the leader. You work the edges of a community that has sealed itself off from the world and find the people inside who still remember what the community was supposed to be.

It is long, non-linear, and does not guarantee success. Communities in late Gone Dark can stabilize at that state. Not every one recovers. The game does not promise a good ending.

What it promises: the attempt is worth making, and what you find inside is always specific.

---

## Trigger

Gone Dark communities are marked in the AR overlay with a specific visual — a community marker that has closed itself. Players encounter them in two ways:

**Incidental contact:** The player approaches a location and is met with immediate hostility from people who look and sound like Holdout survivors, not Hollow. The AR overlay reads the location as a community, not a threat site. Faction NPCs will acknowledge the difference if asked: *"That's a community. Or it was."*

**Signal intelligence:** A Signal cell flags a community that has gone dark in their coverage area — they've lost contact with people they had relationships with, and the documentation of the slide is in their records. The briefing is not a mission assignment. It is: *"We know what happened to them. Someone should try. We can't — they've marked Signal contacts as a threat."*

The questline begins when the player chooses to engage rather than route around.

## Stage 1 — Reading the Perimeter

**"Don't approach the gate. Not yet."**

Every Gone Dark community has a perimeter — fencing, barricades, warning markers that escalated from cautious to hostile as the slide progressed. The perimeter is the community's expression of what it has become: the gap between inside and outside made physical.

Before any contact attempt, the player observes.

**What to look for:**

*The perimeter's internal logic.* Is the fortification uniform, or are there sections that feel different — older, newer, more maintained, clearly an afterthought? Different sections of perimeter tell the story of different moments in the slide. The original cautious fencing. The addition when something happened. The later hardening when someone was lost.

*Activity patterns.* When are people visible on the perimeter and when aren't they? How many? How do they carry themselves — exhausted, vigilant, or something that looks more like performance? Are there any moments when the perimeter is less watched than others?

*Evidence of recent contact.* Signs of a recent confrontation outside the perimeter. Discarded equipment that doesn't match Hollow styling. A Warden Marker placed by a Sworn near the location. Something a Signal reconnaissance drone flagged and didn't follow up on.

*The exits that aren't the gate.* Gone Dark communities have informal exits — people get out for specific reasons that the community tolerates without discussing. Night movements. A specific individual who leaves at irregular intervals and returns. A route that bypasses the perimeter's watch posts. These exits are evidence that the seal is not total.

**What the assistant says, if consulted:**

*"There are people in there who didn't choose this. You're not trying to break in. You're trying to find a way to be let in."*

*"The person who leaves at night — track where they go. They're looking for something the community can't provide anymore."*

## Stage 2 — The Edge Case

**"Someone in there still has doubt."**

Every Gone Dark community has at least one person who is not fully committed to what the community has become. They may have been there since the beginning. They may have joined during the slide and now recognize what they joined into. They are not a dissident — they haven't left, they follow the community's rules, they participate in the defense. But something in them is still calibrating.

Finding the edge case is the crux of the questline. They are not labeled. The player finds them through observation and contact.

**The night-exit person:**

If the player tracked the informal exit in Stage 1, they have a person. Someone who leaves at night — for medicine, for trade, for contact with someone outside the perimeter, for reasons the community doesn't fully inquire into because they need the person and the community's rules have a certain pragmatic flexibility for what it needs.

Making contact with them outside is possible — but specific. They are already doing something semi-unauthorized. If the player approaches wrong, they lose the contact and the community's defenses heighten. If the player approaches correctly — slowly, non-threateningly, demonstrating knowledge of the community and its history that signals something other than predation — the person stops.

*"How do you know about us?"*

That question is the opening. The player's answer matters. Not the specific words — the quality. Are they telling the truth? Are they asking something rather than wanting something?

**The person they might be:**

A community medic who joined late, has no prior history with the community, and is professionally unable to close off entirely from the outside world — people outside need medicine too. They've been doing small unauthorized exchanges for weeks. They know it's a violation of the community's current rules and they haven't stopped.

An original community member who was part of the decision to close the gate — who voted for Stage 2, Stage 3, who held the line when someone challenged Stage 4 — and who is starting to understand what they voted for. They are not guilty. They made the calculation that seemed correct. The calculation is starting to feel different.

A young adult who grew up in the community — one of the post-Cascade children who has known nothing else — who has been reaching the age of assessment and whose assessment of the community they grew up in is coming up with results that don't match what the community says it is.

Each edge case type has a different conversation, a different need, a different way they can help — and a different cost to themselves for helping.

**What helping looks like:**

The edge case is not a spy. They're not going to feed the player information or undermine the community's defenses. What they can do is carry a communication into the community that wouldn't otherwise reach it — not a message, exactly. A question. A piece of information about someone outside the perimeter that a specific person inside has been wondering about. A piece of evidence about what happened to a survivor who the community condemned but who actually made it.

Small things. The kind of thing that creates doubt without announcing it.

## Stage 3 — The Internal Fracture

**"The gate closed because someone decided it should. Not everyone agreed."**

Gone Dark communities are not monolithic inside. The slide happened because someone consolidated the decision-making, and the consolidation is never complete. The people who agreed least completely are the most recoverable — and they still exist in late Gone Dark, because the community never fully cleared out the people who remembered when they weren't this.

The player, operating through the edge case, identifies the fracture line.

**What the fracture looks like:**

*Generational:* Original community members who remember the Before — who built this community in the early months, who made it what it was before the slide — carry a specific weight that the people who joined later don't have. They remember when the gate was open. Some of them voted to close it and now live with that. Some of them voted against and were overruled. The generational line between original members and later joiners runs through the community's internal politics in ways that the leader has been managing since Stage 3 of the slide.

*Practical:* A Gone Dark community's total closure is also a total self-reliance. Some things can't be acquired internally. The community's practical needs — medicine, specific parts, seed stock, knowledge — don't stop because the gate is closed. Someone has been managing those needs in ways that violate the official position. That person knows the official position is operationally incomplete. They may not have said so out loud.

*Personal:* There are people inside the perimeter who have someone outside. A family member who left before the gate closed. A friend from before. Someone they know is out there, alive, and whom the current rules prevent them from contacting. These personal threads run through every closed community. They are the most emotionally volatile fracture line — and the most powerful lever.

**The player's intervention:**

The player uses the fracture line — whichever one is accessible given their build and faction relationships — to create a specific internal conversation that the player is not present for. They set something in motion and then step back.

This requires:
- Knowing enough about the community's internal dynamics to target correctly
- Having something real to offer — not a bribe, but information or material that addresses an actual need
- Trusting that the edge case will carry it correctly and being right about that

The player doesn't control what happens next. The fracture line either opens or it doesn't.

If it opens: a second person inside the community, with more standing than the edge case, begins to push for a re-evaluation. Not of the gate policy — that's too confrontational yet. Of a specific decision. A specific person who was expelled or refused. A specific incident that is remembered differently by different people inside.

If it doesn't: the questline doesn't end. The player tries a different fracture line. Or they wait — the internal pressure accumulates on its own over time, and the player's earlier interventions created conditions, even if the immediate push failed. The questline timeline is measured in weeks of in-game time.

## Stage 4 — The Leader

**"They believe they're protecting their people. They're not wrong that the world is dangerous. They became the thing they were protecting against."**

The leader of a Stage 4 Gone Dark community is not a monster. This is important to understand before any contact.

They made a series of decisions that were defensible — each one looked reasonable, locally, at the time it was made. A loss. A threat. A vote. Another loss. Another response. The community's identity increasingly organized around the leader's position because the leader was the one holding things together when other options collapsed. By Stage 4, the leader is not dictating — the community is sustaining the leader's position because it has forgotten how to sustain itself without it.

The leader, in most cases, knows something is wrong. They can see the cost — the people who left during the slide, the relationships that died, the look on the faces of community members who don't push back anymore. They have been living with this for months. They have not stopped because they don't know how to stop without what they've been holding together coming apart.

**The player's approach to the leader:**

Contact with the leader is possible before the internal fracture resolves — but it rarely works early. The leader has been managing external contact for months as a threat, and the threat frame is deep. A player who pushes direct contact before Stage 3 changes the internal environment will almost always trigger a hardening rather than an opening.

After Stage 3 creates internal movement: the leader is already feeling the pressure from inside. Someone has pushed back, gently. Something has shifted. They are, for the first time in months, in a position where the official position is being questioned by someone they trust.

The player can now appear — not at the gate, but somewhere the leader comes to. The night-exit route, if the leader uses it. A community elder who acts as an informal ambassador. A Signal contact who the leader has a prior relationship with and hasn't fully severed.

**The conversation:**

It is not a confrontation. The player is not there to defeat the leader's position or demonstrate their wrongness. They are there to give the leader something to hold onto that isn't the closed gate.

What the leader needs — always, in every community, shaped differently — is a way to change course that doesn't require them to admit the course was wrong. The cost of admission is too high. The community's identity, the leader's standing, and the narrative of every decision made during the slide are all at stake.

What the player offers is a re-framing that's available without loss of face: something outside changed, not something inside was wrong. A new threat that makes cooperation necessary. A specific resource or protection that only becomes available if the gate opens — even partially. A development in the main story's larger situation that makes the closed-gate position operationally incomplete in a way the leader can acknowledge.

The leader will not change overnight. They will test the re-framing. They will watch whether the player keeps their word about whatever made the opening plausible. They will allow a limited, controlled opening — something small — and observe the result.

**If the player keeps their word:**

The gate opens — not fully, not immediately, but by degrees. The community begins to re-extend. The leader remains, changed by increment rather than confrontation. The community is not what it was before the slide. It is something different and, eventually, functional.

The Warden who has been watching this: *"The Record for that community has a new entry. You're in it."*

**If the player doesn't keep their word:**

The gate closes harder. The leader's evidence that outside contact is dangerous is now confirmed — not by a Hollow raid, but by the person who came offering something and didn't deliver. Late Gone Dark recovery becomes significantly harder after a failed trust attempt. The player has made it worse.

## Stage 5 — After

**"They're not fixed. They're recoverable."**

A recovered Gone Dark community is not the community it was before the slide. The slide happened. The members lived through it. The decisions are in everyone's memory and most of them are in the Record.

What the community is, after: a Holdout that understands what it's capable of becoming. That knowledge is not comfortable. It is also not useless — the communities that have slid and recovered are often more alert to the early signs in themselves than communities that have only ever been healthy. They know Stage 1 from the inside.

**The community leader:** In recovery, most leaders either step back voluntarily or are eased out by a community that is ready to distribute what was concentrated. The step-back is almost always preferable — it allows the leader's contribution to the community's survival to be acknowledged alongside the cost. The Wardens have a specific Record category for this: *Service Under Duress, Completed.* It is not exoneration. It is a witness statement that what happened was complicated.

**The player's standing:** High. The community knows what happened and who was part of it. Trust capital available with this community is significant and built on something real — not faction affiliation, not resources, but the specific memory of someone who came when the gate was closed.

**The Signal:** If the Signal was involved in flagging the community, they will acknowledge the recovery in their network. This increases the player's Signal standing and — more importantly — their standing with other communities that receive Signal communications. The information that someone successfully worked a Stage 4 recovery spreads. Future Gone Dark situations will have NPCs who know this is possible because they heard about this one.

**The community children:** The post-Cascade children who grew up in the slide will carry it. Not as trauma alone — as education. Some of them are already asking the adults around them to explain what happened and why. Some of those explanations will be honest. Some of them will be the beginning of something better.

## Background-Specific Entries

**First Responder:** They have the most natural read on the community's internal state — they spent months before the Cascade building exactly this kind of community knowledge, and Gone Dark communities in stage four still have people who remember the First Responder's network. Some of them will recognize the background. That recognition creates an entry that other backgrounds can't access — not sympathy, but a prior relationship that predates the slide. *"I know who you are. You helped us before. That's different."*

**Homesteader:** Their relationship to land and community is legible to the people inside the perimeter. They understand what someone is protecting when they close a gate. They won't be treated as an outsider in the same way — a Homesteader reads as someone who has made the same calculation the community made, under different conditions, and found a different answer. The conversation is peer-to-peer in a way other backgrounds can't access as easily.

**Researcher:** They document the slide itself — the behavioral progression, the decision tree, the community's specific fracture lines. This documentation has value to the Signal, to the Wardens' Record, and to the community itself in recovery: the Researcher can give the community a clear account of what happened to them, which is something most communities in recovery desperately need and can't construct from inside. *"You want to know what it looked like from outside. I can show you."*

**Veteran:** They read the slide as a textbook case of community failure under sustained pressure — the Compact/community dependency failure mode they know from VSO. Their entry into the conversation is strategic but legible as something other than manipulation, because they understand why the community did what it did and they don't pretend otherwise. The leader will know, immediately, that this person has seen this before. That is unusual and valuable — most people who approach a Gone Dark community don't understand what they're looking at.

**Tradesperson:** They have a practical entry that other backgrounds don't: the community needs things it can't build. A Tradesperson who arrives not at the gate but at the informal contact point with specific items the community needs — not as a bribe, as a demonstration — changes the calculus. The community's practical needs are real, the Tradesperson can address them, and the leader's position depends on being able to tell the community that the gate policy works. It's harder to hold that position when a stranger just repaired something critical without asking anything in return.
