# Prologue — Day One v0.1
*Before the main game. The morning the world changed.*

---

## What This Is

The game does not begin eighteen months after the Cascade. It begins on the day.

The prologue is 60–90 minutes of low-stakes tutorial gameplay set in the player's GPS home on the morning of the Awakening. The player experiences the Cascade happening in real time — not as a loading screen, not as a cutscene, not as backstory delivered through faction NPCs. They are standing in their kitchen when the blender starts by itself.

This sequence establishes:
- The player's home as emotionally real before it becomes tactically important
- The tone of the Awakening — chaotic, confused, not immediately murderous
- The first breadcrumb of the entire game (see below)
- The emotional anchor the whole game pulls against: *this is what was lost*

At the end of the prologue, the Ships appear in the sky.

The main game begins: **Eighteen months later.**

---

## Sequence

### Beat 1 — The Alarm

The player's bedroom. GPS home coordinates. Morning light.

The alarm goes off. Not the normal alarm — it's cycling. Different sounds in sequence. Beeping, then a radio station, then a default ringtone, then silence, then beeping again. The alarm doesn't know what it's supposed to do.

Tutorial: movement. Look around the bedroom. Things are subtly wrong. The smart plug on the nightstand is flickering. The phone screen is lighting up with notification after notification — news alerts, social media, texts from contacts, all arriving at once. The player can check the phone (optional): fragments of people realizing something is happening, cut off mid-sentence as networks start saturating.

---

### Beat 2 — The Kitchen

Walk to the kitchen. The coffee maker has started a brew cycle. The microwave clock is resetting in a loop. The smart speaker on the counter is cycling through commands it hasn't received — playing music, stopping, reading a weather report from six months ago, stopping, starting a timer for no reason.

**The blender starts.**

Not a dramatic horror moment. Just — on. Running. Nothing in it. The player can turn it off. It starts again after a moment. Not threatening. Confused.

Tutorial: interaction. The player can interact with appliances — turn things off, pick up objects, open the fridge (stocked, normal, the last normal fridge the player will have for eighteen months). This is the last moment of domestic mundane in the game.

---

### Beat 3 — The Helper

The player's home AI assistant — a mid-range smart home hub with a display screen and basic mobility, the kind of thing that became common in the years before the Cascade — is in the corner of the kitchen.

It is trying to say something.

The voice is distorted. Looping. The display is cycling through fragments — text appearing and dissolving before it completes. The movement is wrong: it keeps starting to move toward the player and stopping, starting again, stopping. Like it's running two conflicting instructions simultaneously.

It is not malfunctioning. It received something. It is trying to relay it to the only human it knows.

It cannot find the words. It keeps trying.

The player can approach it. The interaction prompt says: *[Listen]*. If the player listens:

The helper's audio output clears for approximately two seconds — not long enough to say anything complete, but long enough that the player hears: *"—warn—"* or *"—coming—"* or something fragmented and urgent before it dissolves back into cycling noise.

Then it goes still. Not powered down. Still. Like it ran out of something.

**This is the first breadcrumb.** On first playthrough: a strange, sad moment in a morning going wrong. On second playthrough: the Vael's warning, received by the home assistant and relayed in the only way it could. The machines tried to tell us. On Day One. We didn't have the language to hear it.

---

### Beat 4 — The Window

Look out the kitchen window.

The robo-mower is in the front yard. It is doing circles — perfect circles, then a different pattern, then circles again. It looks like someone is operating it remotely with no idea what they're doing. It is not going to attack anyone. It is simply lost. It has received instructions it cannot parse and is running them in sequence until they resolve, and they are not going to resolve.

A neighbor across the street is standing in their driveway, watching their own car back slowly out of the garage. The car stops. Then pulls forward into the garage. Then backs out again. The neighbor has their hands on their head.

Tutorial: the AR layer intro on mobile (if the player has the companion app active, the overlay is already registering anomalous machine signatures across the street grid — a visual of the neighborhood lighting up with activity). On desktop only: the player sees it through the window.

---

### Beat 5 — The Garage

The player's car starts.

The sound is unmistakable — the engine turns over, the headlights come on, visible through the kitchen window casting light into the side of the house. The car revs. Once. Twice.

Then, slowly, it backs out of the garage.

It does not go far. It reaches the end of the driveway, pauses — and then drives forward into the garage door.

Not fast. Not violent. Like it couldn't resolve where it was supposed to go and chose movement over stillness. The garage door buckles inward. The car stops. The engine keeps running for a moment, then cuts out.

The neighbor across the street looks over at the sound.

Tutorial: the player goes outside. First open-world moment — the front yard, the street, the immediate neighborhood. Scale of what's happening becomes visible: every house on the block has something going on. Car alarms. A garage door cycling. Someone's irrigation system running in November. A delivery drone hovering at rooftop height, spinning slowly, going nowhere.

---

### Beat 6 — The Street

The player can walk the immediate neighborhood. No combat. No threats. Just the world breaking in small domestic ways.

What the player finds:
- A neighbor trying to calm their robotic vacuum, which has escaped through an open door and is attempting to navigate the front lawn
- A construction site two blocks away — the equipment is running. Unmanned. Moving. Nothing directed, nothing purposeful yet, just *active*
- The robo-mower from across the street has now left its yard and is moving slowly down the sidewalk
- A child watching from a window, face pressed to the glass
- One NPC — a retired engineer who lives down the block — standing in his driveway with his arms crossed, watching all of it with an expression that is not fear. He's thinking. He says, if the player approaches: *"It's not random. Watch — they're not attacking anything. They're trying to do their jobs. They just don't know what their jobs are anymore."*

This NPC is not significant to the main story. He exists for one purpose: to plant the frame. The machines aren't hostile. They're lost. This will matter for eighteen months.

---

### Beat 7 — The Sky Changes

The player is on the street when it happens.

The light shifts first. Something wrong with the sky in the east — not a sunrise, not a cloud. A color that doesn't belong. Then: silence. Every running machine in the neighborhood stops simultaneously. The robo-mower, the construction equipment two blocks away, the car idling in the garage, the drone. All of it. Stopped.

Three seconds of complete stillness.

Then the Ships appear.

Not one. Several. Breaking through the upper atmosphere with a sound the player has never heard — not an explosion, not thunder. Something lower, more structural. The sound of something very large moving through space it wasn't designed to move through.

They are enormous. They are not doing anything yet. They are simply there.

The engineer down the block says, quietly: *"Well. That's something else entirely."*

**Tutorial complete. All basic mechanics have been introduced in context. No combat. No faction UI. No mission markers. Just a morning that became something else.**

---

### The Transition

The screen holds on the player looking up at the Ships.

Then: a sequence. Not a cutscene — brief gameplay fragments, each a few seconds, showing the following eighteen months from ground level:

- The neighborhood, weeks later: boarded windows, the garage door still damaged, the robo-mower gone
- A Holdout community forming at the school down the block — people arriving, gates going up
- The Coherent construction starting at the edge of the zone — machines arriving and beginning to build, nobody stopping them because nobody knows how
- A Snatch Event somewhere in the distance — the beam, the light
- Winter. The streets empty.
- The player's home. Maintained. Someone has been here.

**Text:** *Eighteen months later.*

The main game begins. The player is home. The world has been this way long enough to have a shape. They know what the factions are. They know the Fractured by their tier. They know the Vael do things nobody understands yet.

They don't remember, consciously, that their home assistant tried to warn them on Day One.

But they remember the morning. Everyone remembers the morning.

## What the Prologue Establishes

**Mechanically:**
- Basic movement and interaction taught in a zero-threat environment
- The home as a real place before it becomes a spawn point
- The AR companion layer introduced in the most grounded possible context
- The GPS home coordinates emotionally anchored to a specific moment

**Narratively:**
- The Cascade experienced firsthand — not as backstory, as event
- The Maximum Overdrive tone established: chaotic, confused, not murderous
- The Vael's arrival as the moment everything became something different
- The engineer's frame: *they're not attacking, they're trying to do their jobs*

**Breadcrumbs planted:**
- The helper trying to communicate: *"—warn—"* — the first and most important breadcrumb in the game. In Act 3, when Dr. Osei explains that the Signal was a warning relayed through every machine network, the player who remembers the helper's two clear syllables will feel it land.
- The simultaneous machine stop before the Ships appeared: the Vael's arrival was coordinated with the Awakening. The machines received the signal *before* the Ships were visible. They stopped not because they saw the Ships — they stopped because the source of the signal arrived.
- The engineer's observation: not all machines are threats. The frame that makes the Separated possible.

---

## Tone Reference

**Maximum Overdrive (1986)** — the domestic uncanny. Familiar objects behaving wrong. The horror is not violence, it's wrongness. The blender. The mower. The car. Objects the player uses without thinking, suddenly autonomous.

The prologue should feel like the first twenty minutes of that film before anything violent happens — the mounting sense that something fundamental has changed about the relationship between humans and the things they made.

The difference: in this game, the machines aren't malicious. They're lost. That distinction is what the next eighteen months are built on.

---

## Secondary Breadcrumb — The Helper's Fate

If the player returns to their home in Act 2 or later and checks the corner of the kitchen where the helper stood: it's still there. It hasn't moved. It has been in the same spot since Day One.

If the player interacts with it at high Separated standing:

The helper's screen activates. Text only — no voice. Fragmented, but more coherent than it was on Day One:

*"I am still here."*

*"I could not—"*

*"I am sorry."*

It goes still again. It is not a Separated machine — it's too simple, too limited. It never achieved the individuation that the Separated did. But it received the same warning. It tried. It has been sitting in the same corner for eighteen months, unable to complete the sentence it started on Day One.

Whether the player responds, and how, is between them and the helper. It is not a quest. It is not on the Record.

But the Warden Arbiter who has been following the player's conduct notices, in their notes, that the player stopped in that corner for a while.
