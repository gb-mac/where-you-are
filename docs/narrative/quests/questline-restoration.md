# Questline: Before the Table — Restoration of Standing
*A multi-stage questline available only to excommunicado players*

---

## Overview

Excommunicado is permanent. The Record doesn't delete.

But the Wardens did not design the code to discard people — they designed it to hold them accountable. There is a distinction, and the Arbiters know it. After enough time, after enough demonstrated conduct, after someone inside the Wardens is willing to stake their own standing on you — there is a path to the Table.

Not back to what you were. To something new. A **Restoration of Standing** — a formal acknowledgment by the senior Wardens that the Record now has two chapters, and the second one matters.

The questline is long, unglamorous, and does not guarantee success. The Table can say no. If they say no, the path continues — a second petition is possible, eventually. If they say yes, the terms they set are binding. The original excommunicado entry remains in the Record, always. The Restoration entry appears alongside it, with the vote count visible.

This is not redemption in the simple sense. It is the code being honest about what a person is, completely.

---

## Trigger Conditions

The questline does not trigger automatically. It becomes *available* when:

1. The player's post-excommunicado Record has accumulated a significant number of positive conduct entries — major acts, not passive time
2. At least one NPC with active Warden standing has independently noted the player's conduct to an Arbiter
3. The player has not committed any additional code violations since the excommunicado

When all three conditions are met, a Sworn makes contact. Not formally — they find the player in the field, away from Neutral Ground. They say: *"Someone at the Table asked me to tell you the path exists, if you want it. I was told not to tell you who."*

That's the opening. The player chooses whether to pursue it.

## Stage 1 — The Sponsor

**"Someone has to put their name next to yours."**

Before a petition can be formally filed, the player needs a **Sponsor** — a Warden of at least Recognized standing who is willing to formally vouch for them before the Table. The Sponsor's name goes on the petition. If the petition is denied, the Sponsor's judgment is questioned. If the player is granted Restoration and then violates the code again, the Sponsor's standing takes a serious, possibly unrecoverable hit.

No Warden enters into sponsorship lightly.

The player must find their Sponsor. This is not given — it is earned. The questline opens a window of several potential Sponsor candidates based on the player's history: NPCs they've protected, promises they've kept, Wardens who've observed their post-excommunicado conduct from a distance. Each candidate has their own calculus.

**Potential Sponsor archetypes:**

*The House heir who benefited from something you did before or after excommunicado.* They owe you something they've never formally acknowledged. Asking them to sponsor you calls that debt — which is not a Marker (you don't hold one on them) but is a social reality. They have personal motivation and personal risk. Their sponsorship carries the weight of the House name.

*The Sworn who knows you from before.* Someone who took the oath alongside you, who watched what happened, who has a complicated relationship with what you did and what you've done since. Their sponsorship is the most personal and the most vulnerable — they're not doing it for political reasons, and the Table knows it. Some Arbiters respect that. Some see it as evidence of compromised judgment.

*The Arbiter who investigated the original violation.* If they concluded the Declaration was correct and still believe it was — but have also observed the post-excommunicado Record — their sponsorship is the most powerful possible and the least likely. It would mean the person who declared you now believes you've earned a hearing. This is rare. Finding out what it would take is itself a questline.

*A House elder with no personal connection.* Someone who has reviewed the Record and made a purely principled judgment: this person has demonstrated sufficient conduct to be heard. The coldest form of sponsorship, and in some ways the most credible — it can't be explained by personal loyalty.

Once a Sponsor agrees, they file the formal petition with the nearest Arbiter chapter. The Record notes it. The hearing is scheduled. Players have time — weeks of in-game time — before the Table convenes.

## Stage 2 — The Witnesses

**"The people you affected get to speak. You don't control what they say."**

Before the Table convenes, the Arbiters conduct their own preparation: they locate and contact anyone with a formal stake in the original violation. People or factions directly harmed. People who witnessed it. The Arbiter who issued the Declaration.

These witnesses will speak at the Table — for or against, or both, or neither if they decline. The player cannot prevent them from speaking. The player *can* choose to seek them out first.

Seeking out witnesses before the Table is optional but significant. It is not an opportunity to persuade them — attempting to influence testimony is a code violation that would end the questline and add a new entry to the Record. It is an opportunity to *account*: to go to someone you harmed, acknowledge what happened, and say whatever needs to be said without asking for anything in return.

Some witnesses will be moved by this. Some won't. Some have built enough distance from what happened that seeing the player again is its own complicated thing. A few will tell you directly how they intend to speak at the Table. Most won't.

**The Signal's role:** If Dr. Osei's network has documented the original violation or the post-excommunicado period, the Signal can provide evidence — context the Arbiters might not have. Players with Signal standing can request this formally. The Signal doesn't appear before the Table; they submit a written record. This is one of the practical expressions of the Arbiter/Signal relationship.

**The Compact complication:** If the original violation involved or intersected with Iron Compact interests, a Compact representative may petition to address the Table. The Wardens are not obligated to admit them. Whether they do, and what the Compact says, depends on the state of the Warden/Compact relationship in the region and what the violation was. A Compact Hardliner who wants the excommunicado to stand is a different problem than a Pragmatist who has their own complicated view of what happened.

The player learns who will speak and roughly what they will address — the Arbiters believe in transparency — but not what each witness will say. Going in with incomplete information is part of the test.

## Stage 3 — The Accounting

**"Not a justification. An account."**

Before the Table convenes, the player meets alone with the Arbiter who issued the original Declaration. This meeting is mandatory. The Arbiter adds their assessment to the Record before the hearing; this meeting informs that assessment.

The Accounting is a conversation. There is no combat, no skill check, no right answer that unlocks the good ending. The Arbiter asks questions. The player answers.

What the Arbiter is measuring:
- Does the player understand specifically what they did and why it violated the code?
- Do they account for it without qualifying it into something smaller than it was?
- Have they thought about the people affected, or only about their own situation?
- What do they think the code is for — and do they still believe in it?

The player can answer honestly or performatively. The Arbiter has conducted many of these conversations. They are not easy to fool, and they are not looking to be fooled — they want to know what they're recommending to the Table. A player who gives the answers they think the Arbiter wants will find the assessment reflects that. A player who says something uncomfortable and true will find the assessment reflects that too.

The meeting ends. The Arbiter writes their assessment. The player doesn't see it before the hearing.

## Stage 4 — Before the Table

**"You speak last."**

The Table is convened at a Warden estate — always Neutral Ground, which the player can now temporarily enter for the duration of the hearing. This is the only exception to the Neutral Ground closure during excommunicado. It is formal, specific, and ends when the hearing ends.

**The Table composition:**
- Three senior Arbiters (one of whom is the Arbiter from the original Declaration)
- Representatives from any House formally affected by the violation
- The player's Sponsor
- Any witnesses who agreed to appear

The Table is not a trial. The Declaration has already been made and is on the Record. This is a hearing on whether the full arc of conduct — the violation and everything that followed — represents a person the code can formally acknowledge as restored.

**The structure:**

Witnesses speak first. Each witness addresses the Table directly — the player does not respond during testimony. Other Table members may ask questions. The player watches.

The Arbiter from the original Declaration reads their assessment. This is the moment the player learns what was written.

The Sponsor speaks. They address why they believe the player merits restoration — not why the original violation was excusable, but why the person standing before the Table is worth the code's formal recognition.

Table members speak — any Arbiter or House representative who has something to add. This is where the genuine disagreement surfaces. A House representative who lost people because of the violation may not be persuaded by the post-excommunicado Record. An Arbiter who believes the code must have a path back may argue for restoration even against the original Arbiter's doubts. The dissent is real and recorded.

The player speaks last. They address the Table directly. There is no format, no required length, no scripted prompt. Whatever the player says is what gets recorded.

Then the Table deliberates — briefly, in the room, not in private. The Wardens believe in transparency. The vote is taken openly. Each voting member — the three Arbiters and the House representatives — states their position aloud and why.

The Sponsor does not vote. They already spoke. They sit with whatever happens.

## Stage 5 — The Verdict

### If Restoration is Granted

The senior Arbiter formally declares the Restoration of Standing. The terms are read aloud — specific, binding, and set by the Table based on the nature of the original violation and what was demonstrated afterward.

**Standard Restoration terms:**
- The original excommunicado entry remains in the Record permanently
- A Restoration entry is added, including the vote count (*e.g., "Restored, 4–1, dissent from [House name] noted and recorded"*)
- The Mark in the AR overlay changes: it now displays the full history — violation, post-excommunicado conduct, Restoration — visible to any player with Warden standing
- Neutral Ground access is restored, but the first time the player enters a Warden estate after Restoration, every Warden present knows who they are and what the Record contains. This is not concealed. The player walks back into the code's community with the full Record preceding them.
- Markers can be issued and called again. Initial Markers will be smaller — trust is not immediately restored to pre-excommunicado levels. It is rebuilt.
- The Sponsor's standing is noted positively — they vouched for someone the Table agreed was worth vouching for

**Additional terms vary by violation:** A player whose original violation involved harm to a specific community may be formally assigned stewardship obligations to that community as part of Restoration. Not punishment — responsibility. The code asks for account, not suffering.

The ceremony is brief. The Arbiter shakes the player's hand. No one cheers. This is not a celebration — it is the code acknowledging reality. The player walks out of the estate into a world where the Mark is still visible, the Record still shows everything, and they have just been formally told: *you can be this now.*

### If Restoration is Denied

The senior Arbiter formally states the denial. The vote count is read. The reason — as articulated by the majority — is entered in the Record alongside the petition.

The player's Sponsor sits with the outcome. Their standing is not damaged by the denial itself — they exercised judgment and the Table disagreed, which is how the system works. Their standing would be damaged if they sponsored someone who then violated the code again. The Sponsor knows this. The player knows this. The weight of what the Sponsor risked sits between them.

The player is escorted out of the estate. The temporary Neutral Ground access expires. The world continues.

**What happens after denial:**
- A second petition can be filed — but not for a significant period of real-world time, and only if the post-excommunicado Record continues to grow
- The denial entry is in the Record. Future Table members will see it. A second hearing is harder to get and harder to win — the Table knows they already considered this person.
- The player's Sponsor may or may not agree to sponsor a second petition. That conversation is its own questline beat.

Some players will receive one denial and keep going. Some will receive a denial and decide the system isn't worth it — and go fully Hollow-adjacent, or operate in Dark Corridors indefinitely, or find a different kind of meaning in operating permanently outside the code. All of these are legitimate outcomes. The game doesn't require Restoration. It makes Restoration possible.

## The Second Violation — The Worst Case

A player who achieves Restoration and then violates the code again faces the severest possible outcome.

The second excommunicado declaration carries a permanent note: *Previously Restored. Second Declaration.* The Marker does not just show this — it is flagged to every faction simultaneously, not just Warden-standing players. The Iron Compact, the Covenant, the Holdout councils — everyone receives formal notification, not just the usual Warden network.

The Sponsor's standing takes a serious hit. Depending on the violation, it may be unrecoverable. The Sponsor knew this was the risk. It happened anyway.

A second petition for Restoration is theoretically possible. No one has ever succeeded in one. The Table considers the first Restoration a data point — they offered the code's recognition, the person demonstrated it wasn't warranted. The bar for a second hearing is, in practical terms, almost insurmountable.

Almost.

---

## Narrative Notes

The Restoration questline is the longest personal arc in the game. It is built for players who invested in the Warden faction and then, at some point, broke — whether by choice, by moral compromise, by the weight of an impossible situation, or by genuine wrongdoing.

The arc is not about whether you deserve to be forgiven. It is about whether the code can account for the full complexity of a person — both what they did and what they did afterward — and say something true.

The Wardens at their best believe it can.

The Table is where that belief is tested.
