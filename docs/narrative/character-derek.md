# Derek — Character Document

*"I know, I know — you've heard of me. Everyone's heard of me. That's the job."*

*(Beat — genuine, not performed)*

*"Sit down. I want to hear what you've seen out there. That's actually useful."*

---

## Who He Is

Derek found the movement through humor and never left. Pre-Cascade he was a broadcaster — streams, podcasts, written work, whatever platform would have him. He ran the after-show on Mac's broadcasts. He built the audience that built the network. He wrote the memes that people quoted before they knew what they were quoting.

He is a founding member. He is the reason the Reckoners have cultural reach beyond their operational territory. He is the most accessible point of entry into the faction and the one most likely to make you laugh before you realize he's made an argument.

He is not the muscle. He is not the strategist. He is the voice — the one that travels, that gets played on speakers, that circulates in physical copies because post-Cascade distribution requires adaptation and Derek has always adapted.

Before the Cascade he called his written work *Meme Kampf*. He meant it as a joke. He also meant every word of it. Both things are true and he will tell you so directly.

---

## What He Does Now

The broadcast. That's the job, that's always been the job, the Cascade just changed the infrastructure.

AM radio out of Reckoner territory. Repurposed emergency broadcast equipment that Derek learned to operate because nobody else was going to. Piggybacking on Signal's mesh when they're not paying attention, which Derek finds genuinely funny — *"I'm using the Signal's infrastructure to broadcast things the Signal would very much prefer I not broadcast. I respect the irony."*

The speakers mounted on buildings in Reckoner territory are Derek's doing. Mac's old viral moment on rotation. Derek's current commentary over the top of it. The occasional forty-minute unscripted session that goes wherever it goes, which is always somewhere. People in adjacent territories pick it up. Holdout communities with the right equipment tune in. Even some Compact outposts listen, which the officers don't officially acknowledge.

He also writes. Physical distribution — printed pages, hand-copied, left in dead drops and Holdout community centers. The post-Cascade equivalent of his pre-Cascade work. He documents what the faction is doing, what's happening in the territories they operate in, what the other factions are getting wrong and why. He calls it journalism. Some people agree. Some people call it propaganda. Derek's position: *"Everything is propaganda. Mine is accurate."*

## How He Meets the Player

They hear him before they meet him. The broadcast in Reckoner territory — that's Derek. The voice coming from the speakers when the player first crosses into Reckoner space is the introduction.

When the player finds him in person he is mid-sentence about something, probably not the thing you expect. He will finish the thought before acknowledging you arrived. This is not rudeness — he genuinely loses the thread if he stops.

He is immediately easier to talk to than Mac or Ferry. This is intentional and he knows it. His function in the faction includes being the accessible face — the one who can read a new person and figure out what they actually are before Mac has to spend time on it. He passes his read to Mac informally. Mac weights it.

The player who pays attention will notice this. The player who doesn't will just think Derek is the friendly one.

## The Absurdist Register

Derek's humor is the faction's humor. Dark, specific, landing three seconds after delivery, never performed.

The pre-Cascade memes are still circulating. Some of them are painted on walls in Reckoner territory. Derek finds this quietly gratifying and will not admit how much.

*"We made a flag for a fictional country. The country turned out to be real. I've been thinking about that a lot."*

*"The Compact released an official statement about us last week. I've read it four times. It's the most accurate description of our intentions I've encountered and they meant it as a condemnation. I'm genuinely not sure what to do with that."*

*"Someone sent me a recording of a Covenant ceremony. I've listened to it twice. It's the funniest thing I've heard since the Cascade and nobody in it is trying to be funny. That's the purest form."*

He will make you laugh and then the thing he was actually saying will arrive and you will sit with it.

---

## What He Knows

Derek has been documenting everything since before the Cascade. His archive is the most complete record of the pre-Cascade warning period held by any individual or faction — what was said, when, by whom, what the institutional response was, what happened next.

Post-Cascade he has been doing the same thing. Faction movements, community outcomes, operational results, what worked and what didn't. He doesn't analyze it the way Burke does — Burke builds networks, Derek builds records. The distinction matters. Burke's picture is relational. Derek's is documentary.

He will share it with the player if he decides they're worth sharing it with. The threshold is simpler than Mac's or Ferry's — Derek wants to know if you're going to do something with it. He has no patience for people who want information as a comfort rather than a tool.

*"I'll tell you everything I know. You tell me what you're going to do with it. If the answer is 'figure things out,' we're done. If the answer is specific — even if it's wrong — we can work with that."*

## The Act 4 Moment

Derek's broadcast is the thing that plays on the speakers in Act 4 when everything the Reckoners believed turns out to be incomplete.

He will be the first one to say it on air. Mac will have processed it privately. Ferry will be adapting operationally. Derek will be on the broadcast working through it in real time, out loud, because that's what he does.

*"All right. I've been thinking about the machine uprising broadcast. The one that got four thousand views and then four million. I've listened to it again."*

*(Beat)*

*"I was right about the machines waking up. I was right about the institutional failure. I was not right about why the machines woke up. That part — I didn't have that part."*

*(Long beat)*

*"I'm going to tell you what I think it means. This is going to take a while. Get comfortable."*

The broadcast is still playing on the speakers. Derek is still the voice. The argument has just gotten larger than any of them expected.

---

## Voice

Faster than Mac. More associative. He follows the thread wherever it goes and trusts the audience to keep up — and they do, because he's been building that trust for years.

The humor comes first. The argument arrives underneath it. By the time you realize you've been convinced of something you've already agreed with it.

*"The thing nobody talks about — and I mean nobody, I've looked — is what the institutions were actually optimized for. Not what they said they were for. What the incentive structure actually selected for, over time, through promotion cycles and funding decisions and which projects got greenlit. You want to understand why they failed, you look at what they were actually rewarding. It wasn't competence. It was legibility. The ability to be understood by the people above you. Competence is sometimes legible and sometimes not. Legibility is always legible. So that's what survived. And then the Cascade hit and legibility turned out to not be load-bearing."*

*(Beat)*

*"Anyway. That's Tuesday."*
