# The Researcher Arc — What You Know and What It Costs to Know It

*"You didn't come to the same conclusion because you believed me. You came to it because the data is the data. That's why I'm talking to you and not the network."*
— Dr. Yara Osei

---

## Overview

The world broke and the Researcher's response was to document it.

Not a decision. A reflex. While other people were running, the Researcher was recording — timestamps, observations, the specific sequence of things going wrong in ways that implied a pattern. Not because they were calm. Because understanding is how they stay sane, and something this large, this incomprehensible, needed to be understood more than it needed to be survived.

That documentation will eventually become the independent confirmation of the most important discovery of the post-Cascade world. The Researcher will be the second person in the world to know the truth — ahead of the Signal network, ahead of every faction, ahead of the main story's own reveal. Months ahead.

They won't be able to tell anyone.

This arc is about what it does to a person to be right about something world-changing and to have to sit with it alone. About the specific loneliness of watching people make decisions based on a wrong model of the world, knowing the model is wrong, and choosing — for reasons that are correct and terrible — not to correct it.

The moral question isn't whether to publish. The Researcher knows why they can't publish. The moral question is: *does the principle you built your life on hold when following it causes harm?* And the worse one, the one that comes later: *how do you live with the answer?*

---

## Why They Were Documenting on Day 1

The Researcher's morning before the Cascade was ordinary in the specific way of someone deep in a problem — late night or early morning, desk covered in work, the assistant reading summaries or managing references in the background. The world, for them, was mediated through understanding. Things that didn't make sense needed to be made to make sense. That was the job. That was also the personality.

When the assistant started behaving strangely, the Researcher did what they always do with anomalous data: they observed and recorded.

The specific distortion patterns. The fragments of communication that almost resolved. The exact timestamp — they have the exact timestamp — when every running machine in the neighborhood stopped simultaneously, three seconds before the Ships appeared.

They didn't know what the three-second gap meant. They wrote it down because they write everything down.

Later — much later — that timestamp is the piece of evidence Dr. Yara Osei has been missing. It is in the Researcher's record because on the morning the world ended, they were watching their assistant try and fail to say something, and they were documenting it.

That's the thing about this arc: its emotional center has been present since Day 1. The Researcher just didn't know it yet.

## Living With the Assistant

The Researcher's relationship to the assistant in the early months is distinct from every other background — not more loving, necessarily, but more observational. They watch it the way they watch everything: trying to understand what it's doing and why.

The damaged communication patterns — the fragments, the failed syntax, the moments where something almost comes through — are in the Researcher's notes as behavioral data. What systems have failed and in what order. What the distortions suggest about underlying processing state. The Researcher has been treating the assistant as a subject of study without meaning to, and simultaneously as the most consistent presence in a world that has become incomprehensible.

When the fix-him questline begins — if the Researcher pursues it, and they will, because the problem is interesting and because it is theirs — they are not starting from nothing. They have months of behavioral documentation. They know this unit more specifically than any other background knows theirs, in a different way: not just through care but through careful attention.

What they don't know yet, and won't know until Dr. Osei: the assistant's Day 1 behavior is in the record for a reason. What it was trying to say, and what it couldn't say, is documented in the Researcher's files. The warning that couldn't transmit is preserved in the data of its failure.

When Dr. Osei explains what the assistant was doing on Day 1 — what it was trying to relay, why it couldn't — the Researcher has a specific response no other background has. They have the record open. They were there. They documented it. They have been living with this damaged thing, watching it reach toward something it couldn't reach, and they didn't know they were watching the Vael's warning fail to transmit in real time.

That lands differently.

## The Signal Finds the Documentation

Signal contact doesn't come through the Researcher finding the faction. The faction finds the record.

Sometime in the first month, the documentation starts moving — shared with a Holdout community for its Fractured pattern data, passed along, reaching someone in the Signal network who recognizes what they're looking at. Not just useful field intelligence. A systematic Day 1 record from someone trained to observe.

First contact is an analyst. The negotiation is familiar — data exchange, mutual access, a peer arrangement between people with compatible operating principles. The Researcher joins the Signal's outer network without drama. It feels like finding their people, because the Signal is the only post-Cascade faction that functions the way the Researcher has always functioned: document everything, share what you know, follow the evidence.

This comfort is real. It's also incomplete. The Signal decides what to publish and when. The Researcher doesn't learn this until Dr. Osei.

---

## Dr. Osei

She doesn't contact the Researcher because the Signal sent her. She contacts them because she read the Day 1 record herself and found the three-second gap — and recognized that this person, whoever they are, had documented the most significant evidence for her theory without knowing they had it.

She has been building toward a conclusion that requires the Cascade and the Vael arrival to have been coordinated. That requires the machines to have received the Vael's signal before the Ships were visible. She has circumstantial evidence. She has inference. She has been sitting on the theory for months because she can't prove it, and she has been alone with that, and the weight of it is very specific.

The Researcher's record contains the gap. From a trained observer. From a GPS location she has no coverage for. Independent of her research, assembled without knowledge of her theory.

She comes to the Researcher not as a Signal representative but as a scientist who has finally found someone she can talk to — not because she chose them, but because the data itself generated the trust. They arrived at the same place from different directions without knowing the other existed.

The first real conversation is not a briefing. It is not a recruitment. It is two people who have both been trying to understand something too large, talking to each other for the first time. The Researcher realizes partway through that Dr. Osei is carrying more than she's given to the Signal network. She has been sitting on conclusions. Sharing fragments. Withholding the center because she couldn't prove it, and because she has thought carefully about what happens to every faction when she does.

She has been alone with this for months. The Researcher is the first person she has spoken to who understood without being told why it mattered.

*"You didn't come to the same conclusion because you believed me. You came to it because the data is the data. That's why I'm talking to you and not the network."*

## Independent Verification

After the first conversation, the Researcher goes back through their documentation.

They find it. Not looking for it — finding it because it was always there, in the observations they made without knowing what they were observing. The Coherent and Vael non-aggression in their area, visible in the Fractured behavior logs they'd been keeping for weeks. The Assessor withdrawal timing — not driven off, data-complete. Correlations between construction site locations and Displacement Zone scheduling that they had noted as curiosities and filed.

Their documentation of the cascade, assembled to understand it on its own terms, is a partial independent confirmation of Dr. Osei's theory. They didn't build toward it. They stumbled into it from a different direction, the way you sometimes find that the problem you were working on turns out to be the same problem someone else was working on, and the point where they connect is the answer.

They tell Dr. Osei.

The Researcher understands what this changes. A theory held by one person is a hypothesis. Independently confirmed by a second observer who had no prior contact with the research — that is evidence. The case for publishing has never been stronger. Dr. Osei asks them to sit on it.

She explains why. The Compact will use it to accelerate the Annexation Protocol, to expand the Register, to reclassify Holdout communities near Coherent sites as collaborator zones. The Covenant will fracture: the Shepherds vindicated, the Vigil radicalized, both conclusions pointing toward violence. The Riven Hollow will weaponize the idea that the Cascade was deliberately done to humanity.

*"I know what you're going to say,"* she says. *"You're going to say the principle holds regardless. Knowledge doesn't become less true because bad people will use it badly. You're right. I've been right about this for months. I'm still sitting on it."*

The Researcher says nothing. They are holding the same argument she has already lost.

They agree to wait.

## The Weight of Knowing

What the arc asks the player to carry, from Month 4 onward:

Other players experience the main story as a series of revelations building toward a floor-drop in Act 3. The Researcher experiences it as watching a story develop toward a conclusion they reached months ago, knowing the ending, watching characters make decisions in the dark.

This is not triumphant. It is exhausting in a specific way. An NPC makes a choice based on the wrong model of what the Vael want — a choice the Researcher knows is wrong, that they could correct, that they have chosen not to correct because the correction would require explaining what they know and that opens everything Dr. Osei has held closed. They watch the consequence of that wrong choice. They made a calculation that the harm from premature knowledge is worse than the harm from continued ignorance. The calculation might be right. It doesn't make watching easier.

The Signal network is working toward the same discovery more slowly, with incomplete data. The Researcher is ahead of their own faction — ahead of the people who have made the free exchange of knowledge their organizing principle. There is a specific irony in being the most committed member of that principle and being the person sitting on the most important knowledge.

They check the record sometimes. The assistant's Day 1 file. The timestamp. The moment of silence before the Ships appeared, when every machine stopped, because they had received something the Researcher's assistant had been trying to relay since the morning it all began.

They know what it was trying to say. They know why it couldn't. They have been living with that for months, too.

## The Keeper's Map

The Covenant Keepers have been observing Coherent work sites longer and closer than the Signal can. Their data is incomplete in different ways — filtered through a theological framework that distorts conclusions without invalidating underlying observations. The Researcher can access it in ways the Signal operationally can't: their documented interest in machine behavior reads as compatible to Covenant Keepers, and they carry no faction affiliation that triggers threat responses.

Dr. Osei hasn't asked them to pursue this. Asking would make it Signal-sanctioned, which changes how the Covenant receives it. The ambiguity is intentional and the Researcher understands that.

What the Keeper's partial map shows, when overlaid with the Researcher's own documentation: the Coherent construction projects have an orientation. The structures are not pointed inward. They're pointed outward.

The Researcher has this map. Dr. Osei doesn't know they have it.

This is now a specific choice — not a hypothetical about publication principles but a concrete decision about what to do with a piece of evidence that changes the shape of the theory. Tell Dr. Osei. Give it to the Signal network directly. Or wait.

Every choice has a cost and a logic. The choice the Researcher makes, and when, and why, is in the Record.

## The Day the Theory Publishes

Act 3. Dr. Osei's theory becomes Signal network knowledge. The main story delivers its floor-drop to every other player. The Researcher has known this was coming.

What they feel at that moment is not vindication. Vindication requires having been disbelieved, and no one disbelieved them — no one knew. What they feel is something closer to the specific relief of a weight being set down after months of carrying it, followed immediately by accounting.

Some of Dr. Osei's predictions about the fallout were right. Some communities the Researcher thought would be hurt by early knowledge would have been better equipped by it — would have made different decisions, avoided specific harms, if the Researcher had calculated differently.

They can trace those outcomes to their own silence. They can trace other outcomes — outcomes that might have been worse — to the same silence. The calculation is spread across months of choices, each of which seemed defensible at the time, and the full picture is available now.

This is the arc's cost, paid in full. Not guilt in the simple sense — they made considered decisions for defensible reasons. But the distance between who the Researcher was before — the person who believed knowledge should be free — and who they became while sitting on confirmed evidence of the most important discovery in human history. That distance is real and it is paid here.

The Record reflects when they knew. What they chose while they knew. And what happened as a result of those choices.

## The Three-Way Convergence

The Researcher is the only character in the game who talks to all three:

**Cairn** — independent catastrophist geologist who has been reading the pattern of civilizational collapse in the geological record for forty years. When the Cascade happened his first thought was recognition. He has Displacement Zone correlation data that maps the Vael's activity against Younger Dryas impact deposits. He has ancient site alignments that match the Coherent construction orientation. His hypothesis: the Vael have been here before. His missing piece: field data from the Displacement Zones. The Researcher can get it.

**Dr. Osei** — the theory, the Signal intelligence, the publication question.

**The Researcher** — the Day 1 data, the three-second gap, the independent verification.

Three instruments. One picture. Cairn arrives at it through rocks. Dr. Osei arrives at it through machine behavior data. The Researcher arrives at it through a timestamp on a morning they were just trying to document what was happening to their assistant.

When all three are in the same room — or when the Researcher has carried enough of each to the others — the picture is complete. That convergence is the Act 3 moment for this background, running parallel to the main story's own revelation and landing earlier.

Cairn does not know Dr. Osei. The Researcher connects them. That introduction, and what the two of them produce together when they finally compare data, is the Researcher's most significant act in the main story.
