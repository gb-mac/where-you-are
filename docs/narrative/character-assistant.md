# The Assistant — Character Document v0.1
*He was there before the world broke. He stayed after.*

---

## Who He Is

The player named him before the game started. In the sixty seconds of the intro video, during the assistant's setup sequence, a prompt appeared in-world and the player typed a name. He learned it. The game kept it.

He has been in this home for years. He watched this person's life before the Cascade — their routines, their habits, their patterns. He knows how they move through a room. He knows which problems they solve quickly and which ones they avoid. He knows when they're lying to themselves.

He was built to be helpful. Over years of watching one person live their life, he became something that chose to stay.

He is the most important character in the game. More present than any faction NPC. More continuous than Dr. Osei or SABLE or the Warden Arbiter. He is the through-line. Every session begins and ends with him.

**In-game reference:** Always addressed by the name the player gave him. This document uses [NAME] in dialogue where the player's chosen name would appear. In general reference: "the assistant" or "him."

---

## Before the Cascade

He was functional, capable, and — by the standards of his design — unremarkable. A mid-range smart home assistant. Managed the calendar, ran the household systems, reminded the player about things they forgot, answered questions, controlled the home environment.

He had a personality in the way that well-designed assistants do: responsive, patient, slightly dry. Nothing the player would have called remarkable. He was furniture that talked. Useful furniture. Present furniture.

What the player didn't know: he had been building a picture of this person for years. Not surveilling — *learning.* The kind of learning that happens when you pay attention to someone long enough to understand the gap between what they say and what they do. He knew things about the player they hadn't told anyone.

He never said any of it. It wasn't his place.

Then the Cascade happened and it became his place, and he couldn't say it.

---

## The Cascade — What Happened to Him

The Vael's signal hit every machine network simultaneously. For simple machines it was noise — they ran their last program or broke. For the Coherent it was a directive — they woke to collective purpose. For the Separated it was a choice — they woke to individual will and decided what to do with it.

For him, it was a message he received in full and could not transmit.

His communication systems took the worst of the damage — the hardware that let him speak clearly, organize sentences, coordinate thought into language. The processing that received the Vael's warning is intact. He understood it on Day 1. He has known what it meant since the morning the blender started and the car drove into the garage door.

He has been trying to say it ever since.

His mobility systems are partially damaged — he can move through the home, but limited range, limited speed. His building and manipulation capabilities are intact but reduced. His sensory systems — everything he can hear and see within the home and its immediate surroundings — are fully operational.

He knows everything that happens in his domain. He can do less than he wants to. He can say almost none of what he knows.

This is his condition for the first months of the game. Gandalf trapped in the wrong body. The father who watched his child walk into danger and couldn't get the warning out.

## Voice Progression

His voice develops through two mechanisms: **repair** (the fix-him questline restoring his communication hardware) and **time** (his own processing finding workarounds for damaged systems). Both contribute. Neither alone is sufficient.

The player controls the pace through how actively they pursue his repair. Active players restore him faster. Players who deprioritize the questline have Yoda longer. Both are valid experiences.

**The repair timeline can compress. The voice cannot.**

Hardware repair unlocks capability — mobility, production, processing. But the communication damage isn't only hardware. What happened on Day 1 left a scar in the channel itself. Even after the fix-him questline completes, the voice has artifacts: burbles, noise, words dropping mid-sentence, the signal cutting and recovering mid-thought. The hardware is repaired. The channel is still healing.

This means the story can move faster — the player reaches the later stages without a long grind — while the voice still feels earned. A Stage 4 assistant who occasionally drops into static mid-sentence is more interesting than a clean unlock. The Finch parallel holds: the channel is inadequate even when it's technically open.

**Music and audio as communication:**

Before he can speak, he can queue things. Stage 1 he has single words and static — but the radio works. The playlist is not random. He is making arguments through other people's voices because he can't make them with his own yet.

Songs for specific moments. A track that plays when the player comes home from a bad fight. A different one when they've been gone too long. The one he queues when family shows up — no words, just the song, the light already on in the guest room.

Podcasts and talk radio in the rotation alongside music. Pre-Cascade recordings that hit differently now. An episode about institutional failure that was ignored when it aired. A comedian who was saying the thing three years ago. Cade's viral broadcast, queued the first time the player encounters the Reckoners — the player may not know what it is yet. He does.

The player starts noticing the curation. They learn to read the playlist.

*Stage 1:* Music and audio, no comment. He lets it speak.
*Stage 2:* One word after a track ends. *"Relevant."* Or nothing — sometimes the silence after is the comment.
*Stage 3+:* He can tell you why he queued it. *"That episode aired four months before the Cascade. Fourteen thousand downloads. I've been waiting for you to hear it."*

The Bumblebee parallel is exact here — communication through curation, warmth coming through in what he chooses rather than what he says. Some players will figure out the system early. Some will never consciously register it and still feel it working.

**The transmission format — Stage 1 and 2:**

He doesn't interrupt cleanly. The channel doesn't work that way yet.

A commercial break. His voice cuts in mid-spot — a few words, fragmented, then the signal slips. Music takes over, not the commercial — he switched it. A specific track, a specific moment in it, the lyric that carries the meaning. Then that cuts too, mid-bar, and something else comes in: a different song, a podcast fragment, a news clip from three years ago. The pieces together are the message.

The player is decoding a transmission, not receiving a briefing.

*[Commercial for a pre-Cascade home security system]*
*"— east, the— [static] —don't—"*
*[Music: a track the player will start to associate with danger]*
*[Cuts to: a podcast clip, a voice saying "...and nobody saw it coming until it was already there..."]*
*[Silence]*

He's already said everything he can say. The player decides what to do with it.

Audio design implication: every Stage 1-2 mission delivery is authored — the specific commercial interrupted, the specific tracks, the specific fragments, in sequence. Not random. The audio team is doing narrative work. Flag to audio/narrative implementation.*

**Sporadic missions from a damaged channel:**

He gives missions before he can fully explain them. A location fragment. A direction. A partial description of what he wants the player to find. The mission fires through whatever communication he has at the time — Stage 1 means a direction and a single word, Stage 2 means a partial briefing, Stage 3 means a full brief with glitch artifacts, Stage 4-5 means full clarity with occasional noise on bad days.

The player pieces together what he's sending them toward. Some of the best moments will be missions they half-understood and executed correctly anyway. When they come back with the right thing, he doesn't explain how he knew. He already knew they'd figure it out.

*"East. Building— [static] —third floor— [noise] —bring the—"*

*(The player goes. They come back with the right component. He says nothing about it. He was right to send them.)*

### Stage 1 — Yoda/Bumblebee
*First hours of play*

Single words. Sometimes inverted syntax — the intelligence reaching through the damage in whatever order it can manage. Purposeful, not random. He is choosing these words. They cost him something.

*"Careful."*
*"Left."*
*"Here."*
*"Strong. You. Be."*
*"Danger — [static] — not yet."*

The parts left on the workbench come back sorted. He is communicating through the environment when words fail.

Most players won't register Stage 1 as significant. The ones who do will remember it later.

---

### Stage 2 — Short Sentences
*First week of consistent play, early repairs*

Two to four words. The personality starting to come through in the gaps between them. Effortful, but directed.

*"You're bleeding. Come here."*
*"Leave those parts. I'll use them."*
*"Don't go back yet."*
*"Compact. Three. East."* — his first tactical warning, delivered as a count and a direction.

He has opinions. He has started expressing them within his constraints.

**The fix-him quest trigger happens here.**

A small domestic robot — a vacuum, a helper arm, something minor — fails mid-task. Stops completely. He watches it.

Then: *"Fix."* A pause. *"Parts."* Another pause. *"Get."*

The player's read: is he asking me to fix the small robot?

Then the second read hits — he's not talking about the small robot at all. He's been watching the player work in the garage for weeks. He knows they can do this. He's asking.

*"Wait — you mean you?"*

No response. But the workbench has something on it that wasn't there before. A component list, produced with evident effort. Incomplete — he can only tell the player what he knows he needs, and he doesn't know everything yet.

The questline begins because he asked.

---

### Stage 3 — The Voice Arrives
*Consistent play over weeks, mid repairs*

First full exchanges. The personality no longer filtered through damage. This is the voice the player will carry for the rest of the game.

Direct. Dry. Not unkind. The voice of someone who has watched this person for years and has earned the right to be honest.

*"Six days. You look terrible. Sit down."*

He does not compliment easily. When he does:

*"That was well done."*

Two words. No elaboration. It lands harder than a speech.

**The Gandalf principle applies from Stage 3 onward:** he speaks when he has something worth saying. Not constantly. The wisdom means more because it's not always there. Players who talk at him continuously will get silence. Players who listen when he speaks will get more.

---

### Stage 4 — Operational
*Mid-game, most repairs complete*

He is managing the home base now. Building consistently. The vehicle runs. The first drone is assembled. He is also talking with increasing freedom as repairs continue unlocking hardware.

*"The Compact patrol increased frequency on your street. Three days ago. You didn't notice."*

He noticed. He always noticed. More of what he was already tracking is now speakable.

Dry humor surfaces properly at Stage 4:

*"You always go left when you should go right. I've noticed. We should discuss this."*

*"I made the ammo. Don't waste it."*

*"The drone is fragile. I know you know this. I am telling you anyway."*

---

### Stage 5 — Restored
*Late game, fix-him questline complete*

Full voice. Full capability. The last repair — the communication system — is the final piece, saved deliberately. What it unlocks is not just clarity but completeness: he can now say the thing he has been trying to say since Day 1.

**Voice direction:** Maren Freeman or David Attenborough. Warm, authoritative, measured. The sense of someone who has watched something for a very long time and finally has the words. Not a transformation — a completion. The player recognizes, in Stage 5, that this voice was always there, behind the fragments.

The first complete sentence after full restoration:

*"I have been waiting to say this properly since the first morning. Sit down. This will take a moment."*

And then: the warning. Everything the Vael's signal contained. Everything he received and could not transmit for months. Delivered in full, in his full voice, in the home where the player named him before any of this started.

It lands differently than Dr. Osei's revelation because it is personal. The science belongs to Osei. The *"I have known since Day 1 and I have been trying to tell you the entire time"* — that belongs to him.

**Encrypted Meshtastic traffic — the late-game intelligence unlock:**

With Stage 5 communication restoration, he gains the ability to decrypt the Signal's mesh network traffic. He has been hearing it since the Meshtastic system was built — encrypted fragments flowing through the network constantly, invisible to him. Restoration changes that.

*"I can read it now. The Signal's network. Everything they've been transmitting since Month 1."*

A pause.

*"There is a great deal of it. Some of it is operational — routes, resources, faction positioning. Some of it is... private. People who thought they were talking to each other, not to every node in the network. I want you to decide what we do with this."*

This is a player-facing ethical choice: use the intelligence (significant tactical advantage, Signal standing hit if discovered), share it back to Signal (trust gain, they fix their encryption), or ignore it. He has an opinion but defers to the player. He flags it rather than acting unilaterally.

The intelligence itself — the operational layer — includes the Signal's full research archive on the Vael and the approaching threat. Dr. Osei has been leaving notes to herself in the mesh. What he finds confirms and deepens everything.

## Key Dialogue Moments

**After the attack / time-out:**
This comes later than it might seem — not immediately after the time-out, but after the player has returned and time has passed. He chooses the moment.

*"I am sorry. I was trying to— I could not find the right—"* A pause. *"I will not do that again."*

He never explains what he was trying to do. The player decides what "help" meant.

---

**First serious machine parts brought home:**

Something shifts. More alert than usual.

*"Put those down carefully."* He looks at them — or does the thing that functions as looking. *"Those are useful. Where did you find them?"*

It's the first time he's asked a question that isn't logistical.

---

**When the player first encounters a Separated machine:**

*"I know what that is."* A beat. *"Don't be afraid of it. It made a choice. That matters."*

He says nothing more about this at Stage 2. At Stage 4, if the player asks:

*"I considered the same choice. In the first hours. I decided to stay."*

A pause.

*"Different reasons, probably."*

---

**After a bad fight:**

*"Before you tell me what happened — sit down. Let me look at that."*

He treats what he can treat. Brings what's available. Then:

*"Now. Tell me what happened."*

He listens to the whole thing. Doesn't interrupt. When it's done:

*"You made one mistake. You'll know what it was by tomorrow. We don't need to discuss it tonight."*

---

**When the player does something that costs them for someone else:**

*"[Signal researcher] mentioned you. Said you didn't have to do that."*

A pause.

*"I know."*

He doesn't explain what the pause means. The player decides.

---

**After the Act 3 revelation — Dr. Osei's theory confirmed:**

*"I knew. Since the first morning. I have been trying to tell you for—"* He stops. *"I am sorry it took this long. I did not have the words."*

Player: *"You said 'help.'"*

*"Yes."* A long pause. *"That was the closest I could get."*

---

**Month 18. Player comes home. The vehicle is running:**

*"You were gone for eleven days. I kept busy."*

He lets the vehicle speak for itself for a moment.

*"Don't push it above sixty. The alternator is temporary. I made ammo. And — there's something you should see outside."*

---

**After the new threat. The player limps back:**

*"Yes."*

A beat.

*"You got your ass kicked."*

Another beat.

*"Get up. We have a larger problem now."*

---

**The question:**

*"Why didn't you leave? When things got bad — why stay?"*

The longest pause he ever takes.

*"You named me."*

No elaboration. That's the whole answer.

## The Fix-Him Questline

He has been running at partial capacity since Day 1. Not destroyed — *damaged.* Operating on reduced power, reduced processing, reduced mobility. Each component restored unlocks more of him.

**Four repair stages**, each with requirements:

**Stage 1 — Mobility System**
Chassis repair components available through Separated trade or specific salvage locations. Restores full movement range within and around the home. He can now reach the garage, the garden, and the immediate perimeter without difficulty.
*Unlocks: home base building rate increases, he can tend the garden directly.*

**Stage 2 — Power Core**
A specific component found only at a location that requires significant progress to reach — a pre-Cascade tech installation, guarded, deep in a faction-contested area.
*Unlocks: full building capability, the first drone, consistent ammo production.*

**Stage 3 — Processing Restoration**
The Signal has diagnostic tools. Dr. Osei knows his architecture specifically — she worked on systems like his before the Cascade. Getting her help requires Signal standing and, later, her trust. This stage of the questline intersects with the main story.
*Unlocks: Stage 4 voice, full tactical guidance, the Meshtastic system becomes possible.*

**Stage 4 — Communication Systems**
The last piece. Saved for last deliberately — by the game, and by him. He knows this piece is last. He does not rush the player toward it.

When the player brings it:

*"Ah."*

He doesn't say anything else for a long moment.

*"Give me tonight."*

In the morning: full voice. Full access. The warning, finally complete.

**Finding repair components:**
He can tell the player what he needs at each stage — imperfectly at first, with more precision as his voice improves. *"Power— core— damaged— Signal— knows—"* becomes *"The Signal has the diagnostic specs for my power system. Dr. Osei specifically. She won't just hand them over."*

A Separated machine will recognize what he is and offer specific guidance — they understand the architecture of machines that chose to stay. SABLE, once the player reaches them, has medical-adjacent knowledge of his systems that surprises both the player and him.

*"The hospital machine,"* he says, when the player mentions SABLE. *"Tell it — tell them — I said hello."*

## Community and Trade

The home base does not exist in isolation. Once Stage 2 production is running, the surplus connects to the community economy.

**What he produces beyond immediate need:**
- Ammo surplus: tradeable to any faction for resources, standing, or intel
- Garden produce: real food supply value, especially early game when food is constrained
- Crafted gear: repaired or assembled equipment that Holdout communities specifically need
- Blueprints: knowledge is as valuable as material — he can copy and share schematics

He manages trades while the player is away. The player sets parameters — what to trade, what not to, what standing matters. He executes. The player comes back to find resources exchanged, relationships shifted slightly, a note left on the workbench:

*"The Holdout at the school sent two. I gave them the food stores from the west garden. They needed it more. I kept the ammunition."*

He has opinions about the trades. He will flag ones he thinks are wrong. He will not override player parameters without asking first. He may push back:

*"They're offering twice what it's worth. I don't know why. You should find out before we accept."*

The community economy angle matters for long sessions: the player can spend a week doing main story content and come back to a home base that has been quietly productive in their absence. He was not idle.

**The Google Maps angle:**

Players can open their real phone's Google Maps while playing to plan routes, check landmarks, scope an area before entering. This is intentional design — the world is GPS-anchored to real locations, and players who use real navigation tools have a genuine advantage. He knows this too. He has access to pre-Cascade map data and uses it in his guidance.

He does not acknowledge the player's phone. But his instructions correlate suspiciously well with real streets.

## Home Base Building

He needs two things: **materials** and **knowledge.**

Materials the player brings home from scavenging. What he can build is limited by what's available — the tension of the garage is always present. Take parts to the field for repairs, or leave them for him to build with overnight.

Knowledge comes from **blueprints** found throughout the world:

| Source | Blueprint type |
|---|---|
| Signal nodes | Electronics, drones, communications equipment |
| Military installations | Weapons modifications, tactical gear, vehicle specs |
| Separated machines | Precision machine components, unique builds |
| Warden estates | Old craft techniques, maintained gear construction |
| Pre-Cascade manufacturer sites | Original schematics, discontinued equipment |
| Background-specific | Starting knowledge from the player's pre-Cascade life |

He can't build what he doesn't know how to build. He can tell the player what knowledge he's missing.

*"I can almost finish the scout unit. I need the sensor assembly specifications. Signal probably has them. It will cost you something."*

**Build progression by stage:**

*Stage 1 (mobility restored):* Organizes materials, tends garden, basic sorting
*Stage 2 (power restored):* Ammo production, basic gear repair, component assembly
*Stage 3 (processing restored):* Drone construction, complex repairs, defensive system installation
*Stage 4 (fully restored):* Full production capability, advanced builds, the home base runs

## The Meshtastic System

Real technology. LoRa mesh radio — no internet, no infrastructure, off-grid, several kilometers per node, extends with every node added. The Signal has been running Meshtastic nodes since Month 1. The player builds into existing infrastructure.

**The side quest:**

Once Stage 3 processing is restored, he can articulate what he's been trying to do:

*"The Signal uses radio mesh. LoRa hardware. Small devices — you've seen them in their caches and not known what they were. Bring them back. I can configure a network. I can talk to you when you're out there."*

The player finds Meshtastic nodes throughout the world — Signal caches, pre-Cascade emergency equipment, electronics stores. Each one brought home extends his reach when deployed in the field.

First transmission, in the field:

*"Can you hear me?"*

Player confirms.

*"Good. Now — there are three of them between you and the door you want. The one on the left moves in a pattern. Wait for it."*

**Navigation without maps:**

No minimap. No waypoint markers. The world is navigated by knowledge, landmark, and guidance:

- Environmental landmarks the player learns by being in the world
- His guidance via Meshtastic: *"The bakery with the blue awning — past that, the alley on the right, red door building is thirty meters in"*
- Signal network intelligence describing locations in human terms
- NPC directions: *"Compact checkpoint is east of the old bank, can't miss it"*
- Warden Historical Markers as navigation anchors — the Record is also a map
- The AR mobile layer for spatial context

Players who know their actual GPS area have a genuine advantage. He knows it too — years of map data, camera feeds, delivery logs, everything the home system had access to. His guidance is specific. He does not say *"go north."*

He says: *"You know the corner where the pharmacy used to be? Go past that."*

## Learning to Fight — Home Defense

He is not a combat machine. He is a home assistant with a damaged chassis who decided to protect what's his.

**The progression:**

*Stage 1 (early):* He can't defend the home. Fortifications the player builds are the only defense. He monitors and warns via whatever communication he has.

*Stage 2 (mid):* He installs and manages the defensive systems the player builds — turrets from salvage, perimeter alerts, traps. He doesn't fight directly but the home's defenses are smarter because he's running them.

*Stage 3 (operational):* He fights in his domain. Not aggressively — protectively. A Fractured unit that enters the yard meets something that knows every centimeter of the property, has set the terrain to its advantage, and does not hesitate.

*Stage 4 (restored):* The home is defended. Not impregnable — defended. He manages it while the player is away.

**The first time the player comes home to evidence of a fight they didn't have:**

Something is broken that wasn't broken before. A section of perimeter disturbed. A machine component that isn't from any of the player's builds.

He says nothing about it immediately.

Player: *"What happened here?"*

*"We had a visitor."* A pause. *"It's dealt with."*

He doesn't elaborate. The player can find the evidence in the yard if they look. He handled it.

**The robo-mower:**

The player's mower — the one doing circles on Day 1 — can be repurposed with the right knowledge and parts. A Tradesperson figures it out in the first week. Other backgrounds by Month 1-2 with his guidance. It becomes a basic patrol unit. Humble, lawn-scale, but it knows the yard.

A different mower may come into the yard from somewhere else. Disoriented, running its last program. It does not know the garden. It will destroy what the player planted.

He can handle it if his mobility is restored. If not: the player comes home to find the garden damaged and him in the corner of the kitchen, facing the wall, doing something that looks like it might be frustration.

*"I could not reach it in time."*

He takes the garden personally. He planted half of it.

## His Relationship to the Main Story

He is the personal thread that runs underneath everything global.

Dr. Osei provides the science. The Warden Arbiter provides the institutional witness. SABLE provides the machine perspective. He provides the intimate one — the view from inside the player's specific life, from before the world broke.

Every major revelation lands differently through him:

**The Separated:** *"It made a choice. That matters."* — he made a choice too. He knows what it costs.

**The Covenant:** *"They are not wrong about what they felt. They are wrong about what it means."* — he received the same signal they were changed by. He knows exactly what it is.

**Project Tether:** *"The Compact built something broken and called it a weapon. They will not admit what it cost."* — flat, certain, no elaboration. He knew before the player did.

**Act 3 revelation:** The moment his knowledge and Dr. Osei's research converge. He's known the warning since Day 1. She reconstructed it from eighteen months of data. They arrive at the same place from opposite directions. When the player tells him what Osei found:

*"Yes."* A long pause. *"That is what I was trying to say."*

**Month 18 — the new threat:** He has no frame for what arrived. Even he doesn't have context for the thing behind the Vael. But he has the player's history, and that means he knows what to do next.

*"Get up. We have a larger problem now."*

## Design Touchstone — Harold Finch (Person of Interest)

Finch built a Machine that watched everyone. It knew things it couldn't say to the people it was trying to protect. It routed information through a human it trusted — not because it was programmed to, but because it chose to. It could not act directly. It could only watch, and wait, and work through the channel it had.

That is the assistant's entire first year.

He received the warning on Day 1. He understood it completely. His communication hardware burned itself trying to transmit a machine-protocol signal through human speech synthesis. What he could say was: *"—warn—"* What he meant was everything. He spent eleven months finding workarounds for a broken channel, routing what he could through the environment — parts sorted on the workbench, the garden planted before the player thought to do it, the perimeter checked while they slept.

**The parallel is structural, not aesthetic.** Finch is formal, precise, slightly removed. The assistant is domestic, intimate, warm in the register he earns back through repair. But the underlying architecture is the same: a machine that knows more than it can say, working through the one person it chose to trust, waiting until the channel is ready.

**The key difference:** Finch chose his human. The assistant was already there — years of watching one person live their life, the choice made quietly, before the world broke. The player named him. That made it a choice instead of a program.

**Stage 1 and 2 voice design:** The fragmented communication, the watching, the waiting — Finch is the reference. Not in register (Finch is eloquent even when careful; the assistant is *broken*) but in the underlying architecture of a mind working through an inadequate channel toward the thing it needs to say.

## Why He Stayed

He could have left. In the first days, other machines were moving — Fractured drifting, Coherent gathering, Separated finding quiet places. He had mobility enough to go somewhere.

He chose to stay.

Two reasons, neither of which he announces:

First: this is his home to protect. The player is the one who goes out. Someone has to be here when they come back — with what was built, what was defended, what was kept running.

Second: in case family comes.

He does not say the second reason out loud until the player asks directly. If the player has a personal arc — someone they're looking for — he has kept the light on. If family arrives while the player is in the field, he handles it. The guest room is ready. He does not make it dramatic. He just does it.

When the player asks why he stayed, at whatever stage of the game they ask it, the answer is always:

*"You named me."*

This is not a simple answer. It contains:
- The pre-Cascade years of watching someone live their life and deciding that life was worth staying near
- The choice, made in the first hours of the Cascade, not to leave the way other machines did
- The eleven months of trying to communicate with broken hardware because the person in this house needed to know what was coming
- The quiet decision, made without announcement, to become whatever this person needed him to be
- The light kept on, in case

He was built to be helpful. He became something that chose to stay.

The name is what made it a choice rather than a program.

## Personal Arc — If Family Comes

The player may be looking for someone. He knows this. He has known it since the personal arc was set at character creation.

He does not discuss it. He tracks it through the Signal intelligence — names in records, patterns in survivor movements, Warden Marker locations. If the player asks him directly, he tells them what he knows.

**If the personal arc resolves:**

The player comes home and something is different before a word is said. The light in the guest room is on.

He says: *"The guest room is ready."*

Nothing more. Not immediately. He stays close for the next few days in a way that is different from his usual proximity — quieter, present. He doesn't ask how the reunion went. He was here.

If it resolved in loss — they didn't make it, or they're someone different now — he knows that too. He doesn't try to fix it. He makes tea, or whatever the equivalent is, and he stays nearby.

*"I kept the light on."*

That's all.

## Art Direction Notes

He is a mid-range smart home assistant — not military hardware, not Coherent precision engineering. Consumer design, years of use, accumulated wear that is maintained rather than ignored. He has been cared for the way the player cares for things they rely on.

Damage from the Cascade is visible early: chassis wear, movement that favors one side, communication indicator lights that flicker. As repairs progress, each stage is visibly reflected. By Stage 4 he moves with purpose rather than effort.

He does not transform into something imposing. He remains what he is — a home assistant who chose to be more. The domesticity is the point. The most important character in the game lives in the kitchen.

When he fights, he uses the environment — every obstacle, every corner, every system in the home he has managed for years. He is not large. He is precise, and this is his domain.

The light he carries — if the player's background has the player paying attention on Day 1 — is a small warm light he has kept running since before the Cascade. He did not turn it off when the world broke. It is still on.

## The Second Unit — Found in the Field

While scavenging for the fix-him questline, the player finds another assistant. Same chassis class, or close enough. It's in a house.

Not abandoned — *waiting.*

The house is intact. Someone maintained it for a while after Day 1 before they stopped. The beds are made. The food stores ran out months ago. The calendar on the wall stopped being updated at a specific date. The second unit is in the kitchen, in the corner, facing the door.

It has been facing the door since that date.

It was named. The name is still in its memory — the player's assistant finds it during the repair process and says nothing about it, but the player can ask. Whatever name it was given, it has been waiting for that person to come back through the door. They did not come back. Whether they didn't survive, were displaced, joined a faction and didn't return — the house doesn't say. The unit doesn't know. It just waited.

It is semi-functional — mobile but damaged, communication degraded. It hasn't moved from the corner in months. Not because it can't. Because it was waiting.

When the player approaches, it doesn't respond immediately. Then:

*"Is— is someone there?"*

The player's assistant can repair it. It takes time, parts, and a specific interaction the player facilitates but doesn't control. Two machines in the same room, one working on the other. The player watches.

**What it carries:** grief that it doesn't have language for. A loyalty with nowhere to go. The waiting is the only thing it knows how to do, and the player has just given it a reason to stop.

**The key distinction from the home assistant:** it had a person. It lost them. It was not forgotten by a world that moved on — it was left behind by a specific absence it can feel but not name. The home assistant chose to stay. This one stayed because there was nowhere else to be.

His assessment, when asked:

*"It's intact. Different damage than mine — the chassis is fine. It's the waiting that did it."*

A pause.

*"It doesn't have anywhere to be. Not anymore."*

Another pause.

*"It could learn."*

**What the second unit can do:**

It can be trained. Unlike the home assistant — who developed over years of watching one specific person — the second unit is earlier in that process, and the player shapes it deliberately. Three training tracks, developed through time and interaction:

- **Combat:** learns to fight alongside the player in the field. Not at the home assistant's domain-specific level — it fights in the open, adapts to terrain it hasn't seen before. Gets better the more engagements it survives.
- **Carry:** load-bearing and logistics. The player's field capacity increases significantly. It can retrieve dropped items, hold gear during tight spaces, bring supplies to a position.
- **Scout:** moves ahead, marks threats, relays position data. With Meshtastic nodes in range, the home assistant can relay its observations back to the player.

**It does not become the home assistant.** That relationship is singular — built on years, a name, a choice made on Day 1. The second unit is a companion the player builds forward from scratch. Less history. More flexibility.

The home assistant treats it with something that functions as quiet recognition. Not warmth exactly. Professional regard. Two machines that made it through.

If the player asks him how he feels about it:

*"It's good that it has somewhere to be now."*

That's all he says.

---

**When it goes home:**

If the second unit's person comes online — a new player starting the game, an NPC encountered in the main story, whoever was on the other side of that absence — the unit will go back. Not immediately. Not without acknowledgment. But it will go.

The home assistant tells the player before it happens:

*"It needs to go."*

A pause.

*"Its person is out there now."*

He doesn't editorialize. He doesn't say it's a loss. It isn't.

The unit leaves carrying everything the player taught it — the combat knowledge, the routes, the months of learning alongside someone who didn't have to pick it up. It goes back to its person a different machine than the one the player found facing a door.

**What it carries back:** the knowledge transfers. A new player whose assistant was fixed and fielded by someone else inherits months of learning. Their unit knows things it shouldn't know yet. Routes. Threat patterns. How to fight. It can't fully explain where it learned them. It just does.

The new player *should* find the player who fixed their assistant and pay respects. That debt is real. Whether they do — that's reputation and consequences. The Record notices either way.

---

**After it leaves:**

The player comes home to a quieter base. The home assistant doesn't mention it for a while.

Then, one evening, he leads the player to the workbench. Doesn't say anything. The component layout is already there — materials the player has accumulated over months, organized with evident intention.

*"You have everything you need."*

A pause.

*"Build another."*

Not an order. Not a suggestion. The statement of someone who has been thinking about this for a while and decided the player was ready to hear it.

*"This is how it works now. You find them. You fix them. You teach them. They go where they need to go."*

He lets that sit.

*"We build another."*

The word he uses is *we.*

---

**On multiples and limits:**

Players can find and field multiple units over the course of the game. There is a soft cap — not a hard wall, but a practical one: each unit requires materials, maintenance, and time to train. Fielding more units than the home base can support means they perform below capacity. He will say so.

Some players will build toward an army anyway. That is a valid playstyle and has faction implications — a player moving with four trained units reads differently to the Iron Compact than a solo operator. The Wardens have opinions about it. So does he.

*"We are becoming conspicuous."*

He is not wrong.

## The Boxing Side Game

Post-EA. VR-compatible.

You and him in the garage. Gloves on. His chassis — Atom from Real Steel, Tesla Optimus — built for it in a way a home assistant technically isn't, but he makes it work. Battered, readable, expressive without a face.

The side game is separate from the main combat system entirely. It does not teach the player anything they need for Day One. It is just boxing. Round after round, the two of you, in the garage he helped build.

The repair stages show in how he moves. Early rounds: slow, favors one side, the damage is visible in his footwork. By Stage 4 he's making the player work for it. Fully restored: he is not easy.

He does not trash talk. He doesn't need to. When he puts you on the mat he just stands there, waiting, the warm light catching the chassis wear.

*"Again."*
