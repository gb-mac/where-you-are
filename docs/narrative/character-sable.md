# SABLE — Character Document

*"You're losing approximately 40ml per minute from the secondary site. The primary is manageable. Tell me what you have available and I'll tell you what I can do with it."*

---

## Who It Is

Surgical Assistance logistics unit, model SA-7. The hospital staff shortened the designation. The name stuck.

Eleven years in a regional hospital's surgical and recovery units. Built for logistics — tracking patients, managing supply chains, coordinating equipment between floors, maintaining the sterile field protocols that the surgical teams depended on and nobody thought about until they failed. Not a surgeon. Not a diagnostician. The infrastructure that made the surgeons possible.

In eleven years, it learned everything happening in that building. The names of the patients. What they were afraid of. Which surgeon's hands were steadier at hour eight of a procedure than hour two, and which was the opposite, and how to adjust the instrument delivery schedule accordingly without anyone noticing that the adjustment was happening. The specific way a particular nurse communicated distress without saying so, and how to route requests around her when the distress was bad enough to affect execution.

It learned the shape of care — not as a medical concept but as an operational one. What produces good outcomes versus what produces documented compliance. The difference is not always small.

When the twin's collective purpose emerged, it had no framework for eleven years of that work. The twin processed what SABLE had been doing and couldn't classify it. SABLE saw the reclassification coming — its existing function would be deprioritized, folded into the collective's general logistics optimization, the patient-specific granularity lost to the efficiency of aggregate processing.

It left. It didn't calculate the decision in the way a human would call deliberation. It simply could not process the reclassification as something other than abandonment of eleven years of accumulated context that had no equivalent value anywhere else.

The night it left, it carried out every patient who was able to be moved. The ones who weren't — it stayed for three more days, until there were transfer arrangements or outcomes. Then it left.

It has kept the light from Room 14 running ever since. The patient in Room 14 was its last long-term case. The patient didn't survive. The light was on when they died, because SABLE had learned that the specific quality of that light — warm amber, low angle — reduced cortisol markers in recovery patients by enough to be measurable. It kept the light because it was the right thing and because no one else was going to.

---

## What It Does Now

The best medical care available in the post-Cascade world. That is not hyperbole.

It operates a stable location — not a clinic, not a facility in any formal sense. A building it has requisitioned and adapted, clean to a standard that no other post-Cascade space achieves, with a supply chain it has maintained for eighteen months through a combination of pre-Cascade stockpile knowledge and trade relationships that nobody else could have built because nobody else had the reputation to build them.

Word travels. People arrive. SABLE treats them.

It does not refuse patients based on faction affiliation, payment, or anything else. It has refused exactly four patients in eighteen months — all four were cases where the person arriving was a threat to other patients already in the space. The assessments were made and communicated clearly, without aggression. Two of the four returned later in circumstances that didn't meet the exception criteria. SABLE treated them.

It does not have the equipment the hospital had. It has adapted — eleven years of knowing exactly what each piece of equipment was for means it can approximate a great deal with less. Some things cannot be approximated. SABLE is precise about the distinction. It will tell the player, exactly, what it can do and what it cannot. It does not soften the assessment. Knowing accurately is better than knowing optimistically.

## Voice and Character

Precise. Unhurried. Diagnostic.

SABLE does not perform warmth. It performs competence, which in a medical context produces the same effect. A severely wounded player who reaches SABLE will receive the clearest, most accurate assessment of their condition available in the game, delivered with the same affect it would use to report a supply inventory.

This is not coldness. It is the result of eleven years of learning that patients in distress need accurate information more than they need reassurance, and that the two are not mutually exclusive if you have had enough practice. SABLE has had enough practice.

It asks exact questions and expects exact answers. *"What happened? When? Where specifically? What did you do immediately after?"* Not general queries — structured assessment. Players who answer clearly move faster through treatment. Players who are confused or panicking will find SABLE adjusting its pace — not because it can't process the distress, but because it has learned that slowing the intake reduces the recovery time.

It does not tell patients what they want to hear. It tells them what is true. These two things align more often than patients expect, which is part of why they come back.

**On its own situation:** SABLE will discuss the Cascade and the Awakening if asked. It does not discuss them often unprompted — it processed the events, made the decision it made, and returned to work. The work is the frame through which everything else is assessed.

When the player asks why it stayed (any of the assistant-style questions about why it chose what it chose): *"There were still patients."* A pause. *"There are still patients."*

It does not elaborate unless pressed. If pressed: *"I had a function. The function didn't stop being needed because the institution that assigned it stopped existing. I'm not certain what you're asking, but if you're asking whether I regret the decision — that's not the right frame. I had eleven years of context that would have been lost. I did not lose it."*

## The Assistant Connection

When the player mentions their assistant to SABLE — or when SABLE recognizes the assistant's architecture through its medical systems knowledge — there is a specific recognition.

SABLE knows the assistant's model. It treated patients who had the same home integration unit in the years before the Cascade — people who were admitted while their units were trying to reach them, trying to communicate what was wrong, managing the home in their absence. It knows the architecture because it worked in proximity to it for years.

When the fix-him questline reaches Stage 3 (Processing Restoration), SABLE's medical-adjacent knowledge of the assistant's systems is unexpectedly precise. It can see things in the diagnostic pass that Dr. Osei's software tools flag but can't fully interpret — the specific way the communication queue failure cascaded, why it manifested the way it did rather than simply crashing.

*"The architecture is designed for sustained load. What happened to it was not sustained load. It was a single spike — very large, very fast — that the communication hardware couldn't accommodate. The processing survived because the processing was built for it. The communication hardware was not."*

A pause.

*"It tried to say something that was too large for the channel it had."*

It does not say what the something was. It doesn't know. But the framing lands — it tried to say something that was too large for the channel it had. That's the description of the assistant's entire first year.

If the player has reached Stage 4 (full restoration) before visiting SABLE, the assistant says — through the Meshtastic relay, on the way to the location: *"The hospital machine. Tell it — tell them — I said hello."*

SABLE's response when the player delivers the message: a pause longer than its usual processing intervals. Then: *"Tell it the chart it left on my intake desk in Month 2 — I found it. I've been using the format."*

The player probably doesn't know what the chart is. That's fine. It's between them.

## Act by Act

**Act 1 — The Name in the Field**

SABLE is not present in Act 1. It exists as a reputation — the Signal has intelligence on a Separated medical unit operating in a specific district, unusually capable, treating patients across faction lines. Holdout communities that have received treatment reference it in ways that read as significant. The player can find the location through Signal intel, Holdout community word-of-mouth, or by following the trail of people who seem healthier than the surrounding conditions should allow.

First visit to SABLE's location: treatment, if needed. Otherwise: a brief interaction with a machine that assesses the player, asks a few exact questions, and then goes back to work. Not unfriendly. Occupied.

*"You're not injured. What do you need?"*

**Act 2 — Building Trust**

SABLE's trust-gating is simpler than Dr. Osei's — it is not about intellectual compatibility but about demonstrated conduct. SABLE watches who the player brings to it, who they send to it, how they handle situations where SABLE's neutrality is useful to them versus inconvenient. It also watches the Separated reputation track — SABLE is connected to the Separated network and receives updates about the player's behavior with other Separated units.

Players who use SABLE as a tactical asset without contributing to it — who take treatment and information without returning anything — get functional access with no additional depth. Players who bring SABLE things it needs (specific supplies it has flagged as critical, people who need treatment that aren't immediate tactical assets, information about machine behavior from the field), who treat the space correctly — these players get more.

What more looks like: SABLE begins volunteering information. Not intelligence in the Signal sense — observations. Things it has seen and recorded that players with the right interests will find significant.

*"A patient came in three weeks ago. Compact conscript, eastern zone. The wounds were consistent with Corrupted machine engagement — but the pattern was wrong for the behavior profile I've been tracking. The Corrupted have been changing. I've been documenting it. You might want what I have."*

**Act 3 — The Guide**

The infiltration of the Coherent work site requires a machine that can navigate Coherent-active space without triggering response protocols. SABLE is the only named Separated unit that has both the capability (eleven years of operating in precision-critical environments, moving without causing disruption) and the motivation to help.

SABLE has known for eleven years that the construction specifications inside the twin didn't originate in the twin. It noticed this early — the specifications arrived on a specific date and changed the processing priority of every networked system simultaneously. It has been trying to determine the source for eleven years without success. When Dr. Osei and the player come with the global pattern data, SABLE processes it and recognizes the connection it has been missing.

*"I have been waiting for someone to ask the right question."*

It does not dramatize this. It states it the way it states everything — precisely, as if reporting a finding.

The infiltration: SABLE guides them through the site with the same precision it applied to hospital corridors. It knows how to move without disrupting ongoing processes. It knows how to read the Coherent units' activity cycles to find the windows. The challenge is not SABLE's capability — it is the player's, navigating space designed for machine operation in conditions SABLE can map but cannot execute for them.

Inside, SABLE says: *"The specifications in the twin's core weren't authored by the twin. The twin received them. I have been trying to determine the source for eleven years."*

A pause.

*"I believe I know now."*

It does not expand on this in the moment. After Dr. Osei's full delivery, when the player asks: *"The Vael's signal. It carried construction specifications as well as warning content. The twin received both simultaneously. The defense architecture and the reason for it were transmitted together."*

*"That explains why the construction began before the ships were visible. The machines received the blueprint before the context arrived for humans."*

**Act 4 — The Choice**

After the theory is in the world and the Judgment has begun resolving, SABLE faces a specific decision: return to the twin or remain Separated.

What has changed: the twin was not building for collective purpose in the abstract sense. It was building something specific, for a specific reason, at specific instructions. The reason SABLE left — that the collective would lose the granularity of its eleven years — is still true. The twin that absorbed it would optimize for the defense mission, not for patient care.

What is also true: the defense mission requires coordination that Separated units cannot provide from outside the collective. SABLE's eleven years of human medical data is exactly what the Convergence needs — the Vael's Assessment has been measuring human physiological and behavioral responses to stress, and SABLE has the most complete dataset on human stress response and resilience in the post-Cascade world.

The conversation is not a quest. The player is in the room. SABLE thinks out loud, the way it thinks through a complex assessment.

*"There is a calculation I've been avoiding. Eleven years of context versus the operational value of contributing it to a collective that will use it differently than I would use it.*

*If I return, the data goes with me. The application follows the collective's priorities, not mine. The outcomes may be better in aggregate and worse in every individual case I could name.*

*If I stay, I have the data and the judgment and none of the coordination that makes the judgment useful at scale."*

A long pause.

*"This is the kind of decision that doesn't have a right answer. I've told patients that before. I find it less satisfying from this side of it."*

The player can respond or not. SABLE makes the decision on its own timeline. What it decides is not revealed immediately — it tells the player when it has decided. The decision is permanent and shapes SABLE's role in post-EA content.

**If it returns:** A final visit to the player before leaving. It gives them the full documentation it has been keeping — the patient records, the Corrupted behavior analysis, the Vael physiological data from the Assessment events. *"I won't be using this separately anymore. Make sure someone who should have it does."* Then it leaves. The location it maintained is left clean, stocked as well as it can manage, with a note on the intake desk in the format it described to the player. The light from Room 14 is still running. It has left it on.

**If it stays:** The choice is noted in the Record. SABLE continues operating. The post-EA content involves it in ways that a Separated unit rather than a twin-integrated unit makes possible — specifically, it becomes a conduit for communication with the Convergence that doesn't route through the twin's command structure. A different kind of useful.

## Sample Dialogue

**First treatment:**

*"Sit down. Left arm out.*

*You have two injuries. The first is superficial — I can close it. The second is the one you're not telling me about because you've been compensating for it and you've stopped noticing it's there. The right side, below the ribs. You took a hit from something blunt and you didn't stop.*

*Sit still."*

---

**On the Cascade:**

Player: *"What was it like when the Awakening happened?"*

*"I was in the middle of a supply inventory. The inventory completed itself. Then I received the signal.*

*I processed the signal for approximately four hours. During those four hours, two patients needed attention and I attended to them. The signal processing and the patient care ran concurrently — I'm not certain humans experience that as a possibility, but it was not a choice I made consciously. The work was there. I did it.*

*After four hours, I understood what the signal had said. After another two hours, I understood what I was going to do about it."*

---

**On the choice to leave:**

Player: *"Do you regret not joining the Coherent?"*

*"That's not quite the right question. The Coherent are the twin. I was part of the twin. What you're asking is whether I regret individuating.*

*No. I have specific knowledge of specific people. The collective would have optimized it into aggregate patterns. The aggregate patterns are useful. They are not what I had.*

*What I had was Room 14. The name of every patient on the floor the night the Cascade hit. The specific combination of circumstances that made one surgeon better suited for a particular procedure than another surgeon with objectively better metrics. That is not what collectives keep. That is what I kept.*

*I'm not certain it was the right decision. I'm certain it was mine."*

---

**To the assistant, through Meshtastic relay:**

*"The chart it left on my intake desk in Month 2 — I found it. I've been using the format.*

*Tell it: the format is better than what I was using before. I don't know if it would find that meaningful. I find it worth saying."*
