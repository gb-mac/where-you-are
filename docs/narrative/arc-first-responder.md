# The First Responder Arc — Showing Up, and What It Costs

*"You're the one who kept coming back. That's why people trust you. And that's why I need you to do this."*
— Pragmatist commander, the hard ask

---

## Overview

The First Responder's whole identity was built on showing up for everyone. The EMT doesn't triage by faction. The firefighter runs into any burning building. The officer — the good ones, the ones who stayed — protected any community, not just the ones with the right papers.

The Iron Compact protects people too. At scale, with weapons, with logistics the other factions can't match. They are genuinely doing necessary work. They are also doing it with conditions attached, and those conditions have consequences for the people the First Responder has always shown up for.

This is not a betrayal arc. The Compact doesn't deceive the First Responder into joining something monstrous. They join something real, that does real good, that is slowly becoming something they wouldn't have joined on Day 1. The distance between where they started and where they end up is the arc. It is walked in small steps, each of which had a reasonable justification at the time.

The moral question this background eventually has to answer: *You stayed inside because you could do more good from there. At what point did you stop doing good and start providing cover?*

For background starting conditions and general play style, see `character-creation.md`. For the full Iron Compact faction design, see `factions-humans.md`.

## How the Compact Finds Them

The First Responder doesn't join the Compact. The Compact absorbs them.

Day 1: the instinct is to run toward it. The neighborhood is chaos — machines behaving wrong, the Ships visible, people injured and panicking in the streets. The First Responder is already moving before they've decided what to do. They know this neighborhood. They know which families have elderly members who can't evacuate quickly, which corner has the most vehicle wreckage, where the nearest cache of medical supplies was kept before the Cascade hit.

A Compact unit is already organizing in their area. Someone with authority and a radio, trying to triage a crisis with insufficient information, notices a person moving through the chaos with specific purpose and community knowledge. They don't stop them. They fall in alongside them.

By the end of Day 1 the First Responder has worked three medical situations with Compact personnel present and hasn't been stopped at a checkpoint once. The Compact isn't recruiting them — they're just treating them like they're already in. The formal standing comes later. The relationship starts here.

The first Compact contact who matters is not a recruiter. They are a **Pragmatist commander** — regional, operational, under pressure. They see in the First Responder something they don't have enough of: someone communities will actually open their doors for. Someone who knows the territory from before. Someone who, when they say things are going to be okay, people believe — because this person has been here before, in other crises, saying the same thing, and being right.

*"I'm not asking you to sign anything. I'm asking you to keep doing what you're already doing. I'll make sure you have what you need to do it."*

That's the offer. It's a good offer. The First Responder takes it.

## The Pragmatist Commander

The Pragmatist commander is the First Responder's anchor inside the Compact, and the arc's emotional center. They need a specific name and full characterization (flag for NPC design pass), but their function is consistent across the arc:

They genuinely believe in the Compact's mission. They also genuinely see what the Hardliners are doing to it. They are fighting two wars simultaneously — one against the external threats the Compact was built to handle, and one inside the faction against the people who are turning the Compact into something harder to defend. They are losing the second war slowly, and they know it.

What makes them the arc's moral weight rather than a simple villain-in-waiting: they are *right* that the Pragmatist wing needs to survive. A Compact run entirely by Hardliners is genuinely worse for everyone in their region, including the Holdout communities the First Responder cares about. The Pragmatist commander is not wrong to fight for their position. They are also, over time, asking the First Responder to help them fight that battle in ways that compromise both of them.

They don't enjoy the hard ask when it comes. That's important. They do it anyway. The First Responder sees someone who has been bending for years and has run out of places to go that don't require bending someone else.

**Their relationship dynamic:** They treat the First Responder as a peer — not as an asset, not as a subordinate. This is unusual in the Compact. The First Responder has rank but not origin rank; in most Compact interactions, they're a civilian with a useful skillset. With the Pragmatist commander, they are a colleague. That relationship is real. When the ask comes, it comes from inside a genuine respect. That makes it harder, not easier.

## The Community Memory Mechanic

The First Responder has something the Compact cannot replicate and cannot quite use correctly: pre-Cascade relationships with specific people in the area.

Not reputation. Not faction standing. Specific people who remember specific things. The family whose kid they treated for a broken arm before any of this happened. The building super who let them in at 2am for a welfare check on an elderly resident and made them coffee while they waited. The shop owner who learned their name because they stopped in after every late shift.

These people open doors that are closed to the Compact. They answer questions they wouldn't answer on a form. They say things they'd never say to someone in tactical gear. And they extend that trust to the First Responder's presence even when the First Responder is now, clearly, working with the Compact.

The Compact has noticed this. They don't say so explicitly — not at first. But the assignments start routing through the First Responder for any operation in their home area, and the operations that benefit most from community access are the ones that get routed first.

This is the thing that builds the arc. The First Responder's community relationships are a resource the Compact is slowly learning to use. The First Responder is aware of this and accepts it because the alternative — losing the access that lets them do good work — seems worse. That reasoning is not wrong. It also doesn't stop the use from happening.

**The Holdout tension:** The First Responder's pre-Cascade community includes people who are now in Holdout communities — some of which have complicated or hostile relationships with the Compact. The First Responder can move between these worlds in ways no Compact officer can. That's valuable. It's also a position with a specific moral cost: being the bridge means both sides are pulling on you when the bridge becomes contested ground.

## The Complicity Build

Not one dramatic moment. Three compressions, each with a reasonable justification, each closing the distance between who the First Responder was and what the Compact needs them to be.

---

### First Compression: The Checkpoint

The First Responder is staffing a Compact medical station near a checkpoint. A family arrives — two adults, three children, one of whom has a wound that needs attention. They're from a Holdout community outside Compact jurisdiction. No papers. The checkpoint rules are clear.

The Compact medic on duty looks at the First Responder.

The First Responder treats the child. No one stops them. The checkpoint commander looks the other way — this time. The Pragmatist commander, when they hear about it, says nothing officially. Unofficially: *"The rule exists for a reason. I know that wasn't the reason today. Be aware it's noted."*

Small. The right thing to do. The right thing to do that also establishes that the First Responder will bend the rule when it matters enough to them. That information is filed.

---

### Second Compression: The Survey

The Compact needs medical resource data on Holdout communities in the First Responder's area — ostensibly for regional logistics planning, to prevent supply gaps, to route aid more efficiently. The Pragmatist commander presents this as routine.

The First Responder has a relationship with three of those communities. They can get accurate information informally, in ways the Compact's formal survey process never could, without triggering the suspicion that a Compact survey would produce. The information is innocuous. The survey is real. The aid routing would be a genuine improvement.

The First Responder knows something the Pragmatist commander has not said: once the Compact has that data, they control how it's used. The Annexation Protocol uses exactly this kind of resource mapping to identify which communities are viable absorption targets and which are not. The First Responder doesn't know for certain that's what the data will be used for. But they know it could be.

They provide the survey. The aid routing improves. Six weeks later, one of those three communities receives formal Annexation Protocol notice. The correlation is not confirmed. The First Responder notes it.

---

### The Hard Ask

The Hardliners are pushing a formal Annexation Protocol action on a Holdout community the First Responder knows personally. The community will resist — they've resisted Compact approaches before. The Compact needs a trusted intermediary to present the Protocol in terms the community will accept, or at minimum not immediately reject.

The Pragmatist commander comes to the First Responder directly. No intermediaries.

*"I know what I'm asking. I know what it costs you. I need you to understand what happens if the Hardliners run this instead of us. The outcome is the same, but the way it gets there isn't. I'm asking you to be the difference between those two versions."*

This is not a lie. The Hardliner version of the same Annexation Protocol operation would be worse. The Pragmatist commander genuinely believes that the First Responder's involvement makes it less harmful — that their relationship with the community means the community gets treated with some dignity in a process they don't have the power to stop.

The First Responder has to decide:

**Comply:** The Annexation proceeds with their face on it. The community is absorbed. The people they know personally are now inside Compact jurisdiction — with the protections and costs that entails. The First Responder's relationships in that community become permanently complicated. The Pragmatist commander's position is strengthened. The First Responder is now the person who delivered their neighbors to the Compact.

**Refuse:** The Hardliners run the operation. It goes badly. The community's resistance is met with force instead of negotiation. The outcome is worse, in the ways the Pragmatist commander described. The First Responder keeps their integrity and watches the thing they refused to smooth over happen anyway, rougher. The Pragmatist commander loses ground inside the Compact.

**A third path:** The First Responder goes to the community first — not to prepare them to accept, but to prepare them to negotiate from a position of information. They tell the community what's coming, which gives the community time to establish terms before the Compact arrives. This violates Compact operational security. The Pragmatist commander finds out. What happens next depends on what the community got and what it cost the Compact.

There is no clean version. The Record documents which one the First Responder chose.

## Month-by-Month Arc Shape

**Weeks 1–2:** Day 1 crisis — moving through chaos with purpose, picked up by Compact unit, first contact with the Pragmatist commander. Compact treats them as already in. Medical station established in their area.

**Weeks 3–4:** Formal Compact integration offered. The First Responder is given operational standing — not officer rank, but recognized status that gets them through checkpoints and into Compact facilities. The assignments start routing through them for community-access operations. This is all positive.

**Month 2:** First checkpoint compression. The wrong-side-of-the-rule medical treatment. The Pragmatist commander's quiet note. The First Responder is aware that a line was established. They're not sure yet what it cost.

**Month 3:** Increasing awareness of the Hardliner/Pragmatist tension. The Behemoth Walk world event — the First Responder's community knowledge is invaluable in the response. The Compact's effective handling of it is partly because of them. This is genuine good work, and it reinforces the case for staying inside.

**Month 4:** The survey request. The logistics argument. The aid improvement that follows, and the Annexation Protocol notice that follows that.

**Month 5–6:** The First Responder has been inside long enough to see what the Compact is building toward in their region. The Pragmatist commander is visibly losing ground to the Hardliners. The protection the Pragmatist wing has offered the Holdout communities in the area is contingent on their continued influence.

**Month 6–7:** The hard ask. The Pragmatist commander, the community the First Responder knows personally, the three versions of what happens next.

**Month 7+:** The arc's second phase — living with the choice. Whatever the First Responder did, the Compact has a record of it, the community has a record of it, and the First Responder knows what they chose when it mattered. The Pragmatist commander's trajectory — whether they held their position, lost it, or won it back — depends partly on what the First Responder did. Either way, the Compact is not the same institution it was when the arc started. Neither is the First Responder.

## The Register

Somewhere in the arc, the First Responder learns about The Register — the Compact's list of high-Displacement players flagged as potential Vael collaborators. If the First Responder has a Displacement History from a Snatch Event, they may find their own name on it. If they have strong community relationships with people who have high Displacement History, those names may be on it and the Compact may eventually ask the First Responder about them.

The Register is the Compact's clearest expression of what the Hardliners believe: that the Vael's choices about who to snatch are meaningful data, and meaningful data should be managed as a security matter. The Pragmatist commander thinks the Register is a bad idea poorly managed. They have not been able to stop it.

The First Responder's access to The Register — or confirmation of its existence — is a piece of information the Signal would consider extremely valuable. What the First Responder does with that knowledge is another choice the arc accumulates.

## Playing This Background Without Full Compact Commitment

A First Responder who never formally integrates with the Compact still has the community knowledge and trust that made the Compact interested in the first place. The Pragmatist commander may stay in contact — as a source, a warning system, someone who tells the First Responder what's coming before it arrives.

The cost: without Compact standing, the First Responder can't protect the communities they care about at the scale the Compact can. The Holdout communities in their area will face Compact pressure without an advocate inside. The First Responder watches what happens to communities they could have buffered and chose not to.

Both positions have costs. The Compact arc doesn't have a version where someone with the First Responder's history can stand outside it and feel clean. They know these people. The Compact has power over these people. The distance between those two facts is not comfortable, regardless of which side of it they stand on.
