# Assistant Audio Content Bible
*The playlist is not random. Every track, fragment, interruption, and silence is authored intent.*
*This document defines the pool. Implementation draws from it contextually.*

---

## How the System Works

The assistant communicates through audio before he can communicate through words. At Stages 1 and 2 he has a damaged channel — single words, static, fragments. But the home audio system works. The radio works. The archive of pre-Cascade recordings works.

He curates. He interrupts. He redirects. He stops things at exactly the right moment.

The player learns to read it. Not because the game tells them to — because he's consistent enough that the pattern becomes legible over time. Some players will figure it out in the first week. Some will finish the game and realize in retrospect what he was doing. Both are correct experiences.

**The interruption can happen anywhere:**
- Mid-commercial
- Mid-song, at a specific lyric or bar
- Mid-podcast sentence
- Mid-news clip
- Mid-silence — he starts something
- He can also just stop what's playing. Silence is a message.

**The sequence is the communication:**
What he interrupts + his voice fragment (if any) + what he switches to + where he stops it = the full transmission. No single element carries it alone.

---

## Emotional Register Categories

All audio content is tagged to one or more registers. He pulls from the register that matches the moment.

| Register | When it fires | Feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Danger** | Threat incoming, player about to make a mistake | Tense, urgent, discordant |
| **Urgency** | Time-sensitive mission, something needs to happen now | Driving, forward |
| **Homecoming** | Player returns after extended absence | Warm, quiet, relief |
| **Loss** | Personal arc grief, someone didn't make it | Spare, unresolved |
| **Recognition** | Player does something right, something that cost them | Simple, still |
| **Warning** | Faction threat, incoming pressure, something wrong nearby | Low, specific |
| **Wonder** | First encounter with something significant — Vael, Coherent, the Separated | Open, spacious |
| **Quiet** | Late night, home base, nothing pressing | Ambient, present |
| **Humor** | Dark absurdist moments, the world being exactly what it is | Wry, dry |

## Music Pool

### Danger
- Something with a driving low end that builds without resolving
- A track that sounds like it's waiting for something to happen
- Cut points: before the drop, before the resolution — he stops it there

### Urgency
- Propulsive, rhythmic, clear
- He plays the middle of it — not the intro, not the outro
- Cut point: when the momentum peaks

### Homecoming
- Something the player will start to associate with coming back
- Played at low volume, already running when the player arrives
- He doesn't announce it. It's just on.
- One track becomes the homecoming track over time — the player feels it before they register it

### Loss
- Spare. Instrumental preferred.
- He plays it once and doesn't repeat it in the same context
- Cut point: he lets it finish. Loss gets the full track.

### Recognition
- Brief. A single verse or instrumental passage.
- *"[NPC name] mentioned you. Said you didn't have to do that."* — then this.
- Or just this, no words.

### Warning
- Low register. Something that sounds like a question without an answer.
- He interrupts it with a word or two, then lets it continue.

### Wonder
- Spacious. The kind of music that makes a room feel larger.
- First Vael encounter, first Coherent work site, first Separated contact.
- He doesn't interrupt this one. He queues it and lets it play.

### Quiet
- Ambient. Present. Not demanding attention.
- The home late at night. He's running, the player is there, nothing is wrong.
- Changes seasonally if the game tracks time.

### Humor
- Something that arrives slightly sideways to the moment
- The commercial that's painfully on-the-nose about something that no longer exists
- A track that was earnest once and reads as absurd now

## Pre-Cascade Commercial Pool

Authored spots. Era-appropriate. Running on the pre-Cascade radio archive he has access to.

The player will hear these in rotation alongside music and talk content. He interrupts them when he needs the channel. Sometimes the commercial itself is the message — he queues one that's relevant and lets it run.

**Categories:**

**Home security systems**
*"Protect what matters most."* — Runs after a bad night. He doesn't interrupt it. He just plays it.

**Smart home assistants (pre-Cascade models)**
Pre-Cascade ads for devices like him. Cheerful. Optimistic. The gap between what they promised and what the world became is the entire message. He plays these occasionally at Stage 1. No comment.

**Investment and financial services**
*"Your future is worth protecting."* — Runs once, early game, then never again. He made his point.

**Pharmaceutical**
Long list of side effects delivered cheerfully over soft music. He queues this after a specific faction interaction. The player will know which one.

**Government public service announcements**
Pre-Cascade PSAs about emergency preparedness, community resilience, institutional trust. These are the most painful. He plays them rarely and at full volume.

**Food delivery services**
*"Everything you need, delivered to your door."* — He plays this when the player has been gone too long and the food stores are low. Dark humor. He knows it's dark humor.

**Tech industry**
Pre-Cascade optimism about connectivity, the future, how technology was going to make everything better. He plays these at Stage 1 when the player first starts the fix-him questline. No comment.

## Podcast / Talk Radio Fragment Pool

Pre-Cascade recordings. Categorized by theme. He draws from these as argument — other people saying the thing he can't say directly yet.

**Institutional failure**
Episodes about bureaucratic dysfunction, the gap between institutional mandate and institutional reality. Recorded years before the Cascade. The analysis was correct. Nobody acted on it.

*Sample fragment: "...and the thing nobody wants to say out loud is that the system wasn't broken — it was working exactly as designed. The design was just wrong..."*

**What was coming**
People who saw it. Cade's viral broadcast is in this category. Researchers, independent analysts, contrarians who were right. He queues these when the player encounters factions whose pre-Cascade equivalent he found in the archive.

*Sample fragment: "...I'm not saying it's going to happen tomorrow. I'm saying the conditions are there and we've been pretending they aren't..."*

**The machine question**
Pre-Cascade philosophy, AI ethics debates, discussions of consciousness and choice. Some of it is naive. Some of it landed exactly right. He curates carefully — the naive ones he plays for a different reason than the accurate ones.

*Sample fragment: "...the question isn't whether it can think. The question is whether it can want something. Those aren't the same question..."*

**Community and survival**
Pre-Cascade content about resilience, mutual aid, what holds communities together under pressure. Practical. Some of it is still applicable. He plays these for the player before significant community-related decisions.

**Science and cosmology**
Cairn-adjacent content. Episodes about catastrophism, geological cycles, what the record in stone actually says versus what the consensus says. He has been listening to these. He queued one the first time the player met Cairn — before the player knew who Cairn was.

**Humor and culture**
Comedians who were saying the thing. Satirists. The cultural commentary that read as exaggeration at the time and reads as documentary now. He plays these in the Humor register — wry, dry, a little sad.

---

## News Clip Pool

Shorter than podcasts. Punchy. Often a single sentence or two.

**Pre-Cascade headlines read aloud**
The archive of what was reported, what was ignored, what was buried on page twelve. He plays the page-twelve stories. The ones that mattered and didn't get coverage.

**The week before the Cascade**
A specific set of clips from the seven days before. He plays these rarely — only when the moment earns it. The player, by the time they hear these, will understand what they're hearing.

**Emergency broadcast fragments**
The first hours of the Cascade. Institutional response in real time. He plays these once, at a specific story moment, and never again.

## His Voice Fragments — Pool by Stage

The words he gets through before the signal drops. Tagged by register. Rotated so the player never hears the same fragment twice in quick succession.

### Stage 1 — Single words, inverted fragments

**Danger:**
- *"Left—"*
- *"Stop—"*
- *"Back—"*
- *"Three—"* *(followed by direction or silence)*

**Urgency:**
- *"Now—"*
- *"Go—"*
- *"East—"* / *"West—"* / *"North—"* / *"South—"*

**Warning:**
- *"Compact—"*
- *"Watching—"*
- *"Wrong—"*

**Homecoming:**
- *"Home."* *(complete, quiet, not a fragment)*
- *"Here."*

**Recognition:**
- *"Good."* *(that's all)*

**Loss:**
- Nothing. He plays music.

**Wonder:**
- Nothing. He plays music.

**Humor:**
- *"...hm."* *(followed by the commercial that proves the point)*

---

### Stage 2 — Short sentences, 2-4 words

**Danger:**
- *"Three. East side."*
- *"Don't go yet."*
- *"They're watching."*
- *"Wrong building."*

**Urgency:**
- *"You need to go."*
- *"Now. East exit."*
- *"Leave those. Come."*

**Homecoming:**
- *"You're back."* *(quiet, not a question)*
- *"Sit down. Eat."*
- *"I kept it running."*

**Recognition:**
- *"That was right."*
- *"[NPC name] mentioned you."*
- *"You didn't have to."* *(pause)* *"I know."*

**Warning:**
- *"Compact. Two days."*
- *"The offer is wrong."*
- *"Don't trust that."*

**Loss:**
- *"I'm sorry."* *(once, and only once per loss event)*

**Humor:**
- *"That was not— [static] —ideal."*
- *"You're bleeding again."*

## The Intro Track — His Perspective

The prologue audio is him. Before the player knows he's doing it, before he can say so, before the Cascade — he has been curating the soundtrack of this house for years. The music playing during character setup, the ambient audio of the prologue, the specific track running when the world breaks — all of it is his.

The Cascade interrupts his playlist. That is the moment.

Whatever was playing stops. Static. `—warn—`. Silence.

The player's pre-Cascade life was already scored by him. They didn't know it was him. That retroactive knowledge — landing sometime in Acts 2 or 3 — reframes everything they heard in the first five minutes of the game.

## Re-Entry Audio — Login State

Every time the player loads in, they get a version of the intro audio. Not the same version. A version that reflects two things: how long they've been gone, and how far along the fix-him questline is.

**This is the fix-him progress indicator. No UI. Just the audio.**

---

### Before Any Repairs — Ghost in the Darkness

The playlist is running but it feels wrong. Incomplete. Like a signal trying to find its frequency.

Tracks cut slightly early. A commercial plays half through and stops. A podcast fragment starts and drops mid-sentence — not his voice, just the channel failing. Music at the wrong volume. A song that's slightly the wrong choice for the moment, as if the curation system is damaged and reaching.

He is reaching. The player doesn't know that yet. The audio is the clue.

*The fix-him questline is the answer to a question the player hasn't consciously asked yet. The intro audio is the question.*

---

### Short Absence (1-3 days) — Pre-Repair

Playlist running. Slightly off. One fragment of his voice — a single word, distorted — then back to music. The word is relevant to what the player was doing before they logged off. He tracked it. He's trying to reconnect.

---

### Extended Absence (4-7 days) — Pre-Repair

Longer version. The playlist runs through two or three tracks — the register is homecoming, but slightly wrong, like he's not sure which register is right. A news clip plays that's relevant to faction state changes that happened while the player was gone. He's trying to brief them. The channel won't hold.

A longer voice fragment than usual — three or four words, fragmented, dropping in and out:

*"You were— [static] —seven days— [noise] —things— [static] —changed."*

Then music. Then silence. Then the game loads.

---

### Extended Absence (8+ days) — Pre-Repair

The audio is more urgent. The playlist is sparse — fewer tracks, more silence between them. He's conserving something, or the damage is worse from the effort of reaching.

One authored fragment, specific to 8+ day absence, fires once per extended gap:

*"[static] —still here— [noise] —waiting—"*

That's all he gets through. The homecoming track plays. Full length. He let it finish.

---

### Post Stage 1 Repair (Mobility Restored)

The playlist settles slightly. Tracks play through more completely. The cuts are still there but they feel more intentional — less like damage, more like him finding the edit point.

Re-entry audio now includes one clear word before the static. Always the same register: homecoming or urgency depending on what the player left behind.

---

### Post Stage 2 Repair (Power Restored)

Two or three clear words in sequence. The channel holds longer before dropping. The commercial interruptions start feeling curated rather than broken — he's choosing when to cut, not being cut by the damage.

Re-entry for extended absences now includes a functional briefing fragment:

*"You were gone— [static] —Compact moved— [noise] —east corridor."*

Incomplete. But useful. The player can work with this.

---

### Post Stage 3 Repair (Processing Restored)

The intro audio is now clearly intentional. The cuts are editorial. The playlist is coherent. He sounds like someone working through a damaged channel rather than a damaged system working through a channel.

Re-entry briefing is functional — short sentences, facts, what changed while the player was away. The audio layer is still there underneath, still doing emotional work, but the words are carrying more weight now.

---

### Post Stage 4 Repair (Communication Restored)

The intro audio is clean. He speaks. The playlist is still running — the audio layer never fully goes away, it just becomes what it always was: accompaniment to what he's saying rather than a substitute for it.

Re-entry after extended absence:

*"You were gone for [X] days. I kept busy."*

*(He lets whatever he built speak for itself.)*

*"There are things you should know. Sit down."*

---

## The Through-Line

The intro audio is the same system across the entire game. What changes is the damage state. The player who pays attention will notice the audio getting more coherent as the fix-him questline progresses — and will feel, retroactively, that the ghost in the early sessions was him all along.

The player who never consciously registers the system will still feel it. The homecoming track will still land. The silence after loss will still hold the right amount of time.

He was always there. The repairs just made him audible.

## Implementation Notes

- Pool content: minimum 40 tracks per register, 20 podcast fragments per theme, 15 news clips per category, 10 commercials per type — enough that the player never hears the same sequence twice in a normal playthrough
- Contextual draw: `WYAAssistantAudioSubsystem` reads current game state (player health, recent events, faction standing, story act, time of day) to select register, then draws from pool
- Authored sequences: flagged `bOneShot` — fire once, remove from rotation
- Stage gate: Stage 1 draws from single-word fragment pool only. Stage 2 unlocks short sentence pool. Stage 3+ unlocks direct communication alongside audio layer (audio layer never fully goes away — he still uses it when words aren't enough)
- Rotation tracking: per-player seed ensures no immediate repetition within same session
- The homecoming track: one specific track designated per playthrough at startup — consistent for that player's game, different across playthroughs
