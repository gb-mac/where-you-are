# The Warden Makers — Craft, Provenance, and the Accord

*"What you make, you are responsible for. That includes what it does in the world after it leaves your hands."*
— The Accord, on craft obligation

---

## Overview

Every Warden faction document will tell you the code is short, the oath is absolute, and the Record does not lie. What it won't tell you — until you've spent enough time in a Warden estate to notice — is the quality of the gear on the wall.

The stitching on that plate carrier was done twice where once would have held. The chamfer on the knife's spine is deliberate — not decorative, functional in the specific way that someone who has used a knife in bad conditions would know to make it. The gunsmith's work on the action is clean in a way that takes patience. Someone made these things with intention, and you can read the intention in the work.

That is the Makers.

Within each House, the four craft roles — Armorer, Tailor, Gunsmith, Bladesmith — are not jobs. They carry a single elevated internal title under the Accord: **The Maker.** The specific material is a description. The title is the standing. A Maker has sworn that the work is true, that what they build reflects their understanding of what the carrier will face, and that they are accountable for it — not just until delivery, but after.

If the person who carries it falls, the Maker is a witness to how.

## The Four Roles

### The Armorer

Hard protection: plate carriers, ceramic ballistic inserts, helmets, structural defense components.

The Armorer works in the gap between what gear was designed for and what it actually encounters in this world. Pre-Cascade tactical equipment was built for specific threat profiles — none of which included Fractured machine components, Vael Probe contact, or Coherent material interaction. The Armorer has been adapting that equipment since the Cascade, learning what holds and what doesn't, building profiles for specific enemy types in specific locations.

An Armorer who has outfitted Vanguard for Behemoth encounters knows things about plate failure modes that no documentation records. That knowledge is in their hands, in how they build the next set. You can read it in the reinforcement at stress points.

**What the Armorer knows:** History. They have outfitted the elevated and the fallen. They have made things for people who wore them well and people who didn't. They know which Sworn took the oath seriously from the first day and which ones needed time to grow into it. They also know who made them a request that revealed what they were actually preparing for — and what that preparation implied.

**Story weight:** Highest archive depth. If you want to understand a House's military history since the Cascade, the Armorer is where you start.

---

### The Tailor

Soft kit: fabric, harness, straps, the fit. The construction quality that announces Warden work before the marks do.

The Tailor is the most politically sensitive of the four Makers. Fit is personal. An Armorer can measure at arm's length; a Tailor cannot. They spend time with the Sworn in a way the others don't — close, quiet, specific. They know things about people that the Record doesn't capture because the Record documents decisions, not weight distribution or how someone carries tension in their shoulders.

Warden gear is recognizable partly because of the marks and partly because of the build. Anyone who knows what they're looking at can identify Warden tailoring before the marks confirm it — the join technique, the harness routing, the specific construction at the collarbone where load is distributed. Old Houses pass down techniques. Sworn learn to maintain what they carry. The Tailor's repair is distinct from field patching: it looks like Warden repair, and that means something.

**What the Tailor knows:** People. Not secrets — or not only secrets — but the shape of how someone carries themselves, what they are prepared for, how they changed between one fitting and the next. A Tailor who has worked with a Sworn for years has watched them grow into the code or strain against it long before the Arbiters noticed anything.

**Story weight:** Highest interpersonal intelligence. If you want to understand a specific Sworn — what they were, what they became, what they were preparing for before they disappeared — the Tailor knows.

---

### The Gunsmith

Firearms: maintenance, modification, provenance.

Firearms in the post-Cascade world are not scarce, but functioning, reliable firearms are. The difference between a weapon that works when you need it and one that fails is maintenance — and Warden Gunsmiths maintain to a standard that most operators never see. A piece of Warden Gunsmith work functions consistently under conditions that would end any commercially-maintained firearm.

The Gunsmith also knows every weapon they've touched. Their provenance records are the most complete of all four Makers — serial numbers, modification history, who carried it, when it was last serviced, what it had been through before it returned to the bench. A Gunsmith can look at a weapon and tell you how it's been used: what it's been fired at, how it's been maintained, whether the person who carried it respected it.

**What the Gunsmith knows:** Preparation. Weapons tell you what someone was ready to face. A Warden who comes in to have their carry piece serviced and quietly inquires about a specific caliber's penetration against machine casing is preparing for something specific. The Gunsmith noted it. They note everything.

**Story weight:** Highest intelligence on faction threat assessment. If you want to know what a House was preparing for before something significant happened, the Gunsmith is the record.

---

### The Bladesmith

Edged weapons: knives, swords, blades made to outlast their carriers.

The Bladesmith works in the oldest tradition among the four — and they know it. A blade made by a House Bladesmith is built with a timeline that assumes the weapon will survive everyone who carries it. The steel is chosen for the specific use. The edge geometry is a decision. The handle is fitted. The balance is intentional.

This is also why a blade's provenance carries more weight than any other piece of gear. A firearm can be reconfigured. Armor can be replaced. A Bladesmith's work is specific — it was made for someone, in a specific period, for a specific purpose. Finding a Warden blade on a body, or in a ruin, or in the wrong hands tells you something definite about the chain of events that got it there.

Bladesmiths tend to be the most reclusive of the four Makers. Their work takes longest. They have the fewest pieces in circulation. They sometimes know their work is being carried by someone who shouldn't have it, and they think carefully about whether the Accord requires them to say so.

**What the Bladesmith knows:** The long view. They have a sense of the Houses that spans decades — who mattered, who fell, what has been lost that isn't in the Record because no one thought to write it down at the time.

**Story weight:** Highest historical depth and moral weight. The Bladesmith is the Maker most likely to know things they haven't decided what to do with yet.

## The Provenance Mechanic

Every piece of Warden gear has a Maker. Always. The Accord requires it — a piece of Warden work with no Maker is a piece no one claimed, which means no one is accountable for it. That condition is a violation in itself.

A Maker can read their own work. Given any piece they've made — across years, across wear, across field repairs by other hands — they will recognize it. The join technique. The specific reinforcement. The chamber work. The stitch pattern on the harness. It is as identifiable as handwriting.

**For players:** Recovered Warden gear is a door. Bringing a recovered piece to the right House and finding the Maker who made it opens a conversation that the object alone could not. The Maker's response to seeing their work again — in your hands, in uncertain condition, far from where it should be — is the content.

The Maker is not required to tell you everything. The Accord does not compel them to answer questions from someone with no standing. It does compel them to be honest about what they made and when. What they volunteer beyond that depends on who you are, how you found it, and what they've decided about whether you should know.

## The Council of Memory

The four Makers in a House form an informal council of institutional memory. They have, collectively, outfitted everyone in the House's history. They know who was ready to take the oath before they asked, and who took it before they were ready. They know what the House has faced since the Cascade. They have made the gear for every significant event in the House's Record, and the gear remembers what it was made for even when people don't.

They are not an authority structure — that belongs to the Arbiters and the Moot. They are, instead, the people who know. They compare notes carefully and say little publicly. When a Maker speaks at a formal gathering, the room pays attention, because they don't speak often.

A player who has relationship with all four Makers in a House holds a kind of intelligence no faction — not even the Signal — can fully replicate. They know the House from the inside, through the physical objects that carried its history.

## Gear Made for the Excommunicado

The Accord has a specific clause for this situation, and it is one of the harder things a Maker carries.

When a player or NPC is declared excommunicado, their gear does not change. The Warden work they carry still exists. The Maker who made it is still accountable for it. The Accord does not allow a Maker to deny their work because the person wearing it was declared — the work was made in good faith, and good faith is not retroactively voided.

What the Accord does require: the Maker notes the excommunicado status in their provenance record. The gear becomes contested provenance — Warden work in the hands of someone the Wardens have cast out. The Maker can formally request return of the piece through an Arbiter. The excommunicado player is under no obligation to comply. The Arbiter documents the request and the refusal.

This creates a permanent tension. A Maker who sees their work in the wrong hands has a relationship with that situation that no one else does. Some Makers track the gear obsessively. Some make peace with it. Some are quietly relieved when excommunicado players eventually come to them — not for reinstatement, not for anything formal — just to return the piece. No other conversation required. The Maker takes it back. The provenance record closes.

## Finding a Maker

Makers are not reclusive by temperament, but they are not findable through faction reputation tracks or quest markers. They are findable through the work.

**Three paths:**

**The recovered piece.** You find Warden gear. You bring it to a House. Someone checks it, narrows it to a Maker's signature, and either directs you or doesn't, depending on how you got it and what they think your intentions are.

**The Warden contacts you.** If you've built enough standing — or done work that the Record notes as significant — a Maker may make first contact. Not to recruit. To see who you are. This is more common for the Tradesperson background, for reasons the arc document covers.

**The Arbiter connection.** Arbiters know where the Makers are. A player with Recognized standing can ask. Whether an Arbiter directs you depends on why you're asking.

## NPC Profiles

Each Maker type reads differently in conversation:

**The Armorer** is direct and evaluative. They look at what you're wearing before they look at your face. They will tell you plainly if your current gear is inadequate for what you're doing, and they will not phrase it diplomatically. Their assessment is not hostile — it is the same honest accounting they apply to their own work.

**The Tailor** is observant and patient. They listen more than they speak. When they do speak, they reference things you've said earlier in the conversation — sometimes much earlier. They remember the shape of people.

**The Gunsmith** is precise and systematic. Every question they ask is a step in a diagnostic process. They are not warm, but they are not cold — they are focused. Their workspace is organized in a way that reflects a specific understanding of how work should flow. They notice when yours isn't.

**The Bladesmith** is the hardest to read. Long silences. Questions that seem unrelated until much later. They take time to answer not because they're slow but because they're determining what the true answer is, as opposed to the easy one. When they make a judgment, it is final, and they've already considered the counterarguments.
